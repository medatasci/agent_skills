Scope

This chapter covers metastatic malignant neoplasm to brain as a diagnostic imaging finding, report label, oncology phenotype, and research endpoint. It includes parenchymal brain metastases, solitary and multiple metastases, hemorrhagic metastases, cystic or necrotic metastases, leptomeningeal metastases, dural metastases, treated metastases after surgery or radiation, and recurrence versus treatment-effect assessment.

The chapter does not treat every enhancing lesion in a patient with cancer as a metastasis. It also does not treat radiation necrosis, postoperative enhancement, abscess, demyelination, lymphoma, or glioma as metastasis unless the source report or adjudication supports that label.

Clinical Context

Brain metastases are the most common adult intracranial malignancy. Common primary tumors include lung cancer, breast cancer, melanoma, renal cell carcinoma, and colorectal cancer, but subtype-specific risk depends on molecular status, systemic disease control, screening practice, and treatment era. Contrast-enhanced MRI is the usual diagnostic and surveillance modality because it detects small lesions, leptomeningeal disease, posterior fossa lesions, and treatment response better than CT.

Typical parenchymal metastases are enhancing lesions, often multiple, often at the gray-white junction or watershed regions, and frequently surrounded by vasogenic edema that can be large relative to lesion size. Enhancement may be solid, nodular, or ring-like when necrotic. Some primaries produce hemorrhagic, melanotic, cystic, or calcified appearances. Treatment history changes the meaning of enhancement because surgery, SRS, WBRT, immunotherapy, targeted therapy, and radiation necrosis can mimic progression.

For research use, the key distinction is whether the label means:

  • untreated parenchymal brain metastasis
  • solitary metastasis versus multiple metastases
  • leptomeningeal, dural, or pachymeningeal metastasis
  • hemorrhagic metastasis
  • treated metastasis with response, progression, or treatment effect
  • suspected metastasis versus glioma, abscess, demyelination, lymphoma, or another mimic
  • brain metastasis as endpoint versus extracranial systemic progression

Known-Diagnosis Review Frame

When the diagnosis or report label is brain metastasis, characterize:

  • lesion number: solitary, oligometastatic, multiple, innumerable, or miliary
  • location: supratentorial, infratentorial, cortical/subcortical, gray-white junction, deep gray, brainstem, cerebellum, dural, leptomeningeal, ventricular
  • morphology: solid, ring-enhancing, necrotic, cystic, hemorrhagic, melanotic, calcified, diffusion-restricting, or infiltrative
  • edema and mass effect: vasogenic edema, sulcal effacement, midline shift, herniation, hydrocephalus
  • primary cancer context: known primary, unknown primary, molecular subtype, systemic disease status, CNS-active systemic therapy
  • treatment state: untreated, post-surgery, post-SRS, post-WBRT, post-systemic therapy, radiation necrosis, pseudoprogression, recurrence, or mixed response
  • mimics: glioblastoma, abscess, lymphoma, demyelination, toxoplasmosis, meningioma, cavernous malformation, hemorrhage, infarct, and treatment effect

What To Look For

Acute Or Early Presentation

New brain metastases often appear as enhancing parenchymal lesions on postcontrast T1-weighted MRI with surrounding vasogenic edema on FLAIR/T2. Lesions are often round, nodular, or ring-enhancing and may occur at the gray-white junction or posterior fossa. Acute presentations may include seizure, headache, focal neurologic deficit, hydrocephalus, hemorrhage, or mass effect.

Report-like language:

  • "Multiple enhancing lesions at the gray-white junction with surrounding vasogenic edema, compatible with intracranial metastatic disease."
  • "Solitary ring-enhancing right frontal lesion with extensive vasogenic edema; metastasis is favored in the setting of known lung carcinoma."
  • "Hemorrhagic enhancing lesion in the left cerebellum, suspicious for metastatic disease."

Subacute Or Evolving Presentation

Subacute disease may show growth in lesion size or number, increasing edema, developing necrosis, hemorrhage, or new leptomeningeal/dural involvement. After treatment, lesions may transiently enlarge or enhance more because of pseudoprogression or radiation effect; the time course and treatment type are essential.

Report-like language:

  • "Interval increase in size and surrounding edema of the treated right parietal metastasis; progression versus treatment effect should be considered."
  • "New punctate enhancing lesions in the cerebellum and left frontal lobe, concerning for new metastases."
  • "Increasing sulcal and cranial nerve enhancement, suspicious for leptomeningeal metastatic disease."

Chronic Or Stable Residual Presentation

Stable treated metastases may show unchanged small enhancing foci, stable postoperative cavity enhancement, residual gliosis or FLAIR signal, or stable post-radiation change. Some treated lesions shrink but leave persistent enhancement, hemosiderin, encephalomalacia, or gliosis.

Report-like language:

  • "Stable treated left cerebellar metastasis without new enhancing lesions."
  • "Postoperative cavity enhancement is unchanged and favored treatment-related."
  • "No intracranial metastatic progression compared with prior MRI."

Progressive, Recurrent, Or Worsening Presentation

Progression is suggested by new lesions, enlarging enhancing lesions, increasing edema, increasing mass effect, new leptomeningeal or dural spread, recurrent enhancement along a resection cavity, rising perfusion in a treated lesion, or clinical decline attributable to CNS disease. Progression must be separated from radiation necrosis, pseudoprogression, hemorrhage, infection, and postoperative change.

Report-like language:

  • "New and enlarging enhancing lesions consistent with progressive intracranial metastatic disease."
  • "Increased nodular enhancement along the resection cavity, concerning for local recurrence."
  • "Elevated perfusion within the enlarging treated lesion supports viable tumor over radiation necrosis, although tissue diagnosis may be required."

Improving, Resolving, Or Treatment-Response Presentation

Response may include reduced lesion size, decreased enhancement, decreased edema, fewer measurable lesions, reduced steroid requirement, improved neurologic symptoms, or stable treated lesions without new metastases. Response criteria may require standardized measurement, clinical status, steroid use, and extracranial disease assessment.

Report-like language:

  • "Decreased size and enhancement of multiple brain metastases with improved vasogenic edema."
  • "No new intracranial metastases; treated lesions are stable or decreased."
  • "Findings are compatible with intracranial treatment response."

Context Clues

  • Known primary cancer, molecular subtype, extracranial disease status, and CNS-active therapy affect pretest probability.
  • Melanoma, renal cell carcinoma, thyroid cancer, choriocarcinoma, and some lung lesions are more likely to be hemorrhagic.
  • A solitary lesion can be metastasis, glioblastoma, abscess, lymphoma, demyelination, or radiation necrosis.
  • Leptomeningeal and dural metastases require postcontrast T1 and sometimes spine MRI or CSF context.
  • Treatment timing after SRS, WBRT, surgery, immunotherapy, or targeted therapy changes the interpretation of enhancement and edema.

Report Language Patterns

Findings-style language should describe:

  • lesion number, size, laterality, and anatomic distribution
  • enhancement pattern: solid, nodular, ring, punctate, leptomeningeal, dural
  • edema, mass effect, midline shift, hydrocephalus, hemorrhage, necrosis
  • DWI/ADC, SWI/GRE, perfusion, spectroscopy, and postcontrast findings when relevant
  • treatment state and comparison with prior imaging
  • relevant negative findings such as no new lesions or no leptomeningeal disease

Impression-style language should synthesize:

  • "Multiple enhancing lesions compatible with brain metastases."
  • "Solitary enhancing lesion favored metastatic disease in the setting of known systemic malignancy."
  • "Treated metastasis with imaging changes favored radiation necrosis over progression."
  • "New leptomeningeal enhancement concerning for leptomeningeal metastatic disease."

Uncertainty and cohort-label language:

  • Confident: "brain metastases," "intracranial metastatic disease," "treated metastasis," "metastatic lesion."
  • Probable/favored: "suspicious for metastasis," "favored metastatic disease."
  • Low-confidence/nonspecific: "ring-enhancing lesion," "enhancing lesion," "indeterminate lesion," "treatment effect versus progression."
  • Adjudication trigger: solitary lesion, immunocompromised state, diffusion restriction, open-ring enhancement, postoperative cavity, radiation necrosis, unknown primary, or no known systemic malignancy.

Primary Imaging Modality

Contrast-enhanced MRI is the primary modality for detection, staging, treatment planning, and follow-up of brain metastases. Thin-section 3D postcontrast T1 sequences improve detection of small metastases and treatment-planning accuracy. CT is useful for urgent evaluation, hemorrhage, hydrocephalus, or MRI contraindication, but is less sensitive for small metastases and leptomeningeal disease.

  • T1: metastases are often iso- to hypointense before contrast; hemorrhage, melanin, fat, or calcification can alter signal.
  • T2: variable lesion signal; surrounding vasogenic edema is bright.
  • FLAIR: highlights edema, cortical/subcortical lesions, leptomeningeal abnormality, and treatment effects.
  • DWI/ADC: helps identify abscess, lymphoma, hypercellular lesions, infarct, and treatment complications.
  • SWI/GRE: detects hemorrhagic metastases, microhemorrhage, and calcification susceptibility.
  • Post-contrast T1: key sequence for lesion detection, leptomeningeal/dural disease, resection cavity recurrence, and treatment monitoring.
  • Perfusion, spectroscopy, tractography, volumetry, or other advanced methods: perfusion MRI, MR spectroscopy, amino-acid PET, radiomics, and volumetry may help distinguish recurrence from radiation necrosis or support research endpoints, but require careful validation.

Other Modalities And When They Matter

CT matters for acute neurologic decline, hemorrhage, hydrocephalus, and MRI contraindication. PET/CT and systemic staging matter for primary cancer and extracranial disease. Amino-acid PET may help distinguish viable tumor from treatment effect in selected settings. CSF cytology and spine MRI matter when leptomeningeal disease is suspected. Pathology matters when imaging is indeterminate or when molecular subtype affects treatment.

Locations And Structural Appearance

  • cortical: metastases often occur near the cortical/subcortical gray-white junction because of hematogenous spread.
  • subcortical: common location with disproportionate vasogenic edema.
  • deep white matter: possible, but infiltrative deep white-matter spread raises glioma or lymphoma concerns.
  • periventricular: lymphoma, infection, or leptomeningeal/ependymal spread may be important mimics.
  • corpus callosum: callosal crossing favors glioblastoma or lymphoma over typical metastasis.
  • deep gray nuclei: metastases can occur, but hypertensive hemorrhage, lymphoma, or infarct may mimic.
  • brainstem: small metastases have high clinical impact and require careful contrast-enhanced review.
  • cerebellum: posterior fossa metastases are common and can cause hydrocephalus.
  • hippocampus or mesial temporal structures: relevant to hippocampal-avoidance WBRT eligibility and seizure presentations.
  • postoperative, post-treatment, or post-injury regions: resection cavity enhancement, radiation necrosis, hemorrhage, gliosis, and recurrence require serial comparison.
  • vascular-territory, tract-like, multifocal, diffuse, symmetric, asymmetric, mass-like, cavitary, atrophic, or volume-loss patterns: metastases are often rounded and mass-like rather than vascular-territory lesions; diffuse leptomeningeal or dural patterns require separate labeling.

Typical Appearance

Typical brain metastasis appears as one or more enhancing intra-axial lesions, often round or ring-enhancing, with surrounding vasogenic edema. Lesions often sit at the gray-white junction, in watershed regions, or in the posterior fossa. The edema may be extensive relative to tumor size and usually involves white matter more than cortex.

Atypical Or Red-Flag Appearance

Red flags include solitary infiltrative lesion crossing white-matter tracts, marked diffusion restriction suggesting abscess or lymphoma, open-ring enhancement suggesting demyelination, homogeneous deep periventricular enhancement suggesting lymphoma, dural tail suggesting meningioma, no known primary cancer, systemic infection, immunocompromised state, or enhancement arising soon after radiation or immunotherapy.

Differential Diagnosis And Mimics

Use this section for questions such as "what else could this enhancing lesion be?", "what imaging features support metastasis?", and "what would make a similar-appearing diagnosis more likely?"

Quick Differential Diagnosis Guide

  1. Glioblastoma or high-grade glioma: solitary infiltrative necrotic mass, corpus callosum crossing, cortical involvement, and infiltrative FLAIR.
  2. Abscess: ring enhancement with central diffusion restriction and infection context.
  3. Primary CNS lymphoma: deep/periventricular homogeneous enhancement, diffusion restriction, immunosuppression context.
  4. Tumefactive demyelination: open-ring enhancement, less mass effect, MS-like lesion distribution.
  5. Radiation necrosis or pseudoprogression: treated lesion enlargement or enhancement after SRS/WBRT/systemic therapy.
  6. Dural metastasis versus meningioma: dural-based enhancing lesion, bone and dural-tail context.
  7. Cerebral hemorrhage or cavernous malformation: hemorrhagic lesion without a clear enhancing metastatic mass.

Key Imaging Discriminators

  • Multiple enhancing lesions at gray-white junction strongly support metastases in the right clinical context.
  • Disproportionate vasogenic edema supports metastasis but is not specific.
  • Central diffusion restriction favors abscess over necrotic metastasis.
  • Infiltrative nonenhancing FLAIR crossing the corpus callosum favors glioma.
  • Homogeneous diffusion-restricting periventricular enhancement favors lymphoma.
  • Open-ring enhancement favors tumefactive demyelination.
  • Elevated perfusion can support viable tumor over radiation necrosis, but no single advanced imaging marker is definitive.

Similar-Presentation Diseases And Mimic-Aware Comparison

Brain metastasis often overlaps with MR-RATE mass, hemorrhage, edema, and treatment-effect categories. The safest research workflow is to require explicit metastatic language, known cancer context with a favored metastatic interpretation, pathology, or follow-up response consistent with metastasis. Report language that only says "enhancing lesion" or "ring-enhancing lesion" needs adjudication.

Report Language That Supports Or Argues Against Each Diagnosis

  • Supports metastasis: "brain metastases," "intracranial metastatic disease," "compatible with metastatic lesions," "new metastasis," "treated metastasis."
  • Supports glioma: "infiltrative," "crosses corpus callosum," "nonenhancing tumor," "glioblastoma favored."
  • Supports abscess: "central restricted diffusion," "infectious collection," "pyogenic abscess."
  • Supports demyelination: "open ring," "demyelinating lesion," "MS-like distribution."
  • Supports treatment effect: "radiation necrosis," "pseudoprogression," "treatment-related change," "low perfusion."

When Additional Imaging Or Clinical Context Helps

Additional context is needed when lesions are solitary, ring-enhancing, posttreatment, hemorrhagic, diffusion-restricting, dural-based, or in an immunocompromised patient. Helpful inputs include known primary cancer, molecular subtype, systemic disease status, steroid/immunotherapy history, radiation date, surgical date, serial MRI, perfusion, spectroscopy, PET, CSF, and pathology.

Natural History And Clinical Course

Brain metastases can be asymptomatic, present with seizure or focal deficit, or cause life-threatening mass effect, hemorrhage, hydrocephalus, or herniation. Course depends on primary tumor, molecular subtype, extracranial disease, number and size of lesions, performance status, systemic therapy, local treatment, and treatment era. Lesions may respond, remain stable, progress, recur locally, seed new distant intracranial metastases, or develop leptomeningeal disease.

Treatment, Response, And Outcome Context

Guideline-Based Management Context

Guidelines emphasize multidisciplinary decision-making across surgery, radiation therapy, and systemic therapy. Symptomatic lesions often require local therapy. SRS, postoperative cavity SRS, WBRT with cognitive-sparing strategies, surgery, systemic CNS-active therapy, and supportive care all depend on symptoms, lesion number, lesion size, mass effect, histology, molecular subtype, performance status, and extracranial disease.

Common Treatment Pathways

  • corticosteroids for symptomatic edema when clinically appropriate
  • surgery for selected large, symptomatic, accessible, or diagnostically uncertain lesions
  • stereotactic radiosurgery or stereotactic radiotherapy
  • postoperative cavity SRS or WBRT in selected contexts
  • whole-brain radiation therapy, sometimes with hippocampal avoidance and memantine
  • systemic therapy, targeted therapy, immunotherapy, or chemotherapy with CNS activity
  • laser interstitial thermal therapy or other selected salvage approaches
  • surveillance, supportive care, or palliative care in poor-prognosis contexts

Imaging Appearance After Treatment

Posttreatment imaging may show decreased enhancement and edema, stable treated lesions, hemorrhage, postoperative cavity enhancement, gliosis, necrosis, pseudoprogression, radiation necrosis, or recurrent tumor. SRS-related changes can occur months to years after treatment and may mimic progression.

Evidence Of Treatment Response

Response can include reduced lesion size, decreased enhancement, decreased edema, fewer target or non-target lesions, no new lesions, improved symptoms, reduced steroid need, and stable extracranial disease depending on the response criteria being used.

Evidence Of Progression, Recurrence, Or Treatment Failure

Progression is suggested by new lesions, increasing size or enhancement, increasing edema or mass effect, new leptomeningeal disease, local recurrence at a resection cavity or treated lesion, worsening neurologic symptoms, or need for salvage therapy. Confirmatory imaging or adjudication is often needed when treatment effect is plausible.

Expected Outcomes And Prognostic Factors

Outcomes depend on performance status, age, primary cancer, molecular subtype, extracranial disease, number and volume of brain metastases, leptomeningeal disease, treatment modality, systemic therapy options, steroid dependence, and neurocognitive status.

Statistical Implications Of Treatment And Progression

Treatment introduces confounding by indication because larger, symptomatic, or high-risk lesions are more likely to receive surgery, radiation, or systemic therapy. CNS-active systemic therapies and surveillance intensity vary by primary cancer and molecular subtype. Response assessment must account for steroid dose, clinical status, extracranial disease, new lesions, and treatment-effect mimics.

Evidence Of Active Disease, Progression, Or Recurrence

Active or worsening metastatic disease is suggested by new enhancing lesions, enlarging treated or untreated lesions, increasing edema, increasing mass effect, new leptomeningeal or dural disease, rising perfusion, or repeated growth on serial imaging. Treatment-effect mimics must be explicitly considered after SRS, WBRT, immunotherapy, targeted therapy, or surgery.

Stable Or Chronic Residual Findings

Stable treated disease is supported by unchanged or decreasing enhancement, stable or decreasing edema, no new lesions, and stable postoperative or post-radiation change on comparable MRI. Persistent enhancement, hemosiderin, gliosis, or FLAIR signal can remain after successful treatment.

Improvement, Treatment Response, Or Resolution

Improvement includes decreasing enhancement, lesion shrinkage, reduced edema, less mass effect, fewer measurable lesions, improved symptoms, reduced steroid use, or no new intracranial metastases. Complete resolution of enhancement is not always required for clinical stability.

Serial Imaging Assessment And Interval Change

Compare number of lesions, target lesion diameters or volumes, enhancement pattern, edema, mass effect, hemorrhage, leptomeningeal/dural spread, treatment cavity findings, and new lesions. Use comparable postcontrast sequences and consistent measurement rules. Record treatment dates and systemic therapy changes. Consider RANO-BM or trial-specific criteria when research endpoints require standardized response assessment.

Clinical Endpoints

Common endpoints include overall survival, intracranial progression-free survival, CNS local control, distant brain failure, neurologic death, seizure, steroid requirement, neurologic symptoms, neurocognition, quality of life, functional status, treatment toxicity, need for surgery/radiation, and leptomeningeal progression.

Imaging, Biomarker, And Measurement Endpoints

Relevant endpoints include lesion count, lesion size, enhancing tumor volume, edema volume, target/non-target lesion status, new lesions, leptomeningeal enhancement, dural disease, hemorrhage, necrosis, perfusion metrics, diffusion metrics, spectroscopy markers, PET uptake, local recurrence, radiation necrosis, and treatment-response category.

Common Covariates And Confounders

Clinical Covariates

  • age, performance status, neurologic symptoms, steroid use, seizure history
  • primary cancer type and molecular subtype
  • extracranial disease burden and systemic disease control
  • prior CNS metastasis history and prior local therapy
  • immunotherapy, targeted therapy, chemotherapy, and CNS-active systemic agents

Imaging Covariates

  • MRI protocol and postcontrast timing
  • lesion number, size, volume, location, edema, hemorrhage, necrosis
  • leptomeningeal/dural involvement
  • treatment cavity, radiation field, SRS dose, and prior imaging interval
  • perfusion, diffusion, spectroscopy, PET, or radiomics availability

Treatment And Temporal Confounders

  • surgery, SRS, WBRT, HA-WBRT, LITT, systemic therapy, immunotherapy, targeted therapy, steroid timing, and antiepileptic use
  • time from primary cancer diagnosis, brain metastasis diagnosis, radiation, surgery, and systemic therapy start
  • pseudoprogression, radiation necrosis, immune-related effects, and delayed radiation injury

Acquisition And Protocol Confounders

  • slice thickness, 2D versus 3D postcontrast T1, contrast dose/timing
  • scanner field strength, vendor, coil, motion artifact, susceptibility artifact
  • steroid use before imaging, postoperative blood products, radiation artifact
  • inconsistent lesion measurement method or target-lesion selection

Research Design Implications

  • stratify by primary cancer and molecular subtype
  • separate untreated, treated, recurrent, and treatment-effect lesions
  • adjudicate solitary and ring-enhancing lesions
  • avoid counting ruled-out or differential-only metastasis mentions as positive
  • perform sensitivity analyses excluding indeterminate treatment-effect cases

Statistical Implications

Brain metastasis cohorts are vulnerable to label noise because radiology reports often mix differential diagnosis, known metastatic history, treatment effect, and new/progressive disease. Studies should define whether the endpoint is lesion detection, new brain metastasis, local recurrence, distant brain failure, leptomeningeal disease, radiation necrosis, or overall CNS progression. Treatment comparisons require careful handling of confounding by indication, immortal time bias, informative surveillance, competing risks, and systemic disease status.

Missing Information To Ask For

  • What is the known primary cancer and molecular subtype?
  • Is the lesion untreated, treated, recurrent, or indeterminate?
  • What are the prior surgery, SRS, WBRT, immunotherapy, targeted therapy, and steroid dates?
  • How many lesions are present and where are they located?
  • Is there leptomeningeal or dural disease?
  • Are there diffusion restriction, hemorrhage, perfusion, spectroscopy, or PET findings?
  • Are prior MRIs available for comparison?
  • Is the report making a confident diagnosis or listing metastasis as a differential possibility?

Safety And Claim Boundaries

This chapter supports research planning, imaging-feature extraction, report-language interpretation, cohort definition, endpoint selection, covariate review, and mimic-aware evidence review. It should not be used as a substitute for qualified clinical interpretation, oncology treatment planning, or urgent management of mass effect, hemorrhage, hydrocephalus, or neurologic decline.
