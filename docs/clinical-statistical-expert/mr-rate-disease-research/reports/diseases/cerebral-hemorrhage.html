Cerebral Hemorrhage

Goals

This disease chapter is designed to help a clinical-statistical reviewer:

  1. Given a known or suspected cerebral hemorrhage context, describe how intraparenchymal hemorrhage appears on CT and MRI using realistic radiology-report language.
  2. Identify imaging features, locations, blood-product stage, complications, and etiologic clues that support or argue against cerebral hemorrhage relative to similar MR-RATE categories.
  3. Translate hemorrhage appearance, report language, uncertainty, and interval change into cohort definition, endpoints, covariates, adjudication, and claims language.

Scope

This chapter covers cerebral hemorrhage as a brain imaging finding, report label, and research phenotype. It emphasizes nontraumatic intraparenchymal hemorrhage because that aligns best with the MR-RATE original label. It also discusses hemorrhage-associated mimics and adjacent labels that often affect classification, including microhemorrhage, subdural hemorrhage, cavernous malformation, tumor-related hemorrhage, hemorrhagic transformation of infarct, and vascular malformation.

Clinical Context

Nontraumatic intracerebral hemorrhage can result from hypertension, cerebral amyloid angiopathy, anticoagulation or coagulopathy, hemorrhagic transformation of ischemic infarct, vascular malformation, venous thrombosis, tumor, cavernous malformation, vasculopathy, or other less common causes. Imaging interpretation is heavily dependent on hemorrhage location, blood-product age, mass effect, intraventricular extension, hydrocephalus, vascular imaging, contrast MRI, and serial change.

For research use, the key distinction is whether the label means:

  • acute macroscopic intraparenchymal hemorrhage
  • chronic hemosiderin or remote hemorrhagic cavity
  • cerebral microbleeds or superficial siderosis rather than macrohemorrhage
  • hemorrhagic transformation of ischemic infarction
  • hemorrhage caused by tumor, cavernous malformation, AVM, venous thrombosis, aneurysm, coagulopathy, or trauma
  • broad intracranial hemorrhage without location specificity

Known-Diagnosis Review Frame

When the report or cohort label says cerebral hemorrhage, review the image and context for four questions:

  1. Is the blood intra-axial and intraparenchymal, or is it extra-axial, subarachnoid, intraventricular, or punctate microhemorrhage?
  2. What is the age of blood products on CT and MRI, and are there acute, subacute, chronic, or mixed-stage components?
  3. Are there complications that matter for endpoints or severity: volume, edema, mass effect, midline shift, herniation, intraventricular extension, hydrocephalus, or contrast extravasation?
  4. Are there imaging clues to etiology: deep hypertensive pattern, lobar CAA pattern, cavernous malformation, AVM/dural AVF, venous thrombosis, tumor, hemorrhagic transformation, or coagulopathy?

What To Look For

Acute Or Early Presentation

On noncontrast CT, acute intraparenchymal hemorrhage usually appears as a hyperdense parenchymal collection, often with surrounding low-attenuation edema and local mass effect. The report should describe location, size or volume, edema, mass effect, midline shift, herniation, intraventricular extension, and hydrocephalus when present.

On MRI, acute hemorrhage appearance depends on hemoglobin state, sequence, and timing. Susceptibility-sensitive sequences such as SWI or GRE are central for blood-product detection, while T1/T2 signal changes evolve over time.

Radiology-report-style language may include:

  • "Acute intraparenchymal hemorrhage centered in the right basal ganglia with surrounding edema and mild mass effect."
  • "Large left lobar parenchymal hematoma with intraventricular extension and developing hydrocephalus."
  • "Susceptibility blooming corresponding to acute blood products."
  • "No definite enhancing mass is identified on the current examination, although follow-up may be needed after hematoma evolution."

Subacute Or Evolving Presentation

Subacute hemorrhage may show changing T1/T2 signal as methemoglobin develops, decreasing CT density, evolving edema, and possible rim enhancement related to blood-brain barrier disruption and inflammatory response. Mixed-stage blood products can occur when bleeding continues, rebleeding occurs, or there is an underlying lesion.

Report language may include:

  • "Evolving subacute intraparenchymal hematoma with peripheral T1 hyperintensity and susceptibility artifact."
  • "Interval decrease in hematoma density with persistent surrounding edema."
  • "Smooth peripheral enhancement favored to reflect expected subacute hematoma evolution; follow-up is recommended if concern persists for underlying mass."

Chronic Or Stable Residual Presentation

Chronic hemorrhage may leave hemosiderin staining, susceptibility blooming, encephalomalacia, slit-like or cystic cavity, gliosis, and volume loss. Chronic blood products should be distinguished from acute hemorrhage endpoints.

Report language may include:

  • "Chronic hemosiderin-stained cavity in the left basal ganglia compatible with remote hemorrhage."
  • "No acute intracranial hemorrhage; chronic susceptibility and volume loss are unchanged."
  • "Remote intraparenchymal hemorrhage sequelae with surrounding gliosis."

Progressive, Recurrent, Or Worsening Presentation

Worsening may include hematoma expansion, new hemorrhage, increasing edema, new intraventricular extension, worsening mass effect, herniation, or hydrocephalus. CTA contrast extravasation, irregular margins, and hypodense components within a hematoma may be relevant expansion-risk features depending on source and protocol.

Report language may include:

  • "Interval increase in size of the right thalamic hemorrhage with increased intraventricular extension."
  • "Increased surrounding edema and mass effect with greater midline shift."
  • "New acute hemorrhage adjacent to chronic hemosiderin staining."

Improving, Resolving, Or Treatment Response Presentation

Improvement usually means decreasing hematoma size, resolving edema and mass effect, clearing intraventricular blood, improving hydrocephalus, and expected evolution toward chronic hemosiderin/encephalomalacia. Imaging improvement does not necessarily mean causal treatment success unless the endpoint is explicitly defined.

Report language may include:

  • "Expected evolution and decreased size of the previously seen hematoma."
  • "Decreased mass effect and improved ventricular caliber."
  • "No new acute hemorrhage."

Context Clues

Cerebral hemorrhage interpretation is strengthened by:

  • parenchymal hyperdensity on noncontrast CT or susceptibility blooming on MRI
  • location-specific pattern: deep gray nuclei, thalamus, pons, cerebellum, or lobar cortical/subcortical hemorrhage
  • edema, mass effect, intraventricular extension, hydrocephalus, or herniation
  • vascular imaging or contrast MRI evidence for underlying lesion or etiology
  • prior imaging showing interval hematoma evolution

Report Language Patterns

Findings-style language should describe location, size, blood-product stage, edema, mass effect, extension, and associated lesions:

  • "Acute intraparenchymal hematoma in the left thalamus measuring ..."
  • "Surrounding vasogenic edema with partial effacement of the third ventricle."
  • "Intraventricular extension into the lateral ventricles with mild hydrocephalus."

Impression-style language should synthesize acuity and significance:

  • "Acute right basal ganglia intraparenchymal hemorrhage with mild mass effect."
  • "Lobar hemorrhage with additional strictly lobar microhemorrhages; consider amyloid angiopathy in the appropriate clinical context."
  • "No acute hemorrhage; chronic remote hemorrhage sequelae are unchanged."

Primary Imaging Modality

Noncontrast CT is commonly the first-line acute modality because it rapidly detects hyperdense acute blood, hematoma size, mass effect, hydrocephalus, and intraventricular extension. CT angiography can identify vascular lesions or active contrast extravasation in appropriate contexts.

MRI is highly useful for blood-product staging, detecting microhemorrhages and superficial siderosis, identifying chronic hemorrhage, and evaluating underlying etiologies such as cavernous malformation, cerebral amyloid angiopathy, tumor, venous thrombosis, or vascular malformation. Relevant sequences include T1-weighted, T2-weighted, FLAIR, DWI/ADC, SWI/GRE, contrast-enhanced T1, MRA, MRV, and sometimes ASL or perfusion depending on the question.

Other Modalities And When They Matter

CTA, MRA, catheter angiography, CT venography, MR venography, and contrast MRI may be needed when the hemorrhage pattern suggests aneurysm, AVM, dural AVF, venous thrombosis, tumor, vasculopathy, or other secondary cause. Dual-energy CT can help differentiate hemorrhage from calcification or iodine in selected contexts.

Locations And Structural Appearance

Deep Hemispheric, Brainstem, Or Cerebellar Hemorrhage

Deep hemorrhages in basal ganglia, thalamus, internal capsule, brainstem, or cerebellum often suggest hypertensive arteriopathy in the right clinical context. These locations should be separated from lobar hemorrhage phenotypes because etiologic and statistical implications differ.

Lobar Hemorrhage

Lobar hemorrhage involves cortical or subcortical cerebral hemispheric regions. In older adults, lobar hemorrhage with strictly lobar microbleeds, cortical superficial siderosis, or compatible white matter features can suggest cerebral amyloid angiopathy, but other causes such as tumor, vascular malformation, venous thrombosis, coagulopathy, or trauma must be considered.

Hemorrhage With Extension Or Complications

Intraventricular extension, hydrocephalus, herniation, edema, and mass effect should be recorded because they affect severity, outcomes, and treatment-pathway context. Hemorrhage volume and location are important research variables.

Typical Appearance

Typical cerebral hemorrhage appears as a macroscopic parenchymal hematoma with blood-product signal or density, often with edema and mass effect. On CT, acute hematoma is hyperdense and evolves toward iso/hypodensity over time. On MRI, signal depends on hemoglobin degradation products and timing; SWI/GRE blooming is a key blood-product clue after the earliest phase.

Atypical Or Red-Flag Appearance

Features that should trigger caution or additional adjudication:

  • enhancing mass, nodular enhancement, or persistent lesion after hematoma evolution
  • surrounding edema out of proportion to expected hematoma stage
  • multiple hemorrhages of different ages
  • strictly lobar hemorrhagic lesions suggesting possible CAA context
  • vascular flow voids, nidus, prominent draining vein, or arterialized venous signal suggesting AVM/dural AVF
  • venous sinus thrombosis or hemorrhagic venous infarct pattern
  • cortical/subcortical hemorrhage in a tumor-prone or metastatic pattern
  • calcification or mineralization mimicking blood on CT or susceptibility MRI

Differential Diagnosis And Mimics

Quick Guide

  • **Cerebral infarction:** hemorrhagic transformation can mimic primary hemorrhage; DWI, vascular distribution, and temporal course matter.
  • **Silent micro-hemorrhage of brain:** punctate susceptibility foci are not the same endpoint as macroscopic intraparenchymal hematoma.
  • **Cavernous hemangioma/cavernous malformation:** mixed-signal popcorn lesion with hemosiderin rim or associated DVA can underlie hemorrhage.
  • **Subdural intracranial hemorrhage:** extra-axial crescentic collection is not intraparenchymal hemorrhage.
  • **Tumor or metastasis:** hemorrhagic tumors can present as hematomas; follow-up contrast MRI may be needed after blood products evolve.

Information Needed To Resolve Uncertainty

  • noncontrast CT timing and hematoma density
  • MRI sequences available, especially SWI/GRE, T1, T2, FLAIR, DWI/ADC, and contrast-enhanced imaging
  • exact location: lobar, deep hemispheric, brainstem, cerebellar, intraventricular, subarachnoid, or extra-axial
  • hematoma size or volume, edema, mass effect, midline shift, herniation, and hydrocephalus
  • CTA/MRA/MRV/CTV/angiography findings
  • anticoagulation/coagulopathy, hypertension, age, trauma, tumor history, amyloid angiopathy markers, or prior hemorrhage
  • serial imaging for expansion, resolution, or underlying lesion visibility

Quick Differential Diagnosis Guide

Use this guide when the report label is broad or when an agent needs a compact starting point before reading the full differential matrix.

  • Acute intraparenchymal hemorrhage: hyperdense parenchymal hematoma on CT or MRI blood products with edema, mass effect, or intraventricular extension.
  • Hemorrhagic transformation of infarct: hemorrhage follows an arterial infarct pattern and is accompanied by ischemic DWI/ADC or encephalomalacic vascular-territory change.
  • Cerebral microbleeds: punctate susceptibility foci on SWI/GRE rather than a macroscopic parenchymal hematoma.
  • Subdural hemorrhage: crescentic extra-axial blood along the convexity, falx, or tentorium rather than intra-axial parenchymal blood.
  • Hemorrhagic tumor or metastasis: enhancing mass, disproportionate edema, multiplicity, interval growth, or persistent lesion after blood products evolve.
  • Cavernous malformation: "popcorn" mixed-signal lesion with hemosiderin rim, blooming, and possible developmental venous anomaly.
  • Calcification or mineralization: susceptibility or density that can mimic blood; CT density pattern, phase imaging, and stability help separate it.

Similar-Presentation Diseases And Mimic-Aware Comparison

Cerebral hemorrhage is often confused with adjacent labels because report phrases can use broad terms such as "hemorrhage," "blood products," or "susceptibility." The most important mimic-aware split is macrohemorrhage versus microbleed, followed by intra-axial versus extra-axial location and primary hemorrhage versus hemorrhage secondary to infarct, tumor, vascular malformation, or trauma.

For MR-RATE contrasts:

  • Cerebral infarction and lacunar infarct may show hemorrhagic transformation, but the ischemic lesion distribution and DWI/ADC context should drive the primary label.
  • Silent micro-hemorrhage of brain should be reserved for punctate SWI/GRE foci without a macroscopic parenchymal hematoma.
  • Subdural intracranial hemorrhage is extra-axial and usually crescentic, while cerebral hemorrhage is intra-axial.
  • Glioma and metastatic malignant neoplasm can bleed; contrast enhancement, persistent mass, perfusion, spectroscopy, multiplicity, and interval growth help prevent tumor hemorrhage from being mislabeled as primary ICH.
  • Cavernous hemangioma can present with blood products, but the hemosiderin rim, mixed internal signal, and chronic or recurrent appearance are different from an isolated acute hematoma.

Report Language That Supports Or Argues Against Each Diagnosis

Report language supporting cerebral hemorrhage includes phrases such as "acute intraparenchymal hemorrhage," "parenchymal hematoma," "hyperdense hematoma," "surrounding edema," "mass effect," "midline shift," "intraventricular extension," "hydrocephalus," "CTA spot sign," "hemosiderin staining," and "chronic blood products."

Language arguing for an alternate or more specific category includes "punctate microhemorrhages," "cortical superficial siderosis," "crescentic subdural collection," "hemorrhagic transformation of infarct," "enhancing hemorrhagic mass," "cavernous malformation," "developmental venous anomaly," "calcified lesion," or "vascular malformation."

When Additional Imaging Or Clinical Context Helps

Additional context is most useful when etiology, acuity, or label boundaries are unclear. Noncontrast CT helps confirm acute blood and estimate volume; CTA can evaluate active extravasation, aneurysm, AVM, or other vascular lesion; MRI with SWI/GRE helps identify chronic blood products, microbleeds, cavernous malformation, tumor, and CAA-pattern hemorrhagic markers; contrast MRI helps identify hemorrhagic tumor or inflammatory/infectious mimics; MRV/CTV helps when venous thrombosis is possible; follow-up imaging helps reveal an obscured mass or evolving infarct.

Natural History And Clinical Course

Blood-product signal evolves over time. Acute hematoma on CT becomes less dense with clot breakdown. MRI evolves through oxyhemoglobin, deoxyhemoglobin, intracellular methemoglobin, extracellular methemoglobin, and chronic hemosiderin/ferritin-related susceptibility. Edema often evolves over days to weeks. Chronic hemorrhage may leave hemosiderin staining, encephalomalacia, gliosis, and volume loss.

Evidence Of Active Disease, Progression, Or Recurrence

Active or worsening hemorrhage can include hematoma expansion, new hemorrhage, active contrast extravasation, increased edema, new intraventricular extension, worsening hydrocephalus, increasing mass effect, or new herniation. Recurrent hemorrhage should prompt location-specific etiologic review.

Stable Or Chronic Residual Findings

Stable chronic findings include unchanged hemosiderin staining, chronic cavity, encephalomalacia, gliosis, and volume loss. These should be captured as remote hemorrhage sequelae rather than acute hemorrhage endpoints.

Improvement, Treatment Response, Or Resolution

Improvement can include decreasing hematoma size, resolving edema, decreasing mass effect, clearing intraventricular blood, resolving hydrocephalus, and no new hemorrhage. Imaging response should be tied to a defined clinical or study endpoint rather than assumed from appearance alone.

Serial Imaging Assessment And Interval Change

Serial review should state:

  • whether hematoma size or volume changed
  • whether edema, mass effect, shift, herniation, or hydrocephalus changed
  • whether intraventricular extension is new, persistent, or resolving
  • whether new hemorrhages or microhemorrhages are present
  • whether an underlying enhancing mass or vascular lesion is now visible
  • whether chronic sequelae are stable

Treatment, Response, And Outcome Context

Guideline-Based Management Context

Cerebral hemorrhage is usually managed within acute intracerebral hemorrhage and stroke pathways. Imaging supports diagnosis, severity assessment, detection of complications, identification of secondary causes, and follow-up. The disease chapter should not infer a patient-level management decision from imaging alone.

Common Treatment Pathways

Common pathway concepts include acute hemorrhage confirmation, blood pressure and coagulopathy context, neurocritical care monitoring, management of hydrocephalus or mass effect, neurosurgical or endovascular evaluation in selected cases, and secondary prevention based on etiology.

Imaging Appearance After Treatment

After treatment, imaging may show expected hematoma evolution, postoperative or procedural change, decreased mass effect, ventricular drainage effects, or residual chronic hemosiderin. New bleeding, expansion, or persistent enhancing mass should be handled separately.

Evidence Of Treatment Response

Potential imaging markers include stable or decreased hematoma size, decreased edema or mass effect, improved hydrocephalus, absence of new hemorrhage, and stabilized vascular findings. Clinical outcome endpoints remain distinct.

Evidence Of Progression, Recurrence, Or Treatment Failure

Concerning evidence includes hematoma enlargement, new hemorrhage, increased edema/mass effect, worsening hydrocephalus, new intraventricular extension, rebleeding from vascular lesion, or persistent suspicious mass.

Expected Outcomes And Prognostic Factors

Outcome is influenced by hematoma volume, location, intraventricular extension, hydrocephalus, mass effect, herniation, age, neurologic status, anticoagulation, etiology, and comorbid disease. These factors should be explicit covariates or severity features in research datasets.

Statistical Implications Of Treatment And Progression

Treatment and progression context affects:

  • acute hemorrhage endpoint definition
  • hematoma expansion endpoint definition
  • recurrent hemorrhage endpoint definition
  • chronic hemorrhage burden and microbleed covariates
  • subgrouping by lobar versus deep/infratentorial location
  • subgrouping by suspected etiology
  • handling of hemorrhagic transformation versus primary hemorrhage
  • adjustment for anticoagulation, hypertension, tumor, vascular malformation, and follow-up imaging availability

Candidate Endpoints

Potential endpoints include:

  • presence of acute intraparenchymal hemorrhage
  • hemorrhage location category: lobar, deep hemispheric, brainstem, cerebellar
  • hematoma volume or size
  • intraventricular extension
  • hydrocephalus
  • mass effect, midline shift, or herniation
  • hematoma expansion on serial imaging
  • recurrent hemorrhage
  • chronic hemorrhage sequelae
  • suspected etiology category

Clinical Endpoints

Clinical endpoints can include neurologic deterioration, mortality, functional outcome, seizure, need for neurosurgical or intensive care intervention, hydrocephalus-related intervention, recurrent hemorrhage, and discharge or follow-up disposition. These endpoints require clinical context and should not be inferred from imaging alone.

Imaging, Biomarker, And Measurement Endpoints

Imaging endpoints can include hematoma volume or ABC/2 estimate, hematoma expansion, location class, intraventricular extension, hydrocephalus, edema volume, midline shift, herniation, CTA spot sign, number and distribution of microbleeds, cortical superficial siderosis, suspected etiology, and presence of an underlying lesion on follow-up MRI.

Common Covariates And Confounders

Clinical covariates:

  • age, hypertension, anticoagulant/antiplatelet use, coagulopathy, amyloid angiopathy context, malignancy, trauma, vascular malformation history, substance use, renal disease, and baseline neurologic severity

Imaging covariates:

  • location, volume, edema, mass effect, intraventricular extension, hydrocephalus, CTA/MRA/MRV findings, microbleed burden, superficial siderosis, white matter disease, prior infarct, tumor, cavernoma, AVM, venous thrombosis

Acquisition/protocol covariates:

  • CT timing, availability of CTA, availability of MRI with SWI/GRE, contrast-enhanced imaging, DWI/ADC, MRA/MRV, and follow-up imaging

Research-design confounders:

  • mixing primary ICH with hemorrhagic transformation of infarct
  • mixing macrohemorrhage with microbleeds
  • mixing intra-axial and extra-axial hemorrhage labels
  • missing follow-up MRI when tumor or cavernoma is obscured by acute blood
  • not preserving acute versus chronic hemorrhage status

Clinical Covariates

Important clinical covariates include age, blood pressure and hypertension history, anticoagulant or antiplatelet exposure, coagulation status, trauma, malignancy, renal disease, substance exposure, prior hemorrhage, prior infarct, baseline neurologic severity, suspected cerebral amyloid angiopathy, known vascular malformation, and timing from symptom onset to imaging.

Imaging Covariates

Important imaging covariates include hematoma location, hematoma volume, edema, mass effect, midline shift, intraventricular extension, hydrocephalus, CTA/MRA findings, MRV/CTV findings, microbleed burden, superficial siderosis, white matter disease, prior infarct, tumor, cavernoma, AVM, venous thrombosis, calcification, and follow-up imaging availability.

Treatment And Temporal Confounders

Treatment and temporal confounders include reversal of anticoagulation, blood pressure management, surgical evacuation, external ventricular drainage, thrombolysis or thrombectomy context for hemorrhagic transformation, timing of imaging after symptom onset, and whether follow-up imaging occurs before or after hematoma evolution reveals an underlying lesion.

Acquisition And Protocol Confounders

Acquisition confounders include CT slice thickness, beam-hardening or motion artifact, lack of CTA, lack of SWI/GRE, missing DWI/ADC, lack of contrast MRI, limited posterior fossa quality, field strength, incomplete follow-up imaging, and inconsistent measurement technique across time points.

Statistical And Cohort Design Implications

Cohort definitions should preserve location, acuity, macrohemorrhage versus microhemorrhage distinction, and suspected etiology. Broad "hemorrhage" labels are often too coarse for endpoint work. For MR-RATE-style report labels, the minimum useful representation should distinguish acute intraparenchymal hemorrhage, chronic hemorrhage sequelae, microhemorrhages, subdural hemorrhage, hemorrhagic transformation, and hemorrhagic lesion due to tumor or cavernoma when the report supports that distinction.

Recommended cohort variables:

  • acute/subacute/chronic stage
  • CT-confirmed acute hemorrhage yes/no
  • MRI blood-product evidence yes/no
  • macrohemorrhage versus microhemorrhage
  • lobar/deep/infratentorial location
  • intraventricular extension yes/no
  • hydrocephalus yes/no
  • mass effect/midline shift/herniation yes/no
  • suspected etiology category
  • follow-up imaging available yes/no

Sensitivity analyses should test whether results change when excluding hemorrhagic transformation, tumor-related hemorrhage, cavernoma-related hemorrhage, trauma-associated hemorrhage, or report-only chronic sequelae.

Research Design Implications

Research datasets should preserve hemorrhage type, location, acuity, volume, complications, suspected etiology, and whether the label came from CT, MRI, or report-only language. A single broad cerebral hemorrhage label is usually insufficient for outcome, progression, or algorithm-evaluation studies.

Statistical Implications

Statistical analysis should account for confounding by acuity, scan timing, hemorrhage location, hematoma volume, intraventricular extension, clinical severity, anticoagulation, hypertension, tumor, vascular malformation, and follow-up availability. Misclassification analyses should test whether results change when microbleeds, hemorrhagic transformation, extra-axial hemorrhage, or tumor-related hemorrhage are excluded or modeled separately.

Missing Information To Ask For

Ask for:

  • CT and MRI dates relative to symptom onset or prior imaging
  • exact hemorrhage location and whether intra-axial or extra-axial
  • hematoma size/volume and complications
  • CT angiography, MR angiography, venography, or catheter angiography results
  • anticoagulation/coagulopathy, hypertension, trauma, tumor history, known vascular malformation, or suspected CAA context
  • whether the intended endpoint is acute hemorrhage, expansion, recurrence, chronic sequelae, microbleed burden, or etiology classification
