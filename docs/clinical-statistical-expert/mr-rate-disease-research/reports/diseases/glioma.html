Scope

This chapter covers glioma as a brain MRI finding, report label, neuro-oncology phenotype, and research endpoint. It includes adult-type diffuse gliomas under WHO 2021, high-grade and low-grade appearances, treated glioma, recurrent glioma, treatment effect, and major mimics. Pediatric-type entities and rare gliomas are included only when relevant to MR-RATE report language.

Clinical Context

Gliomas are primary CNS tumors with imaging and prognosis that depend on integrated histologic and molecular classification. MRI is central for diagnosis, biopsy/resection planning, response assessment, and surveillance. Imaging can suggest subtype and grade but cannot replace pathology and molecular testing. Adult-type diffuse gliomas are now commonly organized around IDH-mutant astrocytoma, IDH-mutant/1p19q-codeleted oligodendroglioma, and IDH-wildtype glioblastoma.

Known-Diagnosis Review Frame

Characterize lesion location, infiltrative extent, enhancement, necrosis, edema, mass effect, diffusion, perfusion, spectroscopy, hemorrhage, calcification, cortical involvement, corpus callosum crossing, treatment state, and molecular/pathology context. Record whether the report describes suspected, presumed, pathology-confirmed, treated, recurrent, or progressive glioma.

What To Look For

Acute Or Early Presentation

New glioma may appear as an infiltrative T2/FLAIR hyperintense mass with expansion of cortex or white matter. High-grade disease may show enhancement, necrosis, diffusion restriction, increased perfusion, edema, and mass effect.

Report-like language: "Infiltrative T2/FLAIR hyperintense mass centered in the right frontal lobe, concerning for diffuse glioma." "Irregular ring-enhancing necrotic mass with elevated perfusion, favored high-grade glioma."

Subacute Or Evolving Presentation

Evolution can include increasing FLAIR extent, new enhancement, rising perfusion, worsening mass effect, seizure-related change, or transformation from nonenhancing to enhancing disease.

Report-like language: "New nodular enhancement within a previously nonenhancing glioma is concerning for progression or malignant transformation."

Chronic Or Stable Residual Presentation

Stable disease may show unchanged nonenhancing FLAIR abnormality, stable postoperative cavity, stable nonmeasurable enhancement, or chronic treatment change.

Report-like language: "Stable nonenhancing infiltrative FLAIR abnormality consistent with treated glioma."

Progressive, Recurrent, Or Worsening Presentation

Progression includes new or enlarging enhancement, increasing nonenhancing tumor, new measurable lesion, rising perfusion, worsening edema, new mass effect, or clinical decline. Early postradiation change requires caution for pseudoprogression.

Report-like language: "Increased nodular enhancement and elevated rCBV along the resection cavity are concerning for recurrent tumor."

Improving, Resolving, Or Treatment-Response Presentation

Response may mean decreased enhancing disease, decreased nonenhancing tumor in IDH-mutant glioma, reduced edema, stable or reduced steroid dose, and stable clinical status. Apparent improvement after antiangiogenic therapy can reflect pseudoresponse.

Report-like language: "Decreased enhancement with stable FLAIR abnormality; interpret in the context of steroid and antiangiogenic therapy exposure."

Context Clues

Pathology and molecular markers, treatment date, radiation field, steroid dose, antiangiogenic therapy, immunotherapy, seizure timing, surgery date, and prior MRI strongly affect interpretation.

Report Language Patterns

Findings-style language should describe location, infiltrative extent, enhancement, nonenhancing FLAIR, diffusion, perfusion, edema, necrosis, hemorrhage, calcification, mass effect, resection cavity, and comparison.

Impression-style language may say: "favored diffuse glioma," "high-grade glioma is suspected," "treated glioma without progression," "progression versus treatment effect," or "radiation necrosis favored."

Uncertainty language should separate pathology-confirmed glioma from presumed, favored, suspected, differential-only, or nonspecific mass labels.

Primary Imaging Modality

MRI with and without contrast is primary. T1 evaluates anatomy and enhancement; T2/FLAIR defines infiltrative nonenhancing tumor and edema; DWI/ADC supports cellularity and infarct/abscess mimic review; SWI/GRE evaluates blood products and calcification; postcontrast T1 defines enhancing disease; perfusion, spectroscopy, PET, tractography, and volumetry support selected grading, recurrence, treatment-effect, and planning questions.

Other Modalities And When They Matter

CT helps with calcification, hemorrhage, hydrocephalus, and urgent mass effect. PET can help with tumor extent or recurrence in selected contexts. Pathology and molecular testing are required for integrated diagnosis. EEG, neurocognitive testing, and clinical status can matter for seizure and functional endpoints.

Locations And Structural Appearance

Glioma is usually intra-axial. Cortical/subcortical tumors may present with seizures; deep white matter and corpus callosum involvement suggest infiltrative high-grade disease; brainstem/thalamic/midline tumors require distinct classification; cerebellar and pediatric-type tumors need age-specific caution; postoperative regions require treatment-effect review.

Typical Appearance

Typical diffuse glioma is an infiltrative intra-axial T2/FLAIR hyperintense mass. Lower-grade IDH-mutant tumors are often nonenhancing or minimally enhancing. Glioblastoma often shows irregular rim enhancement, necrosis, edema, mass effect, infiltrative FLAIR, and high perfusion.

Atypical Or Red-Flag Appearance

Red flags include rapid growth, necrosis, irregular enhancement, marked perfusion, corpus callosum crossing, ependymal/leptomeningeal spread, hemorrhage, new diffusion restriction, or posttreatment enlargement where progression and treatment effect overlap.

Differential Diagnosis And Mimics

Use this section for enhancing or infiltrative intra-axial masses, nonenhancing FLAIR lesions, ring-enhancing lesions, and posttreatment change.

Quick Differential Diagnosis Guide

Common mimics include brain metastasis, abscess, lymphoma, tumefactive demyelination, subacute infarct, radiation necrosis, pseudoprogression, cavernous malformation, encephalitis, and treatment-related gliosis.

Key Imaging Discriminators

Infiltrative FLAIR beyond enhancement, cortical/subcortical expansion, callosal spread, elevated perfusion, and persistent growth support glioma. Multiple gray-white junction lesions support metastases; central restricted diffusion supports abscess; open-ring enhancement supports demyelination; low perfusion after radiation supports necrosis.

Similar-Presentation Diseases And Mimic-Aware Comparison

Glioma overlaps with many mass, edema, hemorrhage, infarct, and posttreatment categories. A generic brain mass or enhancing lesion should not be counted as glioma without favored glioma language, pathology, or adjudication.

Report Language That Supports Or Argues Against Each Diagnosis

Supports glioma: infiltrative glioma, diffuse glioma, glioblastoma, astrocytoma, oligodendroglioma, nonenhancing glioma, recurrent glioma. Argues against: metastasis, abscess, lymphoma, demyelination, infarct, radiation necrosis, cavernous malformation.

When Additional Imaging Or Clinical Context Helps

Prior MRI, pathology, IDH/1p19q/MGMT/H3 status, treatment dates, steroid dose, perfusion, spectroscopy, amino-acid PET, DWI, SWI, and surgical/radiation history help resolve uncertain cases.

Natural History And Clinical Course

Course varies by entity and molecular markers. IDH-mutant gliomas often grow more slowly but can transform. IDH-wildtype glioblastoma is aggressive. Recurrence is common after treatment, and distinguishing progression from treatment effect is a central imaging problem.

Treatment, Response, And Outcome Context

Treatment pathways combine surgery, radiation, systemic therapy, tumor treating fields, observation, and clinical trials depending on tumor type, grade, molecular profile, symptoms, location, and performance status.

Guideline-Based Management Context

EANO, NICE, ACR, WHO, BTIP, and RANO sources frame diagnosis, imaging protocol, surgical/pathology integration, adjuvant therapy, follow-up, and response assessment.

Common Treatment Pathways

Observation for selected low-risk lesions; maximal safe resection or biopsy; radiotherapy; temozolomide or PCV depending on entity; tumor treating fields for selected glioblastoma; salvage surgery, reirradiation, systemic therapy, or trials.

Imaging Appearance After Treatment

Postoperative enhancement, ischemia, blood products, edema, gliosis, pseudoprogression, radiation necrosis, pseudoresponse, and recurrent tumor can overlap.

Evidence Of Treatment Response

Response may include decreased enhancement, decreased nonenhancing tumor where applicable, stable/decreased steroid dose, improved symptoms, no new lesions, and RANO-consistent measurement change.

Evidence Of Progression, Recurrence, Or Treatment Failure

Progression includes new/enlarging measurable enhancement, new measurable nonenhancing tumor when relevant, new lesions, rising perfusion, clinical decline, and changes confirmed when pseudoprogression is plausible.

Expected Outcomes And Prognostic Factors

Outcomes depend on age, performance status, tumor type, grade, IDH, 1p/19q, MGMT, CDKN2A/B, extent of resection, treatment, location, tumor volume, and neurologic function.

Statistical Implications Of Treatment And Progression

Treatment and surveillance are confounded by tumor type, grade, resection extent, molecular markers, steroid use, and performance status. Pseudoprogression and pseudoresponse create endpoint ambiguity.

Evidence Of Active Disease, Progression, Or Recurrence

Active disease is suggested by interval growth, new enhancement, new nonenhancing tumor, high perfusion, new mass effect, ependymal/leptomeningeal spread, or recurrent nodular enhancement.

Stable Or Chronic Residual Findings

Stability requires comparable MRI and consistent measurement. Stable postoperative FLAIR, gliosis, cavity enhancement, or treatment change should not be mislabeled progression.

Improvement, Treatment Response, Or Resolution

Improvement includes decreasing tumor measurements, stable/decreased steroids, improved symptoms, and no new lesions. Enhancement decrease after antiangiogenic therapy requires caution.

Serial Imaging Assessment And Interval Change

Compare the same sequences, measurement planes, target/non-target lesions, enhancement, FLAIR, diffusion, perfusion, steroid dose, treatment dates, and clinical status.

Clinical Endpoints

Overall survival, progression-free survival, seizure control, neurologic function, steroid requirement, quality of life, neurocognition, extent of resection, recurrence, treatment toxicity, and functional status.

Imaging, Biomarker, And Measurement Endpoints

Enhancing tumor size/volume, nonenhancing FLAIR volume, rCBV, ADC, spectroscopy metabolites, PET uptake, extent of resection, residual disease, new lesions, RANO response, pseudoprogression, and radiation necrosis.

Common Covariates And Confounders

The main confounders are molecular subtype, grade, treatment timing, steroid dose, antiangiogenic therapy, seizure, radiation field, scanner protocol, and baseline disease burden.

Clinical Covariates

Age, sex, performance status, neurologic deficit, seizure, steroid use, pathology, molecular markers, treatment history, and clinical trial status.

Imaging Covariates

MRI protocol, lesion size, enhancement, FLAIR extent, edema, necrosis, diffusion, perfusion, spectroscopy, PET, location, multifocality, and prior imaging interval.

Treatment And Temporal Confounders

Surgery, biopsy, radiation, chemotherapy, immunotherapy, TTFields, antiangiogenic therapy, steroid changes, and time since treatment.

Acquisition And Protocol Confounders

Field strength, slice thickness, 2D/3D postcontrast T1, contrast timing, FLAIR technique, perfusion method, motion, and site/vendor effects.

Research Design Implications

Stratify by integrated diagnosis, grade, molecular markers, treatment state, and response-assessment method. Adjudicate indeterminate treatment effect and differential-only labels.

Statistical Implications

Glioma cohorts need careful label definitions: suspected versus confirmed, enhancing versus nonenhancing, treated versus untreated, progression versus treatment effect, and WHO/molecular subtype. Time-to-event endpoints must handle interval censoring, pseudoprogression, competing risks, and surveillance bias.

Missing Information To Ask For

Ask for pathology, molecular markers, MRI sequences, treatment dates, steroid dose, prior scans, lesion measurements, symptoms, and whether the report diagnosis is confirmed, favored, suspected, or differential-only.

Safety And Claim Boundaries

This chapter supports research planning, imaging-feature extraction, report-language interpretation, cohort definition, endpoint selection, covariate review, and mimic-aware evidence review. It is not a substitute for qualified clinical interpretation or treatment planning.
