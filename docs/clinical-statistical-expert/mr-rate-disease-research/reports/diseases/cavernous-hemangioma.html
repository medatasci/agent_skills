Scope

This chapter covers intracranial cavernous hemangioma as a brain MRI finding, report label, and research phenotype. Because the MR-RATE original label is "Cavernous malformation," this chapter uses the modern clinical term cerebral cavernous malformation while preserving the ontology name cavernous hemangioma.

The chapter includes sporadic solitary lesions, familial or multiple lesions, brainstem and eloquent-location lesions, DVA-associated lesions, hemorrhagic presentations, seizure-associated presentations, and susceptibility-only punctate lesions when the source context supports a CCM interpretation. It does not cover hepatic, orbital, cutaneous, or other extracranial cavernous hemangiomas except when they affect familial context.

Clinical Context

Cerebral cavernous malformations are low-flow vascular malformations composed of clustered dilated vascular channels without normal intervening brain parenchyma. They can be incidental or can present with seizure, headache, focal neurologic deficit, or hemorrhage. MRI is the main diagnostic modality. Classic lesions have a mixed-signal reticulated core from blood products of different ages and a surrounding hypointense hemosiderin rim. GRE and SWI are important because they accentuate susceptibility blooming.

Sporadic lesions are often solitary and may be associated with a developmental venous anomaly. Familial disease is more likely when there are multiple lesions without a DVA, a family history, or a known KRIT1/CCM1, CCM2, or PDCD10/CCM3 variant. Radiation-induced lesions are another important context.

For research use, the key distinction is whether the label means:

  • a classic solitary cavernous malformation
  • a hemorrhagic cavernous malformation
  • multiple familial cavernous malformations
  • punctate susceptibility-only lesions suspected to be small CCMs
  • an associated DVA rather than the CCM itself
  • a hemorrhagic tumor, AVM, microbleed, calcification, or other mimic

Known-Diagnosis Review Frame

When the diagnosis or report label is cavernous hemangioma or cavernous malformation, characterize:

  • lesion number: solitary, few, multiple, innumerable, or familial-pattern
  • lesion morphology: mixed-signal popcorn core, hemosiderin rim, punctate susceptibility-only focus, or acute hematoma-dominant lesion
  • lesion location: cortical/subcortical, deep gray, brainstem, cerebellar, intraventricular, insular, spinal, or eloquent cortex
  • hemorrhage state: acute/subacute blood, chronic hemosiderin, prior extralesional hemorrhage, or interval hemorrhage
  • DVA association: visible enhancing venous drainage adjacent to the lesion
  • associated findings: edema, mass effect, calcification, enhancement, seizure-related cortical location, or family/radiation context
  • mimics: hemorrhagic metastasis or glioma, cerebral microbleeds, CAA, hypertensive microangiopathy, AVM, capillary telangiectasia, calcification, radiation microbleeds, and traumatic microhemorrhages

What To Look For

Acute Or Early Presentation

An acute hemorrhagic presentation may show a parenchymal hematoma or a lesion partly obscured by acute blood. MRI may show T1 and T2 signal reflecting recent blood products, surrounding edema, and susceptibility blooming. CT may identify acute hemorrhage or calcification, but MRI is needed to identify the underlying lesion when safe and available.

Report-like language:

  • "Acute hemorrhage centered in a lesion with peripheral susceptibility, suspicious for hemorrhagic cavernous malformation."
  • "Underlying cavernous malformation is suspected; follow-up contrast-enhanced MRI after blood-product evolution may better exclude hemorrhagic mass or vascular lesion."
  • "Associated developmental venous anomaly is present adjacent to the hemorrhagic lesion."

Subacute Or Evolving Presentation

Subacute lesions may show methemoglobin-related T1 hyperintensity, a mixed T1 and T2 core, evolving edema, and a dark hemosiderin rim. A lesion that becomes more recognizable as blood products evolve can support a cavernous malformation interpretation.

Report-like language:

  • "Mixed-signal lesion with subacute blood products and a peripheral hemosiderin rim, compatible with cavernous malformation."
  • "Interval decrease in surrounding edema with persistent hemosiderin-rimmed mixed-signal lesion."
  • "No definite arterial flow void or high-flow vascular nidus is seen."

Chronic Or Stable Residual Presentation

The classic chronic appearance is a well-circumscribed mixed-signal, reticulated "popcorn" or "mulberry" lesion surrounded by a hypointense hemosiderin rim on T2, GRE, or SWI. Chronic resolved lesions may be mostly hypointense from hemosiderin. Small lesions may only be visible as punctate susceptibility foci on GRE or SWI.

Report-like language:

  • "Stable hemosiderin-rimmed mixed-signal lesion compatible with cavernous malformation."
  • "Scattered punctate susceptibility foci; in the setting of known familial cavernous malformation, these likely represent additional small cavernomas."
  • "No new edema, mass effect, or acute hemorrhage."

Progressive, Recurrent, Or Worsening Presentation

Concerning change includes new hemorrhage, lesion enlargement, increasing edema, new mass effect, new or worsening focal neurologic symptoms, new seizure activity, increasing number of lesions in familial/radiation contexts, or a new enhancing mass-like component that raises concern for tumor.

Report-like language:

  • "New hemorrhagic component within the known cavernous malformation."
  • "Interval enlargement and increased surrounding edema; hemorrhage related to cavernous malformation is favored, although hemorrhagic neoplasm should be excluded if atypical features persist."
  • "New susceptibility-only lesions are present compared with prior SWI, compatible with interval lesion accrual in familial cavernous malformation."

Improving, Resolving, Or Treatment-Response Presentation

Improvement usually means resolution of acute edema or mass effect, involution of hematoma, or stable postoperative cavity without residual symptomatic hemorrhage. The hemosiderin rim, chronic susceptibility, and gliotic margin may persist. After resection, expected findings include postoperative change, hemosiderin staining, gliosis, and possible adjacent DVA preservation.

Report-like language:

  • "Decreased edema surrounding the known cavernous malformation with expected evolution of blood products."
  • "Postoperative changes of cavernous malformation resection without residual mass-like enhancement."
  • "Persistent hemosiderin staining at the resection margin."

Context Clues

  • Multiple lesions without DVA or radiation history support familial CCM consideration.
  • An adjacent DVA supports sporadic DVA-associated CCM but should not itself be mistaken for the cavernous malformation.
  • Brainstem, deep gray, and eloquent cortex lesions have different clinical stakes than incidental superficial lesions.
  • Seizure presentation is more relevant for cortical/supratentorial lesions.
  • Prior hemorrhage and brainstem location are important natural-history variables in source literature.

Report Language Patterns

Findings-style language:

  • "Well-circumscribed mixed-signal lesion with a peripheral hemosiderin rim."
  • "Susceptibility blooming on GRE/SWI."
  • "Reticulated internal signal compatible with blood products of varying age."
  • "Adjacent developmental venous anomaly."
  • "No arterial flow voids or high-flow vascular nidus."
  • "No surrounding edema or mass effect" when stable and uncomplicated.

Impression-style language:

  • "Imaging appearance is compatible with cavernous malformation."
  • "Hemorrhagic cavernous malformation is favored."
  • "Multiple cavernous malformations, raising concern for familial cavernous malformation syndrome in the appropriate context."
  • "Hemorrhagic mass cannot be excluded; follow-up contrast MRI is advised in the research/adjudication context if the appearance is atypical."

Uncertainty and cohort-label language:

  • Confident label: classic popcorn lesion, hemosiderin rim, SWI blooming, and stable DVA-associated lesion.
  • Probable label: hemorrhagic lesion with DVA and no high-flow vascular features, especially after interval evolution reveals a cavernoma.
  • Low-confidence label: isolated punctate susceptibility focus without family history, larger classic lesions, or supporting context.
  • Exclusion/adjudication trigger: marked progressive enhancement, persistent mass-like edema, arterialized flow voids, vascular nidus, multiplicity matching metastases, or unclear calcification.

Primary Imaging Modality

MRI is the primary modality. It detects the mixed-signal core, hemosiderin rim, small susceptibility-only lesions, edema, DVA association, and mimics better than CT or angiography.

  • T1: may show methemoglobin-related hyperintensity in subacute hemorrhage; chronic lesions can be mixed or low signal.
  • T2: classic lesions show a reticulated mixed-signal core with a dark hemosiderin rim; chronic lesions may be predominantly hypointense.
  • FLAIR: helps evaluate edema, gliosis, and adjacent parenchymal injury.
  • DWI/ADC: not primary for diagnosis; useful to assess infarct, abscess, tumor, or atypical hemorrhagic lesion mimics.
  • SWI/GRE: highlights hemosiderin and blooming; essential for small lesions, familial lesion burden, and microhemorrhagic components, but can exaggerate lesion size.
  • Post-contrast T1: helps delineate associated DVA and assess tumor or other enhancing mimics; CCM enhancement can be variable.
  • Advanced methods: DTI, functional MRI, tractography, perfusion, and spectroscopy may support preoperative planning or mimic assessment rather than routine diagnosis.

Other Modalities And When They Matter

CT can identify acute hemorrhage or calcification in symptomatic settings but is less sensitive and less specific for cavernous malformation than MRI. Catheter angiography is often negative because CCMs are low-flow and angiographically occult; it is mainly relevant when AVM, fistula, aneurysm, or another high-flow vascular lesion remains in the differential. CTA or MRA may help when the hemorrhage pattern suggests an arterial lesion. Genetic testing matters when there are multiple lesions, family history, or no DVA/radiation explanation. Pathology may be the reference standard after surgery.

Locations And Structural Appearance

  • Cortical/subcortical lesions often present with seizure or incidental MRI findings.
  • Deep gray, thalamic, insular, and brainstem lesions carry higher functional stakes and more complex treatment decisions.
  • Cerebellar lesions may present with ataxia, hemorrhage, or incidental findings.
  • Intraventricular or extra-axial-appearing lesions can be atypical and may need expert review.
  • Multiple lesions raise familial, radiation-induced, or diffuse mimics.
  • DVA-associated lesions often occur adjacent to a venous drainage anomaly and may be sporadic.

Typical Appearance

Typical cavernous malformation appears as a small to moderate, well-defined, mixed-signal lesion with a reticulated core and a peripheral dark hemosiderin rim, often blooming on GRE or SWI. It may have little edema or mass effect when stable. An associated DVA can strengthen the interpretation.

Atypical Or Red-Flag Appearance

Red flags include disproportionate edema, progressive or nodular enhancement, restricted diffusion not explained by blood products, persistent mass-like growth, numerous lesions without familial/radiation context, arterial flow voids, vascular nidus, subarachnoid-predominant hemorrhage, or lesion behavior that does not fit blood-product evolution. These features should trigger adjudication for tumor, AVM, venous thrombosis, abscess, calcification, or other hemorrhagic mimics.

Differential Diagnosis And Mimics

Quick Differential Diagnosis Guide

  • Cerebral hemorrhage: acute macrohemorrhage may obscure the cavernoma; look for a persistent hemosiderin-rimmed mixed-signal lesion after evolution.
  • Silent micro-hemorrhage of brain: punctate SWI foci can mimic Type IV or familial CCM; supporting context matters.
  • Hemorrhagic metastasis or glioma: consider when there is persistent nodular enhancement, progressive mass, multiplicity in a metastatic pattern, or disproportionate edema.
  • AVM or dural AVF: arterialized flow voids, nidus, early venous drainage, or angiographic abnormality argues away from classic CCM.
  • Developmental venous anomaly: a DVA may coexist with a CCM but is not the lesion itself.
  • Calcification/mineralization: CT density, phase behavior, and stability can help separate mineralization from blood-product susceptibility.
  • Capillary telangiectasia: often faintly enhancing and usually not a popcorn-like hemosiderin-rimmed lesion.

Key Imaging Discriminators

  • Location and distribution: solitary DVA-associated lesions differ from multiple familial lesions and from diffuse CAA/hypertension microbleeds.
  • Lesion morphology: mixed-signal reticulated core and dark hemosiderin rim support CCM.
  • Mass effect and edema: mild or absent when stable; prominent or progressive edema needs mimic review.
  • Diffusion: not a defining feature; unexpected restricted diffusion can point to infarct, abscess, tumor, or acute blood-product effects.
  • Enhancement: DVA enhancement is helpful; persistent nodular tumor-like enhancement needs adjudication.
  • Susceptibility: blooming supports blood products but is not specific; compare morphology and context.
  • Interval change: blood-product evolution can reveal the underlying lesion; persistent growth or new enhancing mass is concerning.

Similar-Presentation Diseases And Mimic-Aware Comparison

The highest-yield MR-RATE contrasts are cerebral hemorrhage, silent micro-hemorrhage of brain, cavernous malformation, subdural hemorrhage, glioma, brain metastasis, intracranial aneurysm/AVM-related hemorrhage, cerebral edema, gliosis, and encephalomalacia. The chapter should be used to prevent a broad "hemorrhage" label from swallowing a specific CCM phenotype and to prevent small nonspecific susceptibility foci from being overcalled as CCM without supporting context.

When Additional Imaging Or Clinical Context Helps

Additional context helps when the lesion is acutely hemorrhagic, mass-like, punctate, multiple, or treatment-associated. Seek prior MRI, SWI/GRE, contrast-enhanced MRI, CT for calcification, CTA/MRA/DSA if a high-flow lesion is suspected, family history, genetic testing context, radiation history, oncology history, and symptom timing.

Natural History And Clinical Course

CCMs may remain incidental and stable, bleed, produce seizures, grow or change with repeated hemorrhage, or appear as new lesions in familial or radiation contexts. Natural-history sources consistently emphasize that prior symptomatic hemorrhage and brainstem location are important risk variables. Lesion number, familial status, DVA association, and imaging protocol also affect observed burden and interpretation.

Treatment, Response, And Outcome Context

Use this section for study-design context only. Treatment decisions depend on symptoms, location, prior hemorrhage, seizure burden, surgical accessibility, patient factors, and expert judgment.

Guideline-Based Management Context

The Alliance to Cure Cavernous Malformation consensus guidelines provide the most relevant disease-specific management framework. They support MRI-based diagnosis using T1, T2, susceptibility-sensitive sequences, DWI/FLAIR, and contrast when tumor or DVA evaluation is relevant. They also frame genetic testing, family history, follow-up imaging for new or worsened symptoms, and the limited role of catheter angiography unless AVM is a concern.

Common Treatment Pathways

Common pathways include observation and surveillance, antiseizure medication for CCM-related seizures, surgical resection for selected symptomatic lesions, stereotactic radiosurgery in selected difficult cases, genetic counseling for familial disease, and follow-up imaging after new symptoms or hemorrhage.

Imaging Appearance After Treatment

Posttreatment imaging may show postoperative cavity, gliosis, hemosiderin staining, encephalomalacia, blood-product evolution, or residual/recurrent lesion. Associated DVA is often preserved in surgical planning. Radiation or radiosurgery history can create treatment-related imaging changes and confounding.

Evidence Of Treatment Response

Treatment response may be described by seizure control, absence of recurrent hemorrhage, stable or resolved edema, lesion removal, functional status, or symptom improvement. Imaging response should not be reduced to lesion disappearance unless resection status is known.

Evidence Of Progression, Recurrence, Or Treatment Failure

Progression or recurrence may be suggested by new hemorrhage, recurrent same site bleeding, lesion enlargement, new lesions in familial/radiation contexts, increasing edema, persistent symptomatic seizures, residual lesion after resection, or new neurologic deficit.

Expected Outcomes And Prognostic Factors

Important prognostic factors include prior hemorrhage, brainstem or eloquent location, seizure presentation, lesion number, familial genotype, DVA association, age, treatment selection, and follow-up duration. Outcome endpoints must specify whether they measure hemorrhage, seizure control, focal neurologic deficit, functional outcome, recurrence, or imaging stability.

Statistical Implications Of Treatment And Progression

Treatment and progression create confounding by indication: symptomatic, brainstem, hemorrhagic, or surgically accessible lesions are not exchangeable with incidental lesions. Analyses should account for prior hemorrhage, location, baseline symptoms, surveillance intensity, genetic status, treatment timing, and follow-up interval.

Evidence Of Active Disease, Progression, Or Recurrence

Active or worsening disease may include acute/subacute hemorrhage, new edema, new or worsening neurologic symptoms, interval lesion enlargement, new lesions, or recurrent hemorrhage. These findings require protocol-comparable imaging and clinical context.

Stable Or Chronic Residual Findings

Stable chronic findings include an unchanged hemosiderin-rimmed mixed-signal lesion, chronic susceptibility, stable postoperative change, and no new edema, mass effect, or hemorrhage. Stability is strongest when prior imaging includes comparable susceptibility-sensitive sequences.

Improvement, Treatment Response, Or Resolution

Improvement may include resolving hematoma, reduced edema, seizure control, stable postoperative imaging, or no interval hemorrhage. Persistent hemosiderin or gliosis can remain after clinical improvement and should not be overcalled as active progression.

Serial Imaging Assessment And Interval Change

Compare lesion size, blood-product stage, edema, mass effect, enhancement, susceptibility burden, DVA visibility, number of lesions, and sequence comparability. SWI/GRE field strength and slice thickness can change apparent lesion count or size. If prior imaging lacks susceptibility-sensitive sequences, apparent new lesions may reflect improved detection.

Clinical Endpoints

Common clinical endpoints include symptomatic intracranial hemorrhage, new focal neurologic deficit, seizure onset, seizure recurrence, seizure freedom, headache burden when defined, need for surgery or radiosurgery, functional status, neurologic worsening, and mortality.

Imaging, Biomarker, And Measurement Endpoints

Imaging endpoints include lesion presence, lesion count, lesion location, Zabramski type, acute/subacute hemorrhage, edema, mass effect, lesion size, brainstem/eloquent location, DVA association, new lesion accrual, postoperative residual lesion, and SWI/GRE-visible lesion burden. Genetic endpoints include KRIT1/CCM1, CCM2, and PDCD10/CCM3 variant status when available.

Common Covariates And Confounders

Clinical Covariates

  • age and sex
  • symptom presentation: incidental, seizure, hemorrhage, focal neurologic deficit, headache
  • prior symptomatic hemorrhage
  • seizure history and antiseizure medication exposure
  • family history and genetic status
  • radiation exposure
  • pregnancy or hormonal context when relevant to the study question
  • clinical indication for imaging

Imaging Covariates

  • lesion location, especially brainstem, deep gray, cerebellar, cortical, or eloquent cortex
  • lesion count and distribution
  • DVA association
  • Zabramski type or acute/subacute/chronic blood-product state
  • edema, mass effect, enhancement, calcification, and hemorrhage
  • SWI/GRE availability, field strength, and sequence parameters
  • follow-up imaging interval and prior-imaging comparability

Treatment And Temporal Confounders

  • observation, surgery, radiosurgery, antiseizure medication, and timing
  • treatment selection driven by symptoms, location, hemorrhage, or accessibility
  • time from symptom onset or hemorrhage to imaging
  • follow-up schedule and surveillance intensity
  • residual lesion, postoperative blood products, and radiation effects

Acquisition And Protocol Confounders

  • scanner field strength
  • SWI versus GRE availability
  • slice thickness and echo time
  • post-contrast imaging availability
  • CT availability for calcification or acute hemorrhage
  • motion and posterior fossa artifact
  • missing sequences or noncomparable prior studies

Research Design Implications

  • Adjust for prior hemorrhage, lesion location, lesion count, familial status, DVA association, treatment status, and follow-up interval.
  • Stratify by incidental versus symptomatic presentation, brainstem versus nonbrainstem location, solitary versus multiple lesions, and treated versus untreated lesions.
  • Exclude or adjudicate atypical hemorrhagic masses, AVMs, metastases, nonspecific microbleeds, and calcifications.
  • Use sensitivity analyses for punctate susceptibility-only lesions and report-only labels without SWI/GRE.

Statistical Implications

Cohort definitions should distinguish classic CCM, hemorrhagic CCM, familial/multiple CCM, DVA-associated CCM, brainstem CCM, and susceptibility-only possible CCM. Endpoint choice should separate hemorrhage, seizure, focal neurologic deficit, lesion growth, new lesion accrual, treatment response, and postoperative recurrence. Models should avoid immortal time bias and confounding by indication when comparing treatment groups. Claims should state whether data come from adult, pediatric, brainstem, seizure, surgical, radiosurgery, familial, or untreated cohorts.

Missing Information To Ask For

Ask for:

  • MRI sequences available, especially SWI or GRE
  • whether contrast MRI was performed
  • lesion location, number, and size
  • whether there is an associated DVA
  • acute/subacute hemorrhage, edema, or mass effect
  • prior imaging and interval change
  • symptoms: incidental, seizure, focal deficit, hemorrhage, headache
  • family history, genetic testing, radiation exposure, or known familial CCM
  • treatment history: observation, surgery, radiosurgery, antiseizure medication
