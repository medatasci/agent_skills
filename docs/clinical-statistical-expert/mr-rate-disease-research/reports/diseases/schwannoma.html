Scope

This chapter covers intracranial and skull base schwannomas as imaging findings, report labels, and research phenotypes. Vestibular schwannoma is the dominant intracranial pattern, but nonvestibular cranial nerve schwannomas are included when relevant. Peripheral spinal or soft-tissue schwannomas are outside scope except for syndrome context.

Clinical Context

Schwannomas are benign nerve sheath tumors. Vestibular schwannomas most often arise from the vestibular portion of cranial nerve VIII and present as an enhancing mass in the internal auditory canal and/or cerebellopontine angle. MRI with contrast is the key diagnostic and surveillance study. Larger lesions may compress the brainstem, cerebellum, trigeminal/facial nerves, or fourth ventricle. Bilateral vestibular schwannomas or multiple schwannomas raise syndrome context such as NF2-related schwannomatosis.

Known-Diagnosis Review Frame

Characterize nerve/location, intracanalicular versus cisternal component, size, Koos/Hannover stage, IAC widening, enhancement pattern, cystic change, brainstem contact/compression, hydrocephalus, hearing/vestibular context, NF2/schwannomatosis context, treatment state, and interval growth.

What To Look For

Acute Or Early Presentation

Newly detected vestibular schwannoma often appears as a small enhancing intracanalicular mass or an enhancing CPA/IAC mass. It may be T1 iso- to hypointense, T2 hyperintense, and avidly enhancing.

Report-like language: "Enhancing mass within the right internal auditory canal extending into the cerebellopontine angle, most compatible with vestibular schwannoma."

Subacute Or Evolving Presentation

Evolution is typically interval growth, increasing CPA component, increasing brainstem/cerebellar contact, cystic degeneration, or worsening mass effect.

Report-like language: "Interval enlargement of the left vestibular schwannoma with increased contact of the lateral pons."

Chronic Or Stable Residual Presentation

Stable schwannoma may remain unchanged for years under observation or after treatment. Stable posttreatment lesions may show central hypoenhancement or reduced volume.

Report-like language: "Stable intracanalicular vestibular schwannoma without CPA extension."

Progressive, Recurrent, Or Worsening Presentation

Progression is suggested by measurable growth, increasing Koos grade, new brainstem compression, hydrocephalus, recurrent growth after surgery, or sustained enlargement after the expected postradiation pseudoprogression window.

Report-like language: "Progressive residual vestibular schwannoma in the left CPA with increased mass effect on the brainstem."

Improving, Resolving, Or Treatment-Response Presentation

Response after radiosurgery may be stable size, decreased volume, or central loss of enhancement. Transient enlargement can occur and should not be automatically labeled failure.

Report-like language: "Post-SRS vestibular schwannoma with decreased central enhancement and stable overall size."

Context Clues

Hearing loss, tinnitus, vestibular symptoms, trigeminal/facial symptoms, NF2/schwannomatosis, prior surgery, radiosurgery date, cochlear implant planning, and audiometry change interpretation.

Report Language Patterns

Findings-style language should describe exact nerve/compartment, IAC/CPA extent, size, enhancement, cystic change, fundal extension, brainstem compression, hydrocephalus, treatment state, and comparison. Impression-style language should distinguish confident schwannoma from CPA/IAC mass, meningioma, metastasis, epidermoid, or arachnoid cyst.

Primary Imaging Modality

MRI with and without contrast is primary. Thin-section IAC protocol, 3D T2/CISS/FIESTA/SSFP, and postcontrast T1 define the nerve, labyrinth, IAC, CPA, brainstem contact, and postoperative/radiation changes.

Other Modalities And When They Matter

CT helps with IAC widening, bone remodeling, and skull base anatomy. Audiometry, vestibular testing, facial/trigeminal nerve exam, genetic testing, and treatment records matter for interpretation.

Locations And Structural Appearance

Vestibular schwannoma is centered on CN VIII in the IAC/CPA. Facial nerve schwannoma follows the facial nerve canal. Trigeminal schwannoma involves Meckel cave/cavernous sinus with possible dumbbell shape. Jugular foramen schwannoma expands the jugular foramen. Brainstem/cerebellum are compressed rather than invaded in typical benign disease.

Typical Appearance

Typical vestibular schwannoma is an enhancing intracanalicular or CPA/IAC mass, often with an ice-cream-cone configuration and possible IAC widening. Larger tumors may be heterogeneous or cystic.

Atypical Or Red-Flag Appearance

Red flags include rapid growth, acute symptom progression, marked edema, leptomeningeal disease, multiple CPA/IAC lesions with known cancer, aggressive bone destruction, atypical restriction/perfusion, or discordance with expected nerve location.

Differential Diagnosis And Mimics

Use this section for CPA/IAC masses, skull base nerve tumors, and treated lesions.

Quick Differential Diagnosis Guide

Common mimics are meningioma, epidermoid cyst, arachnoid cyst, CPA/IAC metastasis, facial nerve schwannoma, trigeminal schwannoma, paraganglioma, lipoma, and inflammatory neuritis.

Key Imaging Discriminators

Schwannoma follows a cranial nerve and often expands the IAC or neural foramen. Meningioma has broad dural base/dural tail; epidermoid restricts diffusion; arachnoid cyst follows CSF and does not enhance; metastasis often has rapid symptoms or other metastases.

Differential Diagnosis Matrix

See differential-matrix.md for MR-RATE-aware contrasts to meningioma, brain metastasis, glioma, pituitary adenoma, arachnoid cyst, epidermoid-like cystic lesions, cerebellar/brainstem mass effect, and hydrocephalus.

Similar-Presentation Diseases And Mimic-Aware Comparison

A generic CPA mass should not be counted as schwannoma unless report language favors schwannoma or the lesion clearly follows a cranial nerve distribution.

Report Language That Supports Or Argues Against Each Diagnosis

Supports schwannoma: vestibular schwannoma, acoustic neuroma, enhancing IAC/CPA mass, fundal extension, IAC widening, nerve sheath tumor. Argues against: meningioma, dural tail, epidermoid, arachnoid cyst, metastasis, paraganglioma.

When Additional Imaging Or Clinical Context Helps

Thin-section IAC MRI, CT temporal bone, prior MRI, audiometry, vestibular testing, syndrome history, cancer history, and treatment date help adjudicate uncertain lesions.

Natural History And Clinical Course

Most vestibular schwannomas are benign and slow-growing, but growth rates vary. Hearing loss and vestibular symptoms may progress. Larger tumors can cause brainstem compression or hydrocephalus.

Treatment, Response, And Outcome Context

Management includes observation, microsurgery, radiosurgery/fractionated radiotherapy, hearing rehabilitation, and syndrome-specific care.

Guideline-Based Management Context

EANO, CNS, ISRS, and imaging review sources frame MRI diagnosis, observation, radiosurgery, surgery, and follow-up. Treatment choice depends on tumor size, symptoms, hearing, growth, age, NF2 status, and center expertise.

Common Treatment Pathways

Observation with serial MRI; microsurgical resection; stereotactic radiosurgery; fractionated stereotactic radiotherapy; subtotal resection plus radiation; hearing aids, cochlear implant, or auditory brainstem implant in selected contexts.

Imaging Appearance After Treatment

After surgery, residual enhancement can remain in the IAC/CPA. After radiosurgery, transient enlargement and central loss of enhancement may occur. Hydrocephalus, edema, cystic change, and cranial nerve complications should be noted.

Evidence Of Treatment Response

Response is stable or decreased tumor volume, stable or improved brainstem mass effect, no hydrocephalus, no sustained growth, and stable symptoms/hearing when clinically available.

Evidence Of Progression, Recurrence, Or Treatment Failure

Progression is sustained growth, increasing volume, worsening Koos grade, recurrent nodular enhancement after surgery, increasing brainstem compression, hydrocephalus, or treatment-related salvage need.

Expected Outcomes And Prognostic Factors

Key factors include tumor size/volume, Koos grade, growth rate, hearing status, age, NF2/schwannomatosis, treatment modality, resection extent, radiation dose, and follow-up duration.

Statistical Implications Of Treatment And Progression

Observation, surgery, and radiosurgery are confounded by tumor size, symptoms, hearing, age, NF2 status, and center preference. Pseudoprogression can misclassify treatment failure.

Evidence Of Active Disease, Progression, Or Recurrence

Active disease is interval growth, increasing CPA component, new/worsening brainstem compression, hydrocephalus, or recurrent residual enhancing tumor after treatment.

Stable Or Chronic Residual Findings

Stable size/volume, stable enhancement, unchanged IAC/CPA extension, and no new mass effect support stability. Stable post-SRS central hypoenhancement can be expected.

Improvement, Treatment Response, Or Resolution

Improvement includes decreased volume, reduced mass effect, stable hearing/symptoms, or no recurrent/residual growth. Complete disappearance is not required.

Serial Imaging Assessment And Interval Change

Compare exact sequence, slice thickness, IAC protocol, maximum dimensions, volume when available, IAC/CPA components, brainstem contact, enhancement, cystic change, and treatment dates.

Clinical Endpoints

Hearing preservation, tinnitus, vertigo, balance, facial nerve function, trigeminal symptoms, hydrocephalus, surgery, radiosurgery, salvage treatment, quality of life, and functional status.

Imaging, Biomarker, And Measurement Endpoints

Maximum diameter, intracanalicular/CPA extent, volume, Koos/Hannover grade, growth rate, brainstem compression, hydrocephalus, central hypoenhancement, cystic change, residual/recurrent enhancement.

Common Covariates And Confounders

Important covariates include age, hearing status, tumor size, Koos grade, NF2/schwannomatosis, treatment history, MRI protocol, and follow-up duration.

Clinical Covariates

Age, symptoms, audiometry, vestibular symptoms, facial/trigeminal symptoms, NF2/schwannomatosis, prior treatment, and performance status.

Imaging Covariates

IAC/CPA extent, tumor volume, Koos/Hannover grade, brainstem contact, hydrocephalus, cystic change, enhancement pattern, prior imaging interval, and measurement method.

Treatment And Temporal Confounders

Observation versus surgery versus radiosurgery, resection extent, radiation date, transient enlargement window, salvage therapy, and hearing rehabilitation.

Acquisition And Protocol Confounders

Thin-section IAC protocol, postcontrast timing, 3D T2 technique, field strength, motion, artifact, cochlear implant artifact, and slice thickness affect detection and measurement.

Research Design Implications

Stratify intracanalicular, CPA-extension, large/brainstem-compressive, NF2-related, treated, and recurrent lesions. Adjudicate generic CPA mass labels.

Statistical Implications

Schwannoma cohorts need clear distinction between vestibular schwannoma, nonvestibular cranial nerve schwannoma, syndrome-associated schwannoma, treated residual tumor, and CPA/IAC mimics. Time-to-growth and treatment comparisons must handle surveillance bias and pseudoprogression.

Missing Information To Ask For

Ask for nerve/location, IAC/CPA extent, size or volume, Koos grade, hearing/audiometry, symptoms, NF2/schwannomatosis, prior MRI, surgery/radiation date, and whether schwannoma is favored or differential-only.
