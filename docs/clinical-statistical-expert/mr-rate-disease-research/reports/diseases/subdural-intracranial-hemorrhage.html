Scope

This chapter covers intracranial subdural hemorrhage as an imaging finding, report label, and research phenotype. Because the MR-RATE original label is Extra-axial hematoma, the chapter emphasizes extra-axial subdural collections and explains when the report language is specific enough to distinguish SDH from other extra-axial collections.

The chapter includes acute traumatic SDH, subacute SDH, chronic SDH, acute-on-chronic SDH, bilateral chronic SDH, tentorial/interhemispheric SDH, pediatric or abusive-head-trauma contexts, anticoagulant-associated SDH, postoperative/recurrent SDH, and chronic SDH treated by surgery or middle meningeal artery embolization. It does not cover spinal SDH except as a contextual caveat, and it does not treat an extra-axial collection label as equivalent to SDH unless blood-product or report-language evidence supports it.

Clinical Context

Subdural hemorrhage is blood in the potential space between the dura and arachnoid. It is commonly related to tearing of bridging veins after trauma, but risk and presentation vary by age, brain atrophy, anticoagulant or antiplatelet exposure, coagulopathy, intracranial hypotension, shunting, postoperative state, and abusive head trauma in infants.

CT is usually the initial emergency imaging test because it is fast and sensitive for acute blood and mass effect. MRI is useful for subtle, isodense, bilateral, subacute, chronic, septated, posterior fossa, interhemispheric, pediatric, and complex or recurrent collections. MRI also helps characterize blood-product evolution, membranes, loculations, and coexisting parenchymal injury.

For research use, the key distinction is whether the label means:

  • acute traumatic SDH with mass effect
  • subacute SDH in evolution
  • chronic SDH, possibly bilateral or loculated
  • acute-on-chronic SDH with new bleeding into an older collection
  • extra-axial hematoma not otherwise specified
  • subdural hygroma, empyema, postoperative collection, or another mimic
  • associated traumatic injury such as contusion, diffuse axonal injury, infarction, skull fracture, or subarachnoid hemorrhage

Known-Diagnosis Review Frame

When the diagnosis or report label is subdural intracranial hemorrhage, characterize:

  • compartment: convexity, falcine, tentorial, posterior fossa, interhemispheric, unilateral, or bilateral
  • acuity: acute, subacute, chronic, mixed-age, or acute-on-chronic
  • size: maximal thickness, craniocaudal extent, volume if measured, and laterality
  • mass effect: sulcal effacement, ventricular compression, midline shift, cisternal effacement, uncal/subfalcine/herniation pattern
  • internal architecture: homogeneous, mixed density/signal, layering, membranes, septations, loculations, calcification, or organizing capsule
  • associated injury: contusion, SAH, skull fracture, diffuse axonal injury, infarction, edema, hydrocephalus, or postoperative change
  • treatment state: observation, burr-hole evacuation, craniotomy, craniectomy, drain, recurrence, or MMA embolization
  • mimics: epidural hematoma, hygroma, empyema, chronic CSF collection, intracranial hypotension, dural metastasis, and postoperative fluid

What To Look For

Acute Or Early Presentation

Acute SDH usually appears as a crescentic hyperdense extra-axial collection on noncontrast CT, spreading along the cerebral convexity and able to cross suture lines. It may track along the falx or tentorium. MRI signal depends on blood age and sequence, but acute blood products often produce susceptibility on GRE/SWI and may be low signal on T2. The clinically important imaging features are thickness, mass effect, midline shift, cisternal effacement, herniation, and associated traumatic injuries.

Report-like language:

  • "Acute left holohemispheric subdural hematoma measuring up to 9 mm in thickness with sulcal effacement and 4 mm rightward midline shift."
  • "Hyperdense extra-axial blood products along the right cerebral convexity, falx, and tentorium, compatible with acute subdural hemorrhage."
  • "Associated hemorrhagic contusions and traumatic subarachnoid hemorrhage are present."

Subacute Or Evolving Presentation

Subacute SDH can become isodense to cortex on CT and may be detected by secondary signs such as cortical buckling, displaced gray-white interface, effaced sulci, ventricular compression, or midline shift. MRI may show methemoglobin-related T1 hyperintensity and variable T2/FLAIR signal. Mixed density or mixed signal can indicate evolving blood products or repeated bleeding.

Report-like language:

  • "Isoattenuating right frontoparietal subdural collection with effacement of adjacent sulci and mild leftward midline shift, suspicious for subacute SDH."
  • "Subacute subdural hematoma demonstrates T1 hyperintense blood products and FLAIR hyperintense extra-axial collection."
  • "Mixed-signal subdural blood products suggest blood products of different ages."

Chronic Or Stable Residual Presentation

Chronic SDH often appears hypodense on CT relative to brain and may be bilateral. MRI can show CSF-like or variable signal depending on protein, hemolysis, repeated hemorrhage, septations, and membranes. Chronic collections may produce slow mass effect, gait or cognitive symptoms, headache, or focal deficit. Absence of interval enlargement and decreasing mass effect support stability or improvement.

Report-like language:

  • "Chronic left convexity subdural hematoma with mild mass effect but no significant midline shift."
  • "Bilateral chronic subdural collections are present; lack of midline shift likely reflects balanced bilateral mass effect."
  • "Thin residual chronic subdural collection without new hyperdense blood products."

Progressive, Recurrent, Or Worsening Presentation

Progression is suggested by interval enlargement, new hyperdense or T1 hyperintense blood within a chronic collection, increasing mass effect, increasing midline shift, new herniation, new neurologic symptoms, recurrent collection after evacuation, or increasing membrane/septation complexity. Acute-on-chronic SDH often shows mixed density on CT or layered/mixed signal on MRI.

Report-like language:

  • "Interval enlargement of the chronic right subdural hematoma with new hyperdense blood products, compatible with acute-on-chronic hemorrhage."
  • "Increased midline shift and sulcal effacement compared with prior study."
  • "Recurrent loculated chronic subdural collection with internal septations."

Improving, Resolving, Or Treatment-Response Presentation

Improvement means reduced thickness, reduced mass effect, decreasing midline shift, expected postoperative air/fluid evolution, and no new acute blood products. After burr-hole evacuation or craniotomy, residual extra-axial fluid, air, dural enhancement, and small residual blood products may be expected. After MMA embolization, imaging follow-up may show gradual reduction in chronic collection size rather than immediate evacuation.

Report-like language:

  • "Decreased size of the left chronic subdural hematoma with improved mass effect and resolved midline shift."
  • "Expected postoperative changes of burr-hole evacuation with small residual mixed-density subdural collection."
  • "No new acute hemorrhage."

Context Clues

  • Older age, cerebral atrophy, falls, anticoagulants, antiplatelets, alcohol-use history, coagulopathy, shunt, and intracranial hypotension change pretest probability and recurrence risk.
  • Infants require careful abusive-head-trauma context when SDH is unexplained, multifocal, interhemispheric, or associated with retinal hemorrhage or skeletal injury.
  • Trauma patients can have multiple hemorrhage compartments; do not stop after finding one lesion.
  • A purely CSF-density collection without blood products may be hygroma rather than hematoma.
  • Diffusion restriction, rim enhancement, sinusitis, mastoiditis, fever, or leukocytosis should raise concern for subdural empyema.

Report Language Patterns

Findings-style language should describe:

  • anatomy, laterality, and distribution: "left cerebral convexity," "right parafalcine," "tentorial," "bilateral frontoparietal"
  • sequence-specific appearance: "hyperdense on CT," "T1 hyperintense," "FLAIR hyperintense," "susceptibility along the collection"
  • morphology and mass effect: "crescentic," "layering," "mixed density," "septated," "sulcal effacement," "midline shift," "ventricular compression"
  • comparison: "increased from 6 mm to 11 mm," "new hyperdense component," "decreased mass effect"
  • relevant negatives: "no herniation," "no new acute blood products," "no diffusion restriction to suggest empyema"

Impression-style language should synthesize:

  • "Acute left convexity subdural hematoma with mild rightward midline shift."
  • "Chronic bilateral subdural hematomas, right greater than left, without herniation."
  • "Acute-on-chronic right subdural hematoma with increased mass effect."
  • "Extra-axial collection is favored to represent subdural hygroma rather than hematoma in the absence of blood-product signal."

Uncertainty and cohort-label language:

  • Confident SDH: "acute subdural hematoma," "subdural blood products," "acute-on-chronic SDH," "chronic subdural hematoma."
  • Probable SDH: "extra-axial collection with blood-product signal," "favored subdural hematoma."
  • Low-confidence/nonspecific: "extra-axial collection," "subdural fluid," "hygroma versus chronic SDH."
  • Adjudication trigger: "empyema not excluded," "dural lesion not excluded," "isodense collection with mass effect," "postoperative extra-axial collection."

Primary Imaging Modality

The usual first-line modality for suspected acute SDH is noncontrast head CT because it is rapid, widely available, and sensitive for acute blood and mass effect. MRI is important when CT is equivocal, when chronicity or internal architecture matters, when posterior fossa/interhemispheric/subtle SDH is suspected, in pediatric or abusive-head-trauma evaluation, and when mimics such as empyema or neoplasm are possible.

  • T1: useful for subacute methemoglobin and chronic hematoma characterization.
  • T2: acute blood may be low signal; subacute/chronic collections vary.
  • FLAIR: often highlights subdural collections relative to CSF and cortex.
  • DWI/ADC: important for subdural empyema mimic and coexisting infarction.
  • SWI/GRE: detects blood products, susceptibility, and traumatic microbleeds.
  • Post-contrast T1: evaluates membranes, empyema, dural disease, postoperative change, or neoplastic mimics.
  • Perfusion, spectroscopy, tractography, volumetry, or other advanced methods: not routine for SDH diagnosis, but quantitative thickness/volume and membrane imaging may matter for chronic SDH research and recurrence prediction.

Other Modalities And When They Matter

CT is primary in emergency trauma and acute neurologic decline. CTA/CTV may be needed when vascular injury, venous sinus thrombosis, aneurysm, or unexplained hemorrhage is suspected. Catheter angiography is not routine for SDH diagnosis but is relevant to MMA embolization planning. Spine MRI may matter when intracranial hypotension or concurrent spinal subdural blood is suspected. Laboratory studies, medication history, and coagulation status are essential for interpreting risk and treatment context.

Locations And Structural Appearance

  • cortical: convexity SDH compresses sulci and cortex from outside the brain.
  • subcortical: parenchyma may be compressed but the collection remains extra-axial.
  • deep white matter: abnormalities here usually represent associated injury or mimic rather than SDH itself.
  • periventricular: ventricular compression or hydrocephalus may occur from mass effect or associated injury.
  • corpus callosum: abnormality suggests shear injury or infarction, not SDH.
  • deep gray nuclei: hemorrhage here suggests cerebral hemorrhage rather than SDH.
  • brainstem: SDH may cause secondary compression/herniation; intrinsic brainstem injury is a separate associated finding.
  • cerebellum: posterior fossa or tentorial SDH may be subtle and high stakes.
  • hippocampus or mesial temporal structures: may be compressed in uncal herniation; not primary SDH tissue.
  • postoperative, post-treatment, or post-injury regions: extra-axial air/fluid, dural enhancement, recurrent collection, or mixed blood products require comparison with surgical timing.
  • vascular-territory, tract-like, multifocal, diffuse, symmetric, asymmetric, mass-like, cavitary, atrophic, or volume-loss patterns: SDH is extra-axial and crescentic rather than vascular-territory parenchymal signal; bilateral chronic SDH can mask midline shift.

Typical Appearance

Typical SDH is a crescentic extra-axial collection along a cerebral convexity, falcine surface, or tentorium. Acute SDH is often hyperdense on CT. Subacute SDH may become isodense and require attention to mass effect. Chronic SDH is often hypodense or mixed density on CT and variable on MRI, especially when there are membranes, septations, repeated bleeding, or proteinaceous fluid.

Atypical Or Red-Flag Appearance

Red flags include rapid enlargement, marked mass effect, herniation, bilateral collections without obvious shift, diffusion restriction or rim enhancement suggesting empyema, nodular or mass-like dural enhancement suggesting neoplasm, posterior fossa/tentorial location, unexplained pediatric SDH, recurrent SDH after evacuation, or extra-axial collection in the setting of intracranial hypotension or shunt overdrainage.

Differential Diagnosis And Mimics

Use this section for questions such as "what else could this be?", "what imaging features support SDH?", and "what would make a similar-appearing diagnosis more likely?"

Quick Differential Diagnosis Guide

Most common or high-priority imaging-context differentials:

  1. Epidural hematoma: biconvex/lentiform extra-axial hematoma, often arterial and limited by sutures.
  2. Subdural hygroma or chronic CSF collection: CSF-like extra-axial fluid without blood-product signal.
  3. Subdural empyema: infected subdural collection with enhancement and diffusion restriction.
  4. Traumatic subarachnoid hemorrhage: blood in sulci/cisterns rather than a crescentic extra-axial collection.
  5. Cerebral hemorrhage or contusion: intra-axial hemorrhage with parenchymal edema/contusion pattern.
  6. Dural metastasis or pachymeningeal disease: enhancing dural mass or diffuse pachymeningeal thickening, sometimes with secondary subdural fluid.
  7. Intracranial hypotension: pachymeningeal enhancement, brain sag, venous engorgement, and subdural collections.

Key Imaging Discriminators

  • SDH crosses suture lines; epidural hematoma is commonly limited by sutures.
  • SDH molds to the brain surface as a crescent; epidural hematoma is typically lentiform.
  • Blood products, layering, membranes, or susceptibility support hematoma over hygroma.
  • Diffusion restriction and infection context support empyema.
  • Intra-axial edema-centered hemorrhage supports cerebral hemorrhage or contusion rather than SDH.
  • Mixed-density chronic SDH may indicate repeated bleeding or acute-on-chronic change.

Similar-Presentation Diseases And Mimic-Aware Comparison

Subdural hemorrhage overlaps clinically with stroke, seizure, dementia, headache syndromes, trauma, and postoperative change. For MR-RATE labeling, the most important confusion is between a generic "extra-axial hematoma" label and a specific SDH label. Reports that merely say "extra-axial collection" need review for blood-product language before inclusion as SDH.

Report Language That Supports Or Argues Against Each Diagnosis

  • Supports SDH: "subdural hematoma," "subdural blood products," "crescentic extra-axial hemorrhage," "acute-on-chronic subdural collection."
  • Supports epidural hematoma: "lentiform," "biconvex," "epidural," "does not cross sutures."
  • Supports hygroma: "CSF-density," "CSF-intensity," "hygroma," "no blood products."
  • Supports empyema: "restricted diffusion," "rim enhancement," "sinusitis," "infected collection."
  • Supports parenchymal hemorrhage: "intraparenchymal," "intra-axial," "basal ganglia," "lobar hematoma."

When Additional Imaging Or Clinical Context Helps

Additional imaging or context helps when the collection is isodense, bilateral, postoperative, suspected infected, pediatric, recurrent, or associated with unexplained pachymeningeal enhancement. MRI with contrast and DWI helps separate chronic SDH, hygroma, empyema, and dural disease. Prior imaging is critical for acuity, growth, recurrence, and treatment response.

Natural History And Clinical Course

Acute SDH may present immediately after trauma and can progress rapidly when bleeding and mass effect increase. Subacute SDH evolves over days to weeks as blood products change signal and density. Chronic SDH evolves over weeks or months, especially in older adults or people with atrophy, coagulopathy, or antithrombotic exposure. Chronic SDH can remain stable, resolve, enlarge, or rebleed, producing acute-on-chronic imaging.

Treatment, Response, And Outcome Context

Guideline-Based Management Context

Guideline and reference sources emphasize that acute SDH decisions depend on hematoma thickness, midline shift, neurologic status, coma, pupillary findings, ICP, and associated injury. Chronic SDH decisions depend on symptoms, size, mass effect, recurrence, operative risk, and emerging MMA embolization evidence.

Common Treatment Pathways

  • observation with serial imaging for small stable collections
  • reversal or management of anticoagulation/coagulopathy when clinically relevant
  • burr-hole evacuation and drain for many symptomatic chronic SDH cases
  • craniotomy or craniectomy for selected acute, organized, recurrent, or complex cases
  • middle meningeal artery embolization for selected chronic SDH contexts
  • rehabilitation and follow-up for neurologic deficits or traumatic brain injury sequelae

Imaging Appearance After Treatment

Posttreatment imaging may show decreased hematoma thickness, residual extra-axial fluid, pneumocephalus, postoperative dural enhancement, residual blood products, membranes, or recurrent collection. After embolization, the collection may resolve gradually rather than immediately.

Evidence Of Treatment Response

Response is reflected by decreasing hematoma thickness or volume, reduced mass effect, improved midline shift, absence of new acute blood products, clinical improvement, and lack of recurrence on follow-up imaging.

Evidence Of Progression, Recurrence, Or Treatment Failure

Progression or recurrence is suggested by increasing thickness or volume, new hyperdense or T1-bright blood products, new or worsening mixed density, increasing septations/loculations, worsening mass effect, recurrent symptoms, or need for repeat evacuation or embolization.

Expected Outcomes And Prognostic Factors

Important prognostic factors include age, baseline neurologic status, GCS, pupillary findings, hematoma thickness, midline shift, associated traumatic brain injury, anticoagulant exposure, coagulopathy, bilateral disease, recurrence, treatment type, and follow-up duration.

Statistical Implications Of Treatment And Progression

Treatment introduces confounding by indication: larger, symptomatic, or higher-risk collections are more likely to receive surgery or embolization. Time from injury, time from imaging, treatment timing, recurrence definitions, surveillance intensity, death, and loss to follow-up can all bias progression and response estimates.

Evidence Of Active Disease, Progression, Or Recurrence

Active or worsening SDH is suggested by new acute blood products, interval enlargement, increasing mass effect, new midline shift, herniation, new neurologic symptoms, or recurrence after treatment. Mixed-density chronic SDH often requires careful comparison because it may reflect blood products of different ages.

Stable Or Chronic Residual Findings

Stable chronic SDH is supported by unchanged or decreasing thickness, no new hyperdense/T1-bright blood products, stable or improved mass effect, and expected chronic signal characteristics. Stability requires comparison with prior imaging and knowledge of treatment timing.

Improvement, Treatment Response, Or Resolution

Improvement is described as reduced hematoma thickness, decreased mass effect, less midline shift, clinical improvement, and absence of new bleeding. Residual membranes, susceptibility, or small chronic collections may persist after clinical improvement.

Serial Imaging Assessment And Interval Change

Compare maximal thickness, laterality, extent, density/signal, internal architecture, membranes, new acute blood, midline shift, cisternal effacement, ventricular compression, and associated injuries. Use consistent planes and measurement methods. Bilateral collections can hide mass effect because shift may be absent.

Clinical Endpoints

Common endpoints include neurologic deterioration, GCS change, focal deficit, headache, seizure, falls, cognitive or gait symptoms, need for surgery, reoperation, recurrence, ICU length of stay, discharge disposition, mortality, modified Rankin Scale, Glasgow Outcome Scale, and functional recovery.

Imaging, Biomarker, And Measurement Endpoints

Relevant imaging endpoints include maximal hematoma thickness, volume, midline shift, degree of sulcal/cisternal effacement, acute blood component, membrane/septation pattern, bilateral disease, recurrence, postoperative residual volume, time to radiographic resolution, and associated injury burden.

Common Covariates And Confounders

Clinical Covariates

  • age, frailty, fall risk, alcohol-use history, dementia, baseline function
  • trauma mechanism and time from injury
  • GCS, pupillary status, focal deficits, seizure, headache, cognitive change
  • anticoagulant/antiplatelet exposure, coagulopathy, thrombocytopenia
  • shunt, intracranial hypotension, prior cranial surgery, malignancy, infection

Imaging Covariates

  • CT versus MRI and available sequences
  • acute/subacute/chronic/acute-on-chronic status
  • unilateral versus bilateral, convexity/falcine/tentorial/posterior fossa
  • thickness, volume, midline shift, membranes, septations, mixed density
  • associated contusion, SAH, infarction, fracture, edema, DAI, or hydrocephalus

Treatment And Temporal Confounders

  • observation versus surgery versus embolization
  • drain use, burr hole, craniotomy, craniectomy, MMA embolization
  • timing of treatment relative to first imaging
  • antithrombotic reversal and restart timing
  • surveillance schedule and recurrence definition

Acquisition And Protocol Confounders

  • CT slice thickness and subdural windows
  • availability of coronal/sagittal reformats
  • MRI field strength and T1/T2/FLAIR/DWI/SWI/contrast availability
  • motion artifact, postoperative artifact, and scanner/site changes
  • inconsistent measurement plane or windowing

Research Design Implications

  • adjust for age, baseline severity, anticoagulant exposure, hematoma size, shift, acuity, and treatment selection
  • stratify acute, chronic, and acute-on-chronic SDH
  • adjudicate extra-axial collection labels that lack blood-product language
  • perform sensitivity analyses excluding hygroma/empyema/postoperative-only collections
  • define recurrence and progression using imaging and clinical criteria

Statistical Implications

Subdural hemorrhage labels can be noisy because reports may use "extra-axial hematoma," "subdural collection," "hygroma," or "acute-on-chronic" language inconsistently. Cohorts should distinguish acute SDH, chronic SDH, acute-on-chronic SDH, postoperative/recurrent SDH, and nonspecific extra-axial collections. Endpoints should separate radiographic progression, clinical deterioration, surgery, recurrence, mortality, and functional outcome. Claims should acknowledge confounding by indication and surveillance bias.

Missing Information To Ask For

  • Was the finding on CT, MRI, or both?
  • What is the acuity: acute, subacute, chronic, or acute-on-chronic?
  • What are the thickness, laterality, distribution, and midline shift?
  • Are there associated traumatic injuries, infarct, edema, or skull fracture?
  • Is there anticoagulant/antiplatelet use, coagulopathy, shunt, or intracranial hypotension?
  • Was there surgery, drain placement, embolization, or recurrence?
  • Is the report using "extra-axial collection" nonspecifically?
  • Are prior images available for interval comparison?
