# Watershed Infarct

## Goals

This disease chapter is designed to help a clinical-statistical reviewer:

1. Given a known or suspected watershed infarct context, describe how watershed
   or border-zone infarcts present on diagnostic imaging, especially MRI, using
   realistic radiology-report language.
2. Identify imaging features, anatomic distributions, chronicity clues, and
   clinical context that support or argue against watershed infarct relative to
   similar MR-RATE categories.
3. Translate watershed infarct appearance, report language, mechanism
   uncertainty, and timing into cohort definition, endpoint selection,
   covariates, adjudication, misclassification risk, and claims language.

## Scope

This chapter covers watershed infarct as a brain MRI finding, report label, and
research phenotype. It emphasizes supratentorial external/cortical border-zone
infarcts between major cortical arterial territories and internal/deep
border-zone infarcts between superficial medullary and deep perforator arterial
systems.

It includes mechanism context because watershed patterns are often interpreted
with carotid/intracranial stenosis, systemic hypotension, hypovolemia,
postoperative events, embolic burden, vascular imaging, and perfusion imaging.
It does not treat every multifocal infarct as watershed; multifocal embolic
infarcts, lacunar infarcts, demyelinating plaques, and chronic infarct sequelae
need separate adjudication.

## Clinical Context

Watershed infarcts occur in vulnerable border zones between arterial supply
territories. The imaging pattern can suggest hemodynamic compromise, embolic
contribution, or both. Internal watershed infarcts are especially linked to
hemodynamic impairment in many imaging discussions, while external cortical
border-zone infarcts may be embolic, hypoperfusion-related, or mixed depending
on vascular and clinical context.

For research use, the key distinction is whether the label means:

- acute diffusion-restricting watershed infarcts
- cortical border-zone infarcts between ACA/MCA or MCA/PCA territories
- internal/deep watershed infarcts in centrum semiovale or corona radiata
- chronic watershed infarct sequelae
- generic cerebral infarction with uncertain subtype
- multifocal embolic infarcts being described imprecisely as watershed
- hypoperfusion injury in a systemic or carotid/intracranial stenosis context

## Known-Diagnosis Review Frame

When the report or cohort label says watershed infarct, review the image and
context for four questions:

1. Is there an infarct signal pattern, especially restricted diffusion in acute
   cases, rather than only chronic gliosis or nonspecific white matter change?
2. Is the distribution genuinely border-zone: cortical ACA/MCA or MCA/PCA, or
   internal deep watershed parallel to the ventricles?
3. Is there vascular or systemic context that supports hypoperfusion,
   large-artery stenosis/occlusion, cardiac arrest, hypotension, dehydration,
   severe anemia, perioperative injury, or embolic contribution?
4. Does the report language distinguish acute watershed infarct from chronic
   watershed infarct sequelae and from broad cerebral infarction?

## What To Look For

### Acute Or Early Presentation

On MRI, acute watershed infarcts usually behave like acute ischemic infarcts:
DWI hyperintensity with low ADC in a border-zone distribution. FLAIR may be
normal very early or show evolving hyperintensity as edema develops. The key is
not just the diffusion abnormality, but the pattern.

Radiology-report-style language may include:

- "Small foci of restricted diffusion in the left ACA/MCA watershed territory."
- "Multiple acute infarcts in an internal border-zone distribution within the
  centrum semiovale/corona radiata."
- "Acute infarcts arranged linearly parallel to the lateral ventricle,
  compatible with deep watershed infarcts."
- "Bilateral watershed-pattern diffusion restriction, compatible with
  hypoperfusion injury in the appropriate clinical setting."

### Subacute Or Evolving Presentation

Subacute lesions may show persistent or pseudonormalizing diffusion behavior,
more conspicuous T2/FLAIR hyperintensity, mild swelling, cortical or subcortical
edema, and evolving enhancement depending on timing and protocol. Cortical
border-zone infarcts may have wedge-shaped or ovoid cortical/subcortical
involvement in the ACA/MCA or MCA/PCA boundary regions.

Report language may include:

- "Evolving subacute watershed infarcts with increasing FLAIR hyperintensity."
- "Cortical and subcortical signal abnormality in the MCA/PCA border-zone
  territory, compatible with subacute infarction."

### Chronic Or Stable Residual Presentation

Chronic watershed infarcts may leave encephalomalacia, gliosis, cortical volume
loss, or residual FLAIR hyperintensity in a border-zone distribution. Chronic
internal watershed lesions may be less specific if only small deep white matter
T2/FLAIR changes remain; prior imaging and the original DWI pattern become
important.

Report language may include:

- "Chronic encephalomalacia/gliosis in a left watershed distribution."
- "Remote border-zone infarcts involving the left frontal-parietal
  cortical/subcortical region."
- "Chronic deep watershed infarct sequelae in the centrum semiovale."

### Progressive, Recurrent, Or Worsening Presentation

Progression can appear as new DWI lesions in the same watershed territory,
expansion from partial to more confluent internal border-zone lesions, new
bilateral watershed involvement, or interval worsening in the setting of
persistent stenosis or recurrent hypoperfusion. Recurrent watershed-pattern
infarction should trigger review of vascular imaging, perfusion status, systemic
hemodynamics, and embolic sources.

Report language may include:

- "New acute border-zone infarcts superimposed on chronic watershed infarct
  sequelae."
- "Increased number of internal watershed infarcts compared with prior MRI."
- "New bilateral watershed infarcts, a pattern that can be seen with systemic
  hypoperfusion."

### Improving, Resolving, Or Treatment Response Presentation

Improvement is generally described as expected evolution of ischemic injury:
resolution of diffusion restriction, decreasing edema, and development of
chronic gliosis/encephalomalacia. The radiology report should separate imaging
evolution from treatment efficacy unless a clinical trial or treatment-response
framework defines that endpoint.

Report language may include:

- "Expected evolution of previously seen acute watershed infarcts without new
  restricted diffusion."
- "No new infarct; chronic watershed infarct sequelae are unchanged."

### Context Clues

Watershed interpretation is strengthened by:

- severe extracranial or intracranial stenosis/occlusion
- ipsilateral carotid disease for unilateral border-zone infarcts
- systemic hypotension, hypovolemia, anemia, dehydration, cardiac arrest, or
  perioperative low-flow event
- perfusion deficit or impaired cerebrovascular reserve
- bilateral border-zone pattern after global hypoperfusion
- mixed embolic and hypoperfusion context when external cortical lesions are
  present

### Report Language Patterns

Findings-style language should describe signal, location, distribution, and
chronicity:

- "Restricted diffusion with low ADC in a linear internal border-zone
  distribution."
- "Cortical/subcortical DWI lesions at the ACA/MCA border zone."
- "Chronic FLAIR hyperintensity and volume loss in a watershed distribution."

Impression-style language should synthesize pattern and likely subtype:

- "Acute watershed infarcts in the left internal border-zone distribution."
- "Bilateral watershed infarcts, which may reflect hypoperfusion in the
  appropriate clinical context."
- "Remote border-zone infarct sequelae without acute infarct."

## Primary Imaging Modality

MRI is the primary modality for characterizing watershed infarct patterns in
this chapter. DWI/ADC is central for acute infarct detection and timing. FLAIR
and T2 show edema and chronic gliotic change. T1 can show chronic volume loss or
encephalomalacia. SWI/GRE may identify hemorrhagic transformation or coexisting
microbleeds but does not by itself define watershed infarct.

MRA/CTA and carotid imaging provide mechanism context by identifying carotid,
intracranial, or large-vessel stenosis/occlusion. Perfusion MRI, CT perfusion,
SPECT, PET, transcranial Doppler, or cerebrovascular reserve testing may be
relevant when the question is hemodynamic compromise rather than simple infarct
presence.

## Other Modalities And When They Matter

CT can show established infarction and may be first-line in acute stroke
pathways, but small or early watershed infarcts can be occult. CTA, MRA, duplex
ultrasound, and angiography can identify stenosis, occlusion, dissection, or
collateral patterns. Perfusion imaging can support hypoperfusion physiology.

## Locations And Structural Appearance

### External Or Cortical Border Zones

External watershed infarcts involve cortical or cortico-subcortical regions at
junctions between ACA/MCA and MCA/PCA territories. They may be wedge-shaped,
ovoid, triangular, or gyriform depending on timing and anatomy. Anterior
border-zone lesions often involve frontal regions near the ACA/MCA boundary;
posterior border-zone lesions often involve parietal-occipital regions near the
MCA/PCA boundary.

### Internal Or Deep Border Zones

Internal watershed infarcts involve deep white matter between superficial
medullary arteries and deep perforator territories. They commonly appear as
multiple small lesions in the centrum semiovale or corona radiata, often aligned
parallel to the lateral ventricles. A confluent band-like or "string of pearls"
pattern on DWI/FLAIR supports internal border-zone infarction more than a single
random deep lacune.

### Bilateral Or Global Hypoperfusion Patterns

Bilateral watershed infarcts may occur with systemic hypoperfusion, cardiac
arrest, severe hypotension, severe anemia, or perioperative low-flow states. The
distribution, symmetry, and clinical timing matter for interpretation.

## Typical Appearance

Typical watershed infarct on MRI is an acute ischemic lesion pattern in a
recognized border-zone distribution:

- DWI hyperintensity with low ADC when acute
- evolving T2/FLAIR hyperintensity and edema when subacute
- chronic gliosis, encephalomalacia, or volume loss when remote
- cortical/subcortical wedge-like or ovoid lesions for external border-zone
  infarcts
- linear, rosary-like, or band-like deep white matter lesions parallel to the
  ventricles for internal border-zone infarcts

## Atypical Or Red-Flag Appearance

Features that should trigger caution or adjudication:

- lesions scattered across multiple arterial territories without a border-zone
  distribution, favoring embolic shower
- a single small basal ganglia, thalamic, internal capsule, or pontine lesion,
  favoring lacunar infarct
- ovoid periventricular/callosal/juxtacortical lesions with dissemination,
  favoring demyelinating disease
- chronic white matter hyperintensity without restricted diffusion, cavity,
  encephalomalacia, or prior watershed-pattern infarct
- mass-like enhancement, abscess-like diffusion restriction, vasculitis pattern,
  venous infarct pattern, or posterior reversible encephalopathy pattern

## Differential Diagnosis And Mimics

### Quick Guide

- **Cerebral infarction:** watershed infarct is a subtype; keep the broad label
  and subtype label separate when the distribution is clear.
- **Lacunar infarct:** usually a single small perforator-territory infarct;
  internal watershed infarcts are often multiple, linear, or band-like.
- **Multiple embolic infarcts:** can be multifocal, but are usually scattered
  across arterial territories rather than aligned in border zones.
- **Demyelinating disease:** may create white matter lesions and deficits, but
  the distribution and diffusion/vascular pattern should differ.
- **Gliosis/encephalomalacia:** may be chronic sequelae of watershed infarcts,
  but should not be counted as acute watershed infarct without timing evidence.

### Key Imaging Discriminators

| Comparator | Features Supporting Watershed Infarct | Features Supporting Comparator |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Cerebral infarction | Border-zone distribution; ACA/MCA, MCA/PCA, or internal watershed pattern; stenosis/hypoperfusion context. | Territorial cortical infarct, deep perforator infarct, or nonspecified cerebral infarct without watershed pattern. |
| Lacunar infarct | Multiple deep lesions aligned parallel to ventricles; internal border-zone distribution; hypoperfusion/stenosis context. | Single small lesion in basal ganglia, thalamus, internal capsule, or pons in perforator territory. |
| Embolic infarcts | Border-zone clustering with low-flow context. | Random multifocal cortical/subcortical lesions across multiple territories or bilateral embolic shower. |
| Demyelinating disease | Acute vascular DWI/ADC pattern and arterial border-zone distribution. | Ovoid periventricular, juxtacortical, infratentorial, callosal, or spinal lesions with dissemination. |
| Gliosis/encephalomalacia | Chronic sequela exactly in prior watershed distribution. | Nonspecific chronic FLAIR change or cystic volume loss without a documented watershed infarct pattern. |
| Cerebral edema | Edema centered on acute border-zone infarcts. | Diffuse edema or edema from tumor, infection, inflammation, venous congestion, trauma, or PRES. |

### Similar-Presentation Diseases From The MR-RATE Set

High-priority MR-RATE contrasts:

1. Cerebral infarction: parent category and frequent reporting fallback.
2. Lacunar infarct: main subtype mimic for internal watershed lesions.
3. Gliosis and encephalomalacia: chronic sequelae and chronic mimic labels.
4. Demyelinating disease of central nervous system: white matter mimic.
5. Cerebral edema: associated finding that can be mistaken for primary label.
6. Silent micro-hemorrhage of brain: coexisting small-vessel marker, not an
   infarct subtype.

### Differential Diagnosis Report-Language Cues

- "Watershed distribution" or "border-zone distribution" supports the target.
- "String of pearls" or "linear band parallel to the lateral ventricle" supports
  internal watershed infarct.
- "Lacunar infarct" should be checked for location and multiplicity before
  relabeling as watershed.
- "Multiple embolic infarcts" should be checked for random multiterritory
  scattering rather than border-zone clustering.
- "Chronic gliosis" or "encephalomalacia" should be mapped as chronic sequelae
  unless the endpoint is remote watershed infarct.

### Information Needed To Resolve Uncertainty

- DWI/ADC timing and whether lesions are acute, subacute, or chronic
- exact lesion distribution relative to vascular territories
- vascular imaging results: carotid stenosis, intracranial stenosis, occlusion,
  dissection, collateral status
- systemic low-flow context: hypotension, hypovolemia, anemia, cardiac arrest,
  surgery, dehydration, severe stenosis
- embolic source evaluation and multiterritory pattern
- prior imaging for interval change

## Natural History And Clinical Course

Watershed infarcts are ischemic injuries whose course depends on infarct size,
location, mechanism, recurrence, and treatment of underlying vascular or
systemic contributors. Symptoms may be fluctuating or progressive in some
hypoperfusion contexts. Internal watershed infarcts can indicate severe
hemodynamic compromise and may carry recurrence or deterioration implications
when the underlying stenosis or low-flow state persists.

## Evidence Of Active Disease, Progression, Or Recurrence

Imaging evidence of active or recurrent disease includes new restricted
diffusion in border zones, increased lesion burden in a watershed distribution,
new bilateral watershed lesions after systemic hypoperfusion, or recurrent
lesions ipsilateral to high-grade stenosis. Clinical-statistical endpoints
should distinguish new acute infarcts from stable chronic sequelae.

## Stable Or Chronic Residual Findings

Stable chronic findings include unchanged FLAIR hyperintensity, gliosis,
encephalomalacia, cortical volume loss, or chronic deep white matter injury in a
known watershed distribution. These are important for baseline phenotype and
covariates but should not be counted as acute stroke endpoints unless the study
endpoint is chronic infarct burden.

## Improvement, Treatment Response, Or Resolution

Imaging improvement usually means no new diffusion restriction, expected
resolution of diffusion abnormality, decreasing edema, or stable chronic
sequelae. Treatment response is usually inferred from clinical and vascular
management context, not from infarct disappearance. Revascularization, secondary
prevention, hemodynamic correction, and risk-factor management may change future
lesion risk, but the infarcted tissue evolves to chronic sequelae.

## Serial Imaging Assessment And Interval Change

Serial review should state:

- whether any new DWI/ADC lesions are present
- whether lesions are in the same watershed territory or new territories
- whether prior acute lesions have evolved to chronic gliosis or
  encephalomalacia
- whether vascular or perfusion findings have changed
- whether the pattern is compatible with recurrent hypoperfusion, embolism, or a
  different mimic

## Treatment, Response, And Outcome Context

### Guideline-Based Management Context

Management context follows acute ischemic stroke and cerebrovascular disease
pathways rather than a watershed-only algorithm. Imaging workup often includes
brain imaging to confirm infarction, vascular imaging to assess stenosis or
occlusion, and perfusion/hemodynamic assessment when low-flow physiology is a
central question.

### Common Treatment Pathways

Common pathways include acute stroke evaluation, reperfusion eligibility when
appropriate, antithrombotic and vascular risk management, treatment of carotid
or intracranial stenosis when indicated, correction of hypotension or systemic
low-flow triggers, and rehabilitation. The disease chapter should not infer a
specific treatment decision from imaging pattern alone.

### Imaging Appearance After Treatment

After treatment or hemodynamic correction, imaging may show expected infarct
evolution rather than reversal. New DWI lesions after intervention or ongoing
low-flow state should be recorded as recurrence/progression. Stable chronic
sequelae should be separated from new ischemia.

### Evidence Of Treatment Response

Imaging evidence relevant to response includes absence of new watershed infarcts,
improved perfusion or reserve when measured, and stable vascular findings after
intervention or medical management. Clinical outcome and recurrent infarct risk
require separate clinical/statistical endpoints.

### Evidence Of Progression, Recurrence, Or Treatment Failure

Concerning evidence includes new watershed DWI lesions, increasing deep
border-zone lesion burden, new bilateral low-flow pattern, worsening perfusion,
or persistent severe stenosis/occlusion associated with recurrent symptoms.

### Expected Outcomes And Prognostic Factors

Outcome depends on lesion burden, cortical versus internal pattern, bilateral
involvement, recurrence, vascular stenosis, hemodynamic reserve, embolic source,
age, vascular risk factors, and treatment timing. Internal border-zone patterns
may signal hemodynamic compromise and can be important for risk stratification.

### Statistical Implications Of Treatment And Progression

For studies, treatment and progression context affects:

- acute infarct endpoint definition
- recurrent infarct endpoint definition
- baseline chronic infarct burden
- subgrouping by carotid/intracranial stenosis or systemic hypoperfusion
- censoring or covariate handling for revascularization or acute treatment
- sensitivity analysis separating cortical watershed, internal watershed,
  embolic-mixed, and uncertain cases

## Candidate Endpoints

Potential endpoints include:

- presence of acute watershed infarct on DWI/ADC
- cortical versus internal watershed subtype
- lesion count or burden in border-zone regions
- bilateral versus unilateral watershed pattern
- recurrent acute watershed infarct on serial imaging
- chronic watershed infarct burden or sequelae
- association with stenosis, perfusion deficit, or systemic low-flow context

## Common Covariates And Confounders

Clinical covariates:

- age, vascular risk factors, atrial fibrillation, diabetes, hypertension,
  smoking, hyperlipidemia
- hypotension, cardiac arrest, hypovolemia, anemia, dehydration, perioperative
  context, sepsis, medication-related low blood pressure

Imaging covariates:

- carotid stenosis, intracranial stenosis, large-vessel occlusion, collateral
  status, perfusion deficit, prior infarct burden, white matter disease,
  microbleeds, atrophy

Acquisition/protocol covariates:

- availability and timing of DWI/ADC
- availability of vascular imaging and perfusion imaging
- time from symptom onset to imaging
- motion and slice thickness affecting small lesion detection

Research-design confounders:

- conflating watershed infarct with broad cerebral infarction
- conflating chronic gliosis/encephalomalacia with acute infarct
- mixing cortical watershed, internal watershed, embolic, and hypoperfusion
  mechanisms without subgrouping
- incomplete vascular imaging or missing hemodynamic context

## Statistical And Cohort Design Implications

Watershed infarct should usually be treated as a subtype of cerebral infarction,
not as an entirely separate parent category. Cohort definitions should preserve
both the broad ischemic infarct label and the watershed subtype when evidence
supports it.

Recommended cohort variables:

- acute versus chronic watershed infarct
- cortical/external versus internal/deep watershed subtype
- unilateral versus bilateral pattern
- DWI-confirmed versus report-only diagnosis
- vascular imaging support present/absent
- hypoperfusion/systemic low-flow context present/absent
- embolic source present/absent or mixed/uncertain
- chronic sequelae present/absent

Sensitivity analyses should test whether conclusions change when excluding
report-only cases, uncertain mimics, chronic-only sequelae, embolic-mixed cases,
or cases without vascular imaging.

## Missing Information To Ask For

Ask for:

- MRI sequences available, especially DWI/ADC and FLAIR
- timing relative to symptoms or low-flow event
- exact anatomic distribution of lesions
- CTA/MRA/carotid ultrasound/perfusion findings
- evidence of hypotension, hypovolemia, anemia, cardiac arrest, surgery, or
  severe stenosis
- whether lesions are new, chronic, or stable on prior imaging
- whether the study needs acute stroke endpoints, chronic infarct burden, or
  mechanism classification

## Quick Differential Diagnosis Guide

Use the lesion distribution first, then mechanism context.

- Favor watershed infarct when acute DWI lesions cluster in external cortical
  border zones, internal deep border zones, or a linear/string-of-pearls pattern
  parallel to the lateral ventricles.
- Favor lacunar infarct when a single small deep lesion sits in a perforator
  territory without a border-zone distribution.
- Favor embolic shower when lesions are scattered across multiple arterial
  territories without a classic internal or cortical watershed pattern.
- Favor chronic gliosis or encephalomalacia when the abnormality is stable,
  CSF-like, volume-losing, and lacks acute diffusion restriction.

## Similar-Presentation Diseases And Mimic-Aware Comparison

Watershed infarct overlaps with several MR-RATE categories:

- `cerebral-infarction.md`: parent ischemic infarct category; preserve the
  watershed subtype when distribution supports it.
- `lacunar-infarct.md`: internal watershed lesions may resemble multiple deep
  lacunes, but watershed lesions tend to be linear, rosary-like, or border-zone
  distributed.
- `gliosis.md` and `encephalomalacia.md`: chronic watershed sequelae may leave
  FLAIR gliosis and volume loss; acuity requires DWI/ADC or interval change.
- `demyelinating-disease-of-central-nervous-system.md`: white matter lesions
  can mimic infarcts, but vascular-territory distribution and acute diffusion
  restriction support infarction.
- `cerebral-edema.md`: edema may accompany acute infarction but should not be
  used as the infarct subtype label.

## Report Language That Supports Or Argues Against Each Diagnosis

Supportive watershed language includes:

- "border-zone infarcts"
- "watershed distribution"
- "ACA/MCA or MCA/PCA junction"
- "internal watershed infarcts"
- "linear band-like foci in the centrum semiovale"
- "string-of-pearls pattern"
- "hypoperfusion pattern"

Language that argues for another label includes "single punctate lacunar
infarct", "embolic shower", "multifocal infarcts in multiple vascular
territories", "demyelinating plaques", "chronic gliosis", or "remote
encephalomalacia" without acute border-zone diffusion restriction.

## When Additional Imaging Or Clinical Context Helps

Additional context is especially important when mechanism matters. Helpful
inputs include CTA/MRA/carotid ultrasound, perfusion imaging, collateral status,
large-vessel stenosis or occlusion, blood-pressure or systemic low-flow event,
cardiac arrest, anemia, hypovolemia, perioperative context, embolic source,
prior imaging, and timing relative to symptoms.

## Clinical Endpoints

Potential clinical endpoints include acute neurologic deficit, recurrent
ischemic stroke, stroke progression, functional outcome, cognitive outcome,
death, and need for vascular intervention. Keep endpoints separate from imaging
phenotypes unless the study explicitly links them.

## Imaging, Biomarker, And Measurement Endpoints

Potential imaging endpoints include:

- acute watershed infarct on DWI/ADC
- cortical versus internal watershed subtype
- unilateral versus bilateral watershed pattern
- lesion count, volume, or burden in border-zone territories
- perfusion deficit or diffusion-perfusion mismatch
- vascular stenosis or occlusion
- chronic watershed gliosis or encephalomalacia on follow-up

## Clinical Covariates

Important clinical covariates include age, hypertension, diabetes, smoking,
hyperlipidemia, atrial fibrillation, known carotid or intracranial stenosis,
cardiac arrest, hypotension, hypovolemia, anemia, surgery, sepsis, dehydration,
and timing from symptoms or low-flow event to imaging.

## Imaging Covariates

Important imaging covariates include DWI/ADC availability, FLAIR timing, CTA or
MRA availability, carotid imaging, CT or MR perfusion, collateral status, lesion
laterality, lesion burden, prior infarct burden, white matter disease, atrophy,
microbleeds, and previous imaging.

## Treatment And Temporal Confounders

Treatment and time can change appearance and interpretation. Record acute
reperfusion treatment, antithrombotic exposure, revascularization, blood
pressure management, systemic resuscitation, time from onset to imaging, and
whether the apparent lesion burden reflects baseline chronic disease or new
interval infarction.

## Acquisition And Protocol Confounders

Protocol confounders include missing DWI/ADC, limited FLAIR quality, lack of
perfusion imaging, incomplete vascular imaging, motion artifact, thick slices,
scanner field strength, slice orientation, and inconsistent follow-up imaging.

## Research Design Implications

For research, define watershed infarct as both a distributional imaging pattern
and a mechanism-hypothesis variable. Do not force every watershed-pattern lesion
into either hypoperfusion or embolic bins unless vascular, perfusion, and
clinical context support that adjudication.

## Statistical Implications

Model watershed infarct with hierarchical labels when possible: broad cerebral
infarction, watershed subtype, cortical/internal subtype, acute/chronic state,
and mechanism confidence. Use sensitivity analyses for cases lacking vascular
imaging, perfusion data, or clear clinical low-flow/embolic context.

## Safety And Claim Boundaries

Use this chapter for research planning, known-diagnosis imaging
characterization, report-language extraction, differential framing, and
clinical-statistical reasoning. Do not treat watershed-pattern imaging as a
patient-specific mechanism diagnosis without clinical and vascular context.
Treatment discussion should remain research-context language grounded in stroke
guidelines.
