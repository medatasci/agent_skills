# Lacunar Infarct

## Goals

This disease chapter is designed to help a clinical-statistical reviewer:

   infarcts present on diagnostic imaging, especially MRI, using realistic
   radiology-report language.

   uncertainty into cohort definition, endpoint selection, covariates,
   adjudication, misclassification risk, and claims language.

- Common report language: acute lacunar infarct, small acute infarct, recent

## Scope

This chapter covers lacunar infarct as a brain MRI finding, report label, and
research phenotype. It emphasizes acute and chronic small subcortical infarcts
in perforator territories, including basal ganglia, thalamus, internal capsule,
deep white matter, and pons.

It includes overlap with cerebral small vessel disease because many lacunar
infarcts occur with other small-vessel disease markers. It does not treat every
small deep infarct as a small-vessel lacunar infarct; embolic, branch
atheromatous, and border-zone mechanisms may produce similar small deep
lesions.

## Clinical Context

Lacunar infarcts are small infarcts in noncortical regions supplied by deep
penetrating arteries. The clinical concept is often tied to lacunar stroke
syndromes and small-vessel disease, but imaging labels can include symptomatic
acute lacunar infarcts, silent acute lesions, and chronic lacunes found
incidentally.

For research use, the key distinction is whether the label means:

- acute small subcortical infarct
- chronic lacune of presumed vascular origin
- generic small-vessel disease burden
- broad cerebral infarction with lacunar subtype
- uncertain small deep lesion that needs adjudication

## Known-Diagnosis Review Frame

When the diagnosis or label is lacunar infarct, characterize:

- acuity: acute/recent, subacute/evolving, chronic/remote, or uncertain
- location: basal ganglia, thalamus, internal capsule, corona radiata, centrum
  semiovale, pons, or other perforator territory
- morphology: small round, ovoid, or tubular lesion; cavity versus noncavity
- size: classically small, often less than about 15 mm, but acute lesions may
  appear larger because of edema
- diffusion: restricted diffusion for acute/recent infarct
- chronic cavity: CSF-like signal on all sequences, often with a FLAIR/T2
  hyperintense rim
- coexisting small-vessel disease markers: white matter hyperintensities,
  microbleeds, perivascular spaces, and brain atrophy
- competing mechanism: embolic source, branch atheromatous disease, large
  artery disease, or internal border-zone infarct pattern

## What To Look For

### Acute Or Early Presentation

Acute lacunar infarct usually appears as a small, focal deep lesion in a
perforator territory. On DWI it is bright, with low ADC when true restricted
diffusion is present. On T2/FLAIR it may be subtle early, then become a small
hyperintense focus. T1 may be mildly hypointense. CT can miss acute lacunar
infarcts, especially in the first 24 hours, because of small lesion size.

Report-like description:

- "Small focus of restricted diffusion in the left posterior limb of the
  internal capsule, compatible with an acute lacunar infarct."
- "Punctate acute infarct in the right thalamus."
- "Small acute infarct in the pons in a perforator distribution."

Findings that should not be overcalled:

- a susceptibility-only microbleed without DWI/ADC infarct signal
- a CSF-like perivascular space without surrounding gliotic rim or acute signal
- diffuse white matter hyperintensity without a focal infarct cavity or acute
  restricted diffusion

### Subacute Or Evolving Presentation

Subacute lesions may remain T2/FLAIR hyperintense and can show evolving
diffusion signal. Enhancement can occur in acute or early subacute infarcts and
should not automatically imply tumor or infection. Edema around a small
subcortical infarct may make the acute lesion look larger than the eventual
chronic lacune.

Report-like description:

- "Evolving subacute lacunar infarct in the right corona radiata."
- "Small subacute infarct in the left basal ganglia with expected FLAIR
  hyperintensity."

### Chronic Or Stable Residual Presentation

Chronic lacunes are small CSF-like cavities in deep gray or white matter or the
brainstem. They are typically low signal on T1, high signal on T2, suppressed or
dark centrally on FLAIR like CSF, and may have a thin hyperintense FLAIR rim
from surrounding gliosis. Chronic lesions should not show acute restricted
diffusion.

Report-like description:

- "Chronic lacunar infarct in the right thalamus."
- "Remote lacunar infarcts in the bilateral basal ganglia."
- "Small chronic lacune in the left centrum semiovale with surrounding gliotic
  FLAIR signal."

### Progressive, Recurrent, Or Worsening Presentation

Lacunar infarcts do not usually "progress" as a single lesion in the way a tumor
does. Worsening usually means a new small subcortical infarct, increasing burden
of lacunes, progression of associated small-vessel disease markers, or a
complication such as hemorrhagic transformation. New lesions in different
territories should trigger review for embolic, large-vessel, inflammatory,
genetic, or other nonclassic mechanisms.

### Improving, Resolving, Or Treatment-Response Presentation

Acute diffusion restriction and edema can resolve, while the chronic cavity,
gliotic rim, volume loss, or small-vessel disease burden may persist. Clinical
improvement does not mean the infarct disappears on imaging. In longitudinal
studies, the time point and sequence availability matter because acute lesions
can evolve into smaller chronic lacunes or less conspicuous residual findings.

### Context Clues

Helpful context includes:

- symptom timing and lacunar syndrome, if known
- hypertension, diabetes, smoking, chronic kidney disease, and other vascular
  risk factors
- prior lacunes, white matter hyperintensities, microbleeds, enlarged
  perivascular spaces, and brain atrophy
- vascular imaging when branch atheromatous disease, large artery disease, or
  embolic source is plausible
- prior imaging to distinguish chronic lacune from new infarct

### Report Language Patterns

Findings-style language:

- "Small focus of restricted diffusion in the left thalamus."
- "Chronic CSF-signal lacune in the right basal ganglia."
- "Tiny remote lacunar infarct in the pons."
- "Scattered chronic lacunar infarcts with background chronic small-vessel
  ischemic change."

Impression-style language:

- "Acute lacunar infarct in the left posterior limb of the internal capsule."
- "Chronic bilateral basal ganglia lacunar infarcts."
- "No acute infarct; chronic lacunar infarcts and small-vessel ischemic
  changes."
- "Small deep infarct pattern is favored; correlate with vascular risk factors
  and clinical syndrome."

Uncertainty and cohort-label language:

- Confident: "acute lacunar infarct", "chronic lacunar infarct", "remote
  lacune".
- Probable: "favored chronic lacunar infarct", "likely lacune".
- Low confidence: "tiny CSF focus", "prominent perivascular space versus old
  lacune", "nonspecific small-vessel change".
- Adjudication trigger: "deep watershed versus lacunar infarcts", "embolic
  small infarcts", "demyelinating lesion versus infarct".

## Primary Imaging Modality

MRI is the most useful modality for distinguishing acute and chronic lacunar
infarcts.

- T1: acute lesions can be mildly hypointense; chronic lacunes are CSF-like low
  signal.
- T2: acute/subacute lesions are hyperintense; chronic lacunes are CSF-like
  bright.
- FLAIR: acute/subacute lesions are hyperintense; chronic lacunes often have a
  CSF-like center with a thin hyperintense gliotic rim.
- DWI/ADC: DWI bright with low ADC supports acute/recent infarct; absence of
  restriction supports chronicity but depends on timing.
- SWI/GRE: helps detect microbleeds, hemorrhagic transformation, or cavernous
  malformation mimics.
- Post-contrast T1: enhancement can occur in acute/subacute infarcts but is not
  the defining feature.
- Advanced methods: high-resolution vessel imaging, perfusion, and quantitative
  small-vessel disease burden measures can support research questions, but are
  not always available in routine MR-RATE-style reports.

## Other Modalities And When They Matter

Noncontrast CT is commonly used early in stroke evaluation, mainly to exclude
hemorrhage and other emergent findings, but it is insensitive for small acute
lacunar infarcts. CTA/MRA, carotid imaging, echocardiography, or other vascular
workup may matter when the lesion pattern is atypical for small-vessel lacunar
infarct, when the patient is young, or when embolic/large-vessel mechanisms are
plausible.

## Locations And Structural Appearance

- Cortical: cortical involvement argues against classic lacunar infarct and
  should raise territorial embolic infarct, watershed infarct, tumor, seizure,
  or inflammatory mimic.
- Subcortical: common; look for small deep perforator-territory lesions.
- Deep white matter: corona radiata and centrum semiovale lesions may be
  lacunar, embolic, or internal border-zone depending on pattern.
- Periventricular: periventricular white matter hyperintensity alone is not a
  lacunar infarct; a focal cavity or acute DWI lesion is needed.
- Corpus callosum: callosal lesions are atypical for classic lacunar infarct and
  should raise demyelination, diffuse axonal injury, cytotoxic lesion, tumor, or
  other processes.
- Deep gray nuclei: basal ganglia and thalamus are classic lacunar locations.
- Brainstem: pons is a classic location.
- Cerebellum: small cerebellar infarcts occur but should be assessed by vascular
  territory and mechanism rather than automatically labeled lacunar.
- Hippocampus or mesial temporal structures: atypical; consider PCA infarct,
  seizure-related change, encephalitis, or transient global amnesia mimics.
- Structural pattern: small round, ovoid, or tubular lesion in a perforator
  territory favors lacunar infarct; linear border-zone "string of pearls",
  cortical wedge-shaped infarct, mass-like lesion, or susceptibility-only focus
  argues for a different label.

## Typical Appearance

Typical acute lacunar infarct is a small DWI-bright/ADC-low lesion in a
subcortical perforator territory. Typical chronic lacune is a small CSF-signal
cavity, often with a thin FLAIR/T2 hyperintense rim, in the deep gray matter,
deep white matter, or pons.

## Atypical Or Red-Flag Appearance

Red flags include:

- cortical involvement
- multiple lesions in several vascular territories
- linear internal border-zone distribution
- lesion larger than expected for a lacunar infarct
- marked mass effect or progressive enlargement
- nodular/ring enhancement not following expected infarct evolution
- susceptibility-only lesion without infarct morphology
- absence of small-vessel disease context in a young patient
- callosal, juxtacortical, or periventricular ovoid lesions suggesting
  demyelination

## Differential Diagnosis And Mimics

### Quick Differential Diagnosis Guide

- Cerebral infarction: broad parent category; use lacunar subtype only when
  location, size, and morphology support a small deep perforator infarct.
- Watershed infarct: important subtype mimic; internal border-zone infarcts can
  look like multiple small deep infarcts but tend to form linear or rosary-like
  patterns.
- Silent micro-hemorrhage of brain: common small-vessel disease companion and
  mimic when only SWI/GRE punctate foci are described.
- Gliosis / white matter disease: chronic small-vessel FLAIR hyperintensity can
  be adjacent to lacunes but is not itself a lacunar cavity.
- Demyelinating disease: can produce small white matter lesions, but
  distribution, callosal/juxtacortical involvement, dissemination, and
  enhancement pattern differ.
- Enlarged perivascular space: common benign mimic of chronic lacune, especially
  when CSF-like and linear along a vessel without a gliotic rim.

### Key Imaging Discriminators

- Location and distribution: deep perforator territory favors lacunar infarct;
  cortical or multiterritory lesions argue against classic lacunar mechanism.
- Morphology: small round, ovoid, or tubular focal lesion favors lacunar
  infarct; linear border-zone chains favor watershed infarct.
- Diffusion: acute DWI bright/ADC low lesion supports recent infarct.
- Chronic cavity: CSF-like central cavity with possible FLAIR gliotic rim
  supports chronic lacune.
- Susceptibility: punctate SWI/GRE-only lesions favor microbleeds; blooming with
  mixed-signal core can suggest cavernous malformation.
- Interval change: acute lesion evolving to chronic lacune supports infarct;
  progressive enlargement or mass-like enhancement should trigger mimic review.
- Clinical context: lacunar syndrome and vascular risk factors support but do
  not prove lacunar infarct; embolic or large-vessel mechanisms can still
  produce small deep infarcts.

### Similar-Presentation Diseases And Mimic-Aware Comparison

The highest-yield MR-RATE distinctions are lacunar infarct versus broad cerebral
infarction, watershed infarct, microbleeds, gliosis/white matter disease, and
demyelinating disease. The most common practical error is treating any small
deep abnormality as a lacunar infarct. The most important research error is
failing to distinguish acute lacunar infarct from chronic lacune or from
coexisting small-vessel disease burden.

### Report Language That Supports Or Argues Against Each Diagnosis

Findings phrases that support the label:

- "small focus of restricted diffusion"
- "small acute infarct in the thalamus/internal capsule/pons"
- "chronic CSF-signal lacune"
- "remote lacunar infarct"

Impression phrases that support the label:

- "acute lacunar infarct"
- "chronic lacunar infarcts"
- "remote lacunes"

Phrases that suggest a mimic:

- "punctate susceptibility focus"
- "prominent perivascular space"
- "deep watershed pattern"
- "demyelinating plaque"
- "mass-like enhancement"

Phrases that should trigger exclusion, adjudication, or sensitivity analysis:

- "favored lacune versus perivascular space"
- "small infarct, embolic versus lacunar"
- "deep watershed versus lacunar infarcts"
- "nonspecific white matter disease"

### When Additional Imaging Or Clinical Context Helps

Seek additional context when lesion mechanism matters, when lesions are
multiple, when location is atypical, when DWI is absent, when the lesion is
equivocal with perivascular space, or when an embolic/large-vessel source would
change cohort definition. Prior imaging is especially important for chronicity.

## Natural History And Clinical Course

Acute lacunar infarcts may be symptomatic or silent. Over time, some acute small
subcortical infarcts evolve into chronic lacunes, while others may leave less
conspicuous residual abnormality. Chronic lacunes often coexist with white
matter hyperintensities, microbleeds, enlarged perivascular spaces, and brain
atrophy. Accumulating lacunes and other small-vessel disease markers are
associated with cognitive and functional outcomes in population and clinical
research, but symptom correlation is imperfect.

## Treatment, Response, And Outcome Context

### Guideline-Based Management Context

Use stroke and small-vessel disease guidelines for clinical-context claims.
Imaging chapters should not infer treatment eligibility from the report label
alone. In research, the management context matters because lacunar infarcts are
usually not treated as large-vessel occlusion thrombectomy targets, while acute
stroke workup may still include CT, CTA, MRI, vascular risk assessment, and
secondary prevention.

### Common Treatment Pathways

Treatment context usually concerns the underlying stroke and vascular risk
profile rather than the imaging cavity itself:

- acute stroke evaluation and hemorrhage exclusion
- antithrombotic and vascular risk management when clinically appropriate
- hypertension, diabetes, lipid, smoking, and lifestyle risk-factor management
- rehabilitation for deficits
- longitudinal monitoring for recurrent stroke, cognitive impairment, and
  small-vessel disease burden

### Imaging Appearance After Treatment

Treatment does not erase the infarct. Acute diffusion restriction and edema can
resolve; chronic lacune, gliosis, and small-vessel disease markers may persist.
Follow-up imaging may show a smaller cavity than the acute lesion size.

### Evidence Of Treatment Response

Imaging response is not usually measured as lesion shrinkage for lacunar
infarct. Useful research outcomes include absence of new infarcts, stable
lacune burden, stable or slowed small-vessel disease progression, functional
outcomes, recurrent stroke, and cognitive outcomes.

### Evidence Of Progression, Recurrence, Or Treatment Failure

Progression is usually reflected by:

- new acute small subcortical infarct
- increasing number of chronic lacunes
- increasing white matter hyperintensity burden
- new microbleeds or worsening small-vessel disease burden
- recurrent stroke or functional decline

### Expected Outcomes And Prognostic Factors

Important factors include age, vascular risk factors, lesion location,
symptomatic versus silent presentation, total small-vessel disease burden,
recurrent events, cognitive status, gait/balance outcomes, and treatment/risk
factor era.

### Statistical Implications Of Treatment And Progression

Analyses should account for:

- confounding by indication and vascular risk factor treatment
- time from symptom onset to imaging
- surveillance intensity and prior imaging availability
- recurrent event definitions
- competing outcomes such as hemorrhage, dementia, death, or functional decline
- missing sequence bias, especially absent DWI or SWI/GRE
- hierarchical labeling of broad infarct and lacunar subtype

## Evidence Of Active Disease, Progression, Or Recurrence

Active or recent disease is supported by DWI restriction with low ADC in a small
deep perforator distribution. Recurrent disease is supported by interval new
lesions or increasing lacune burden. Increasing small-vessel disease burden
without new DWI-positive infarct should be described separately.

## Stable Or Chronic Residual Findings

Stable chronic lacune is supported by CSF-like signal on all sequences,
possible FLAIR/T2 gliotic rim, no acute restricted diffusion, and stability on
prior imaging. Chronic lacunes should be distinguished from generic white
matter hyperintensity and enlarged perivascular spaces.

## Improvement, Treatment Response, Or Resolution

Improvement can mean resolution of diffusion restriction or edema, clinical
improvement, or no new infarcts. Chronic residual cavities and gliosis may
remain permanently.

## Serial Imaging Assessment And Interval Change

Compare:

- whether the lesion is new or chronic
- DWI/ADC status
- change from acute lesion to chronic cavity
- new lesions elsewhere
- coexisting small-vessel disease burden
- protocol differences, especially DWI slice thickness and SWI/GRE availability

## Clinical Endpoints

- symptomatic lacunar stroke
- recurrent ischemic stroke
- recurrent lacunar infarct
- cognitive decline or vascular cognitive impairment
- gait impairment or falls
- functional outcome/disability score
- mortality

## Imaging, Biomarker, And Measurement Endpoints

- presence/absence of acute small subcortical infarct
- chronic lacune count and location
- incident lacunes on follow-up
- white matter hyperintensity volume or severity
- cerebral microbleed count and distribution
- enlarged perivascular space burden
- brain atrophy
- total small-vessel disease score or multimarker burden

## Common Covariates And Confounders

### Clinical Covariates

- age
- hypertension
- diabetes
- smoking
- dyslipidemia
- prior stroke or TIA
- atrial fibrillation or embolic source
- renal disease and other vascular risk factors
- symptom onset and stroke syndrome

### Imaging Covariates

- DWI/ADC availability
- FLAIR and T1/T2 availability
- SWI/GRE availability for microbleeds
- lesion size, location, and number
- white matter hyperintensity burden
- microbleed burden
- brain atrophy
- prior imaging availability

### Treatment And Temporal Confounders

- time from symptom onset to imaging
- acute stroke therapy exposure
- antithrombotic exposure
- vascular risk-factor control
- rehabilitation and follow-up intensity
- interval between serial scans

### Acquisition And Protocol Confounders

- scanner field strength
- DWI/ADC quality and slice thickness
- FLAIR resolution
- SWI versus GRE sensitivity for microbleeds
- motion artifact
- site and vendor differences
- missing sequences

### Research Design Implications

Separate:

- acute lacunar infarct endpoint
- chronic lacune prevalence
- incident lacune on follow-up
- total small-vessel disease burden
- broad ischemic stroke endpoint

Use adjudication or sensitivity analysis when reports say "lacune versus
perivascular space", "deep watershed versus lacunar", or "small embolic infarct
versus lacunar infarct".

## Statistical Implications

For cohort construction, do not collapse every small deep lesion into a single
lacunar label. Keep these labels separable when the source data allow it:

- acute or recent small subcortical infarct
- chronic lacune of presumed vascular origin
- broad cerebral infarction
- watershed or internal border-zone infarct
- enlarged perivascular space
- diffuse white matter disease or gliosis
- cerebral microbleeds and other small-vessel disease markers

For endpoints, specify whether the endpoint is incident acute infarct, chronic
lacune prevalence, new lacune on follow-up, total small-vessel disease burden,
recurrent stroke, cognitive outcome, or imaging progression. Use adjudication
or sensitivity analysis when reports use uncertain phrases such as "lacune
versus perivascular space" or "small deep infarct, possibly embolic".

## Missing Information To Ask For

- Was the lesion acute/recent on DWI with low ADC, or chronic/remote and
  CSF-like?
- What is the lesion location: basal ganglia, thalamus, internal capsule,
  corona radiata, centrum semiovale, or pons?
- Is the lesion single, multiple, cortical, territorial, border-zone, or in a
  perforator distribution?
- Are SWI/GRE images available to separate infarct from microbleed?
- Are prior studies available to determine interval change or cavitation?
- Are CTA, MRA, carotid imaging, cardiac evaluation, or clinical syndrome
  details available when mechanism matters?
- Does the report mention enlarged perivascular spaces, diffuse white matter
  disease, demyelination, or other mimics?

## Safety And Claim Boundaries

Use this chapter for research planning, known-diagnosis characterization,
report-language extraction, differential framing, and cohort/statistical
reasoning. Keep treatment and prevention discussion at research-context level
and tie it to professional guidance or trial evidence. Do not infer that a
small deep MRI lesion is definitely small-vessel lacunar disease when the
pattern suggests embolic disease, branch atheromatous disease, internal
border-zone infarction, demyelination, tumor, infection, or another mimic.
