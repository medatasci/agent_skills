## Sources That Worked

- NCBI Bookshelf/StatPearls provides clinical context, risk factors, lacunar
  stroke syndromes, CT/MRI roles, and acute versus chronic MRI appearance.
- STRIVE-1 and STRIVE-2 provide research terminology for recent small
  subcortical infarct, lacune, white matter hyperintensity, perivascular space,
  microbleed, and atrophy.
- Small-vessel disease reviews provide the broader context needed to avoid
  confusing lacunar infarct with generic small-vessel disease.
- Radiopaedia provides concise sequence-specific radiology language and a
  practical differential list.
- Stroke-mimic and perivascular-space reviews strengthen the differential
  between lacunar infarct, cerebral infarction, watershed infarct, microbleeds,
  gliosis/white matter disease, demyelination, and enlarged perivascular
  spaces.
- SPS3 papers support cohort, covariate, recurrence, mortality, BP, diabetes,
  metabolic-syndrome, and microbleed considerations for statistical design.
- Video sources provide human-friendly examples for acute DWI/ADC interpretation
  and small-vessel disease context, but remain candidate evidence until
  transcripts are reviewed.
- Cognitive-outcome, recurrent-stroke, and long-term prognosis papers support
  endpoint selection for cognition, disability, recurrence, mortality, and
  vascular events.

## Main Evidence Conclusions

- Acute lacunar infarct is best supported by a small DWI-bright/ADC-low lesion
  in a noncortical perforator territory.
- Chronic lacune is best supported by a small CSF-like cavity, often with a
  FLAIR/T2 gliotic rim, in deep gray matter, deep white matter, or brainstem.
- Small-vessel disease markers frequently coexist, but white matter
  hyperintensity, microbleed, perivascular space, and lacune should remain
  distinct research labels.
- Watershed infarct, embolic small infarcts, demyelination, microbleeds, and
  enlarged perivascular spaces are high-priority mimics.
