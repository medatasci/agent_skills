## Scope

This chapter covers intracranial meningioma as a diagnostic imaging finding,
report label, neuro-oncology phenotype, and research endpoint. It includes
incidental suspected WHO grade 1 meningioma, symptomatic or growing lesions,
skull base disease, optic nerve sheath meningioma, intraventricular meningioma,
atypical/anaplastic meningioma, and postoperative or post-radiation residual
or recurrent disease.

It does not treat every dural enhancing lesion as meningioma. Dural metastasis,
solitary fibrous tumor, schwannoma, lymphoma, inflammatory disease,
postoperative enhancement, and skull base tumors need adjudication when report
language is uncertain.

## Clinical Context

Meningiomas arise from meningothelial cells and are the most common primary
intracranial tumor in adults. Many are incidental and slow-growing. MRI
usually shows a dural-based extra-axial mass with vivid enhancement, often
with a dural tail, CSF cleft, cortical buckling, and variable edema. CT helps
with calcification, hyperostosis, and bone destruction. Grade and molecular
risk require pathology, but imaging can suggest aggressive behavior.

## Known-Diagnosis Review Frame

Characterize location, size, dural attachment, relationship to venous sinuses,
cranial nerves, vessels, optic apparatus, brainstem, and skull base foramina.
Record enhancement pattern, calcification, hyperostosis or destruction, edema,
brain invasion concern, diffusion restriction, necrosis, hemorrhage, cystic
change, perfusion, DOTATATE uptake, prior surgery/radiation, and interval
growth or recurrence.

## What To Look For

### Acute Or Early Presentation

Newly detected meningioma commonly appears as an extra-axial dural-based mass
with avid enhancement. Symptoms may come from seizure, mass effect, edema,
cranial nerve compression, visual symptoms, hydrocephalus, or venous sinus
involvement.

Report-like language:

- "Dural-based enhancing mass along the left frontal convexity with broad
  dural attachment and mild adjacent vasogenic edema, favored meningioma."
- "Extra-axial enhancing petroclival mass causing mass effect on the pons."

### Subacute Or Evolving Presentation

Evolving concern is usually interval growth, new edema, new symptoms, venous
sinus narrowing, optic canal involvement, or increasing skull base extension.
Posttreatment lesions may show postoperative enhancement or transient
enlargement after stereotactic radiotherapy.

Report-like language:

- "Interval enlargement of the right parafalcine meningioma with increased
  vasogenic edema."
- "Post-radiotherapy enlargement may represent transient treatment effect;
  continued follow-up is recommended in the research context."

### Chronic Or Stable Residual Presentation

Stable incidental meningioma may remain unchanged for years. Chronic residual
postoperative disease can be stable along a dural margin, venous sinus, skull
base, or optic canal.

Report-like language:

- "Stable small calcified right frontal convexity meningioma."
- "Stable residual enhancing tissue along the superior sagittal sinus."

### Progressive, Recurrent, Or Worsening Presentation

Progression is suggested by measurable growth, new irregular margins,
heterogeneous enhancement, necrosis, bone destruction, brain invasion concern,
increasing edema, venous sinus occlusion, cranial nerve compromise, or
recurrent nodular enhancement after surgery.

Report-like language:

- "Progressive nodular enhancement along the resection margin, concerning for
  recurrent meningioma."
- "Increased edema and irregular enhancement raise concern for higher-grade
  behavior, although histology is required for grading."

### Improving, Resolving, Or Treatment-Response Presentation

Response may mean stable or decreasing residual tumor, reduced edema, stable
postoperative enhancement, or controlled disease after radiation. Complete
resolution is not expected for many residual tumors.

Report-like language:

- "Decreased size of the treated meningioma with improved mass effect."
- "Stable posttreatment enhancement without evidence of progression."

### Context Clues

Prior imaging, age, symptoms, pregnancy/hormonal context, radiation exposure,
NF2 or genetic context, venous sinus invasion, skull base location, pathology
grade, molecular markers, Simpson resection grade, radiation dose, and time
from treatment change interpretation.

### Report Language Patterns

Findings-style language should describe location, size, dural attachment,
enhancement, calcification, hyperostosis, edema, mass effect, venous sinus or
skull base involvement, cranial nerve/optic apparatus contact, treatment state,
and comparison.

Impression-style language may say:

- "Imaging features are most compatible with meningioma."
- "Stable presumed meningioma."
- "Residual/recurrent meningioma along the resection cavity."
- "Dural-based metastasis or solitary fibrous tumor is not excluded."

Uncertainty language should separate confident meningioma from presumed,
probable, differential-only, or nonspecific dural-based mass labels.

## Primary Imaging Modality

MRI with and without contrast is the primary modality for diagnosis, anatomy,
edema, mass effect, sinus/cranial nerve relationships, follow-up, and
posttreatment assessment.

- T1: usually iso- to hypointense; evaluates anatomy and postcontrast disease.
- T2: variable; edema and cystic change are bright.
- FLAIR: edema, brain-tumor interface, and adjacent gliosis.
- DWI/ADC: hypercellularity and atypical mimics; not a grade substitute.
- SWI/GRE: calcification, hemorrhage, and venous structures.
- Post-contrast T1: key for enhancement, dural tail, residual, recurrence.
- Perfusion, spectroscopy, tractography, volumetry, DOTATATE PET/MRI: useful
  for selected grading, surgical/radiation planning, and recurrence questions.

## Other Modalities And When They Matter

CT helps identify calcification, hyperostosis, bone destruction, and skull
base remodeling. CTA/CTV/MRV may matter for vascular encasement or venous
sinus involvement. DOTATATE PET can help delineate SSTR-positive meningioma,
especially at the skull base or after treatment. Pathology and molecular
testing define grade and aggressive-risk features.

## Locations And Structural Appearance

- cortical/convexity: dural-based mass with cortical buckling and edema.
- subcortical/deep white matter: edema or invasion concern, not tumor origin.
- periventricular/intraventricular: choroid plexus region meningioma can mimic
  other intraventricular tumors.
- corpus callosum: callosal crossing favors glioma/lymphoma over meningioma.
- deep gray nuclei: usually displaced rather than primary location.
- brainstem: skull base or petroclival lesions may compress the brainstem.
- cerebellum: posterior fossa/tentorial lesions can cause hydrocephalus.
- hippocampus/mesial temporal: may be compressed by middle cranial fossa mass.
- postoperative/post-treatment: distinguish scar, residual tumor, recurrence,
  and radiation effect.
- patterns: meningioma is usually extra-axial and dural based; infiltrative
  intra-axial patterns need mimic review.

## Typical Appearance

Typical meningioma is a well-circumscribed extra-axial dural-based mass with
avid homogeneous enhancement, broad dural contact, possible dural tail, CSF
cleft, cortical buckling, calcification, hyperostosis, and variable edema.

## Atypical Or Red-Flag Appearance

Red flags include rapid growth, irregular margins, heterogeneous enhancement,
necrosis, cystic change, marked edema, bone destruction rather than
hyperostosis, diffusion restriction, brain invasion concern, venous sinus
occlusion, and early recurrence after treatment.

## Differential Diagnosis And Mimics

### Quick Differential Diagnosis Guide

1. Dural metastasis: cancer history, multiple dural lesions, bone destruction.
2. Solitary fibrous tumor: dural-based mass, often more aggressive/vascular.
3. Schwannoma: CPA/IAC mass centered on cranial nerve canal.
4. Glioma: intra-axial infiltrative mass and callosal spread.
5. Lymphoma: homogeneous enhancement and restricted diffusion.
6. Pituitary macroadenoma: sellar-centered mass with gland/cavernous sinus
   pattern.
7. Chordoma/epidermoid/inflammatory disease: skull base or CPA mimics.

### Key Imaging Discriminators

Extra-axial signs, dural attachment, hyperostosis, calcification, and vivid
enhancement support meningioma. Bone destruction, multiple lesions, marked
diffusion restriction, intra-axial infiltration, branch nerve-canal origin, or
systemic cancer context support alternatives.

### Differential Diagnosis Matrix

See `differential-matrix.md` for MR-RATE-aware comparisons, including brain
metastasis, glioma, schwannoma, pituitary adenoma, lipoma, arachnoid cyst,
hyperostosis of skull, cerebral edema, and cavernous hemangioma.

### Similar-Presentation Diseases And Mimic-Aware Comparison

Meningioma overlaps with many MR-RATE neoplasm and extra-axial categories. A
generic "dural-based mass" or "enhancing extra-axial lesion" should not be
counted as meningioma unless meningioma is favored or otherwise confirmed.

### Report Language That Supports Or Argues Against Each Diagnosis

Supports meningioma: "favored meningioma," "presumed meningioma," "dural-based
extra-axial enhancing mass," "hyperostosis," "calcified meningioma."
Argues against: "dural metastasis," "solitary fibrous tumor," "schwannoma,"
"intra-axial infiltrative mass," "lymphoma," or "pituitary macroadenoma."

### When Additional Imaging Or Clinical Context Helps

Prior imaging, CT bone windows, contrast MRI, MRV/CTV, DOTATATE PET, pathology,
molecular markers, cancer history, surgical history, and radiation history help
resolve uncertain dural lesions.

## Natural History And Clinical Course

Many incidental meningiomas grow slowly or remain stable. Growth, symptoms,
grade 2/3 pathology, subtotal resection, sinus/skull base involvement, and
molecular high-risk features increase recurrence/progression concern.

## Treatment, Response, And Outcome Context

### Guideline-Based Management Context

Guidelines support observation for selected incidental/asymptomatic lesions,
surgery for symptomatic or growing tumors when feasible, and radiation or
radiosurgery for selected residual, recurrent, inoperable, or high-grade
disease.

### Common Treatment Pathways

Observation with serial MRI; microsurgical resection; stereotactic
radiosurgery or fractionated radiotherapy; reoperation; systemic/targeted
therapy in selected refractory disease or trials.

### Imaging Appearance After Treatment

Posttreatment imaging may show resection cavity enhancement, dural thickening,
residual enhancing tumor, radiation change, edema, transient enlargement, scar,
or recurrent nodular tumor.

### Evidence Of Treatment Response

Response is stable or decreased residual tumor, reduced mass effect/edema, no
new nodular enhancement, durable post-radiation stability, or controlled tumor
volume by trial criteria.

### Evidence Of Progression, Recurrence, Or Treatment Failure

Progression includes measurable growth, new nodular enhancement, increasing
edema, venous sinus compromise, new neurologic symptoms, recurrence after
resection, or growth after radiation.

### Expected Outcomes And Prognostic Factors

Important factors include WHO grade, extent of resection, location, size,
edema, venous sinus/skull base involvement, prior radiation, recurrence
history, molecular markers, age, symptoms, and performance status.

### Statistical Implications Of Treatment And Progression

Treatment comparisons are confounded by indication because larger,
symptomatic, skull base, recurrent, or high-grade tumors are more likely to be
treated. Surveillance intensity, imaging interval, and measurement method affect
growth and progression endpoints.

## Evidence Of Active Disease, Progression, Or Recurrence

Interval growth, new edema, new symptoms, recurrence along a resection margin,
increasing venous sinus involvement, or post-radiation growth beyond expected
transient enlargement suggest active disease.

## Stable Or Chronic Residual Findings

Unchanged size, stable enhancement, stable calcification/hyperostosis, and no
new edema or mass effect support stability. Stable residual enhancement may be
expected after subtotal resection or radiation.

## Improvement, Treatment Response, Or Resolution

Improvement means decreased tumor size or mass effect, reduced edema, stable
postoperative findings, or durable post-radiation control. Complete resolution
is uncommon for residual meningioma.

## Serial Imaging Assessment And Interval Change

Compare size, volume, enhancement, edema, brain-tumor interface, sinus
involvement, bone change, and posttreatment findings on comparable MRI. For
small lesions, measurement plane and slice thickness can create apparent
change.

## Clinical Endpoints

Symptoms, seizure, cranial nerve deficit, visual decline, headache, surgery,
radiation, recurrence, progression-free survival, overall survival,
neurocognition, quality of life, steroid use, and functional status.

## Imaging, Biomarker, And Measurement Endpoints

Tumor diameter, volume, growth rate, edema volume, sinus invasion, bone
involvement, diffusion/perfusion metrics, DOTATATE uptake, extent of resection,
residual/recurrent enhancement, and response/progression category.

## Common Covariates And Confounders

### Clinical Covariates

Age, sex, symptoms, seizure, visual/cranial nerve deficits, prior radiation,
NF2/genetic context, hormone-related context, pathology grade, molecular
markers, and performance status.

### Imaging Covariates

Location, size, volume, edema, calcification, hyperostosis, bone destruction,
venous sinus involvement, skull base extension, diffusion, perfusion,
DOTATATE uptake, prior imaging interval, and measurement method.

### Treatment And Temporal Confounders

Observation versus surgery versus radiation; extent of resection; time from
treatment; postoperative enhancement; transient enlargement; reoperation;
reirradiation; trial/systemic therapy exposure.

### Acquisition And Protocol Confounders

Scanner, contrast timing, 2D versus 3D T1, slice thickness, CT bone windows,
motion, artifact from clips/hardware, PET tracer availability, and inconsistent
measurement plane.

### Research Design Implications

Stratify presumed versus pathology-confirmed meningioma, grade, location,
treatment state, and recurrent disease. Adjudicate nonspecific dural masses and
perform sensitivity analysis excluding uncertain mimics.

## Statistical Implications

Meningioma labels are noisy because reports may say presumed meningioma,
dural-based mass, treated meningioma, residual tumor, or differential-only
meningioma. Cohorts should separate incidental presumed meningioma, treated
meningioma, high-grade meningioma, and recurrent disease. Time-to-progression
analyses must handle surveillance bias and interval censoring.

## Missing Information To Ask For

Was the lesion pathology confirmed or presumed? What is the location, size,
grade, treatment history, growth interval, edema, bone change, sinus/skull base
involvement, and cancer history? Are CT bone windows, prior MRI, DOTATATE PET,
or pathology/molecular markers available?

## Safety And Claim Boundaries

report-language interpretation, cohort definition, endpoint selection,
covariate review, and mimic-aware evidence review. It is not a substitute for
