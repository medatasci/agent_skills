## Scope

This chapter covers pituitary adenoma/PitNET as an MR-RATE brain MRI report
category, sellar/parasellar mass label, endocrine tumor phenotype, and research
endpoint. It includes microadenomas, macroadenomas, functioning adenomas,
nonfunctioning adenomas, apoplexy, postoperative residual or recurrent tumor,
and major sellar mimics.

## Clinical Context

Pituitary adenomas are common sellar lesions arising from anterior pituitary
cells. MRI interpretation depends on size, function, endocrine testing, visual
field status, cavernous sinus and optic pathway involvement, treatment history,
and whether the lesion is incidental, symptomatic, secreting, postoperative, or
recurrent. Imaging can strongly support the diagnosis but should preserve
uncertainty when the report only describes a nonspecific sellar mass.

## Known-Diagnosis Review Frame

For a known or suspected pituitary adenoma, record lesion size, microadenoma
versus macroadenoma status, sellar/suprasellar extent, relation to optic
chiasm, infundibulum position, cavernous sinus invasion or encasement, ICA
relationship, sphenoid/clival extension, cystic/hemorrhagic/necrotic change,
dynamic enhancement, treatment state, and endocrine phenotype when available.

## What To Look For

### Acute Or Early Presentation

Small functioning microadenomas may be subtle. On dedicated pituitary MRI they
often appear as a rounded or focal region of delayed or decreased enhancement
relative to the normal anterior pituitary, sometimes with gland asymmetry,
subtle sellar floor remodeling, or infundibular deviation.

Report-like language: "Focal hypoenhancing lesion within the right pituitary
gland measuring 5 mm, suspicious for pituitary microadenoma." "No definite
sellar lesion is identified on routine brain MRI; dedicated pituitary protocol
may be considered if biochemical suspicion persists."

### Subacute Or Evolving Presentation

Macroadenomas may enlarge over time with suprasellar extension, optic chiasm
contact or compression, cavernous sinus extension, sphenoid sinus or clival
extension, cystic degeneration, hemorrhage, or worsening hypopituitarism.

Report-like language: "Interval enlargement of a heterogeneously enhancing
sellar/suprasellar mass with increased optic chiasm elevation." "Progressive
right cavernous sinus extension with partial encasement of the cavernous ICA."

### Chronic Or Stable Residual Presentation

Stable lesions may show unchanged microadenoma size, stable macroadenoma
dimensions, unchanged postoperative residual enhancing tissue, or chronic
sellar remodeling without new optic pathway compression.

Report-like language: "Stable hypoenhancing pituitary microadenoma." "Stable
residual enhancing tissue along the left cavernous sinus, compatible with
residual adenoma."

### Progressive, Recurrent, Or Worsening Presentation

Progression includes interval growth, new optic chiasm compression, new or
worsening cavernous sinus invasion, new cranial nerve symptoms, increasing
residual tumor after surgery, or biochemical recurrence for functioning tumors.

Report-like language: "Increasing nodular enhancement in the operative bed is
concerning for recurrent or residual pituitary adenoma." "Increased
suprasellar extension now contacts the optic chiasm."

### Improving, Resolving, Or Treatment-Response Presentation

Treatment response can include dopamine-agonist shrinkage of prolactinoma,
postoperative decompression of the optic apparatus, decreased residual tumor,
or stable residual cavernous sinus disease after surgery/radiation.

Report-like language: "Marked decrease in size of the previously seen
macroadenoma with resolution of optic chiasm compression." "Postoperative
changes without nodular residual enhancing adenoma."

### Context Clues

Endocrine phenotype, prolactin level, hook-effect testing, IGF-1, cortisol or
ACTH workup, visual field testing, pregnancy status, prior surgery, radiation,
dopamine agonist exposure, and prior MRI strongly affect interpretation.

### Report Language Patterns

Findings-style language should describe size in three dimensions, enhancement,
location within gland, sellar/suprasellar extent, optic chiasm relationship,
infundibulum deviation, cavernous sinus/ICA relationship, hemorrhage,
postoperative change, and comparison.

Impression-style language may say: "pituitary microadenoma," "pituitary
macroadenoma," "sellar/suprasellar mass favored pituitary macroadenoma,"
"residual/recurrent adenoma," "pituitary apoplexy," or "Rathke cleft cyst is
favored over adenoma." Preserve "possible" or "suspected" when imaging is
equivocal.

## Primary Imaging Modality

Dedicated pituitary MRI with thin-section small-field-of-view coronal and sagittal T1 images before and after gadolinium is primary. Dynamic contrast-enhanced imaging is particularly useful for microadenomas. T2 helps characterize cystic, hemorrhagic, and inflammatory mimics. Routine brain MRI can miss small lesions.

## Other Modalities And When They Matter

CT can help evaluate bone remodeling, calcification, acute hemorrhage, surgical anatomy, and MRI contraindications. CTA/MRA is useful when aneurysm is a sellar mimic. Visual field testing, endocrine labs, pathology, and molecular/lineage classification are essential non-imaging context.

## Locations And Structural Appearance

Pituitary adenomas arise in the sella from anterior pituitary tissue. Microadenomas are usually intraglandular. Macroadenomas expand the sella and may extend superiorly into the suprasellar cistern, laterally into the cavernous sinus, inferiorly into sphenoid sinus or clivus, or posteriorly/superiorly toward the hypothalamus and optic pathways.

## Typical Appearance

Microadenomas are often focal hypoenhancing or delayed-enhancing intrapituitary lesions. Macroadenomas are sellar masses with variable heterogeneous enhancement, often iso- to hypointense on T1 and variable on T2, with possible cystic change, necrosis, hemorrhage, and sellar expansion.

## Atypical Or Red-Flag Appearance

Red flags include rapid growth, marked invasive behavior, apoplexy, unusual stalk thickening, strong homogeneous dural-based enhancement, calcified suprasellar cystic/solid lesion, aneurysm-like flow void, leptomeningeal/systemic metastasis context, or inflammatory features suggesting hypophysitis.

## Differential Diagnosis And Mimics

Use this section for sellar and parasellar masses, hypoenhancing gland lesions, cystic sellar lesions, postoperative enhancement, and pituitary apoplexy.

### Quick Differential Diagnosis Guide

Common mimics include Rathke cleft cyst, craniopharyngioma, meningioma, aneurysm, hypophysitis, empty sella, arachnoid cyst, epidermoid, metastasis, lymphoma, germinoma, lipoma, and postoperative change.

### Key Imaging Discriminators

Pituitary adenoma is supported by intraglandular origin, delayed/hypoenhancement for microadenoma, sellar expansion for macroadenoma, endocrine phenotype, and typical suprasellar/cavernous extension. Rathke cleft cyst is supported by a midline nonenhancing cyst with intracystic nodule. Meningioma is supported by dural base, dural tail, hyperostosis, and separate normal gland. Aneurysm is supported by flow void or vascular imaging.

### Differential Diagnosis Matrix

See `differential-matrix.md` for MR-RATE-aware contrasts to Rathke cleft cyst, empty sella, intracranial meningioma, schwannoma, lipoma of brain, metastatic malignant neoplasm, intracranial aneurysm, arachnoid cyst, cyst of pineal gland, glioma, and pituitary-region mimics.

### Similar-Presentation Diseases And Mimic-Aware Comparison

Pituitary adenoma overlaps with the MR-RATE neoplasm/mass categories when reports use generic terms such as sellar mass, suprasellar mass, or enhancing mass. It overlaps with cyst/developmental categories when the lesion is cystic or incidental. Generic mass language should not be counted as adenoma unless the impression favors adenoma or clinical/pathology context supports it.

### Report Language That Supports Or Argues Against Each Diagnosis

Supports adenoma: pituitary adenoma, PitNET, microadenoma, macroadenoma, hypoenhancing pituitary lesion, delayed enhancement, sellar/suprasellar mass favored adenoma, residual adenoma. Argues against adenoma: Rathke cleft cyst, craniopharyngioma, meningioma, aneurysm, hypophysitis, empty sella, arachnoid cyst, metastasis, no discrete adenoma.

### When Additional Imaging Or Clinical Context Helps

Dedicated pituitary protocol, dynamic contrast, thin-section coronal/sagittal images, prior MRI, endocrine labs, visual field testing, CTA/MRA, pathology, operative note, and treatment dates help resolve uncertain cases.

## Natural History And Clinical Course

Many pituitary adenomas are indolent, but course varies by size, hormone secretion, invasion, and treatment. Microadenomas may remain stable. Macroadenomas can cause mass effect, hypopituitarism, and visual field defects. Prolactinomas may shrink with dopamine agonists. Nonfunctioning adenomas are often detected after mass effect or incidentally.

## Treatment, Response, And Outcome Context

Treatment depends on functional status, size, symptoms, optic pathway compression, invasion, endocrine syndrome, and recurrence risk. Observation, medication, transsphenoidal surgery, radiation, and hormone replacement all affect imaging and endpoints.

### Guideline-Based Management Context

Endocrine Society incidentaloma guidance frames MRI follow-up, visual field testing, endocrine testing, and surgery triggers. CNS guidance frames nonfunctioning adenoma imaging and management. Pituitary Society, Endotext, and endocrine guidelines frame prolactinoma, acromegaly, Cushing disease, apoplexy, and postoperative surveillance.

### Common Treatment Pathways

Observation for stable incidental lesions; dopamine agonist treatment for many prolactinomas; transsphenoidal surgery for symptomatic mass effect or many non-prolactinoma functional tumors; radiation or stereotactic radiosurgery for selected residual/recurrent disease; hormone replacement for hypopituitarism.

### Imaging Appearance After Treatment

Postoperative sella can show packing material, peripheral enhancement, fat graft, blood products, and distorted gland. Nodular thick enhancement, especially if persistent or growing, may suggest residual or recurrent adenoma. Dopamine agonist response may markedly shrink prolactinoma.

### Evidence Of Treatment Response

Response can be radiographic shrinkage, optic pathway decompression, stable or decreased residual tumor, hormone normalization, visual field improvement, or decreased need for additional treatment.

### Evidence Of Progression, Recurrence, Or Treatment Failure

Progression includes growth on serial MRI, increasing residual nodular enhancement, new chiasmal compression, increasing cavernous sinus invasion, recurrent biochemical hypersecretion, or new hypopituitarism/visual deficit.

### Expected Outcomes And Prognostic Factors

Outcomes depend on tumor size, functional subtype, cavernous sinus invasion, Knosp grade, residual tumor, hormone secretion, prior treatment, surgical access, age, visual status, and endocrine deficits.

### Statistical Implications Of Treatment And Progression

Treatment selection is confounded by size, symptoms, endocrine phenotype, optic compression, invasion, and center practice. Postoperative imaging creates measurement ambiguity; biochemical and imaging endpoints should be analyzed separately when possible.

## Evidence Of Active Disease, Progression, Or Recurrence

Active disease is suggested by new or enlarging sellar lesion, macroadenoma growth, increasing suprasellar extension, new optic chiasm compression, increasing cavernous sinus/ICA involvement, recurrent nodular postoperative enhancement, or recurrent hormonal excess.

## Stable Or Chronic Residual Findings

Stable microadenoma, stable macroadenoma, stable postoperative residual cavernous sinus enhancement, chronic sellar remodeling, or stable empty sella after treatment can be chronic findings rather than progression.

## Improvement, Treatment Response, Or Resolution

Improvement includes decreased tumor size, decompressed optic chiasm, reduced mass effect, stable or normalized hormone markers, decreased residual enhancement, and no new cavernous sinus or suprasellar extension.

## Serial Imaging Assessment And Interval Change

Compare the same pituitary protocol, contrast timing, slice thickness, lesion dimensions, volume if available, chiasm contact, cavernous sinus/ICA relationship, postoperative baseline, treatment dates, and endocrine/visual endpoints.

## Clinical Endpoints

Visual field deficit, visual acuity, ophthalmoplegia, headache, hypopituitarism, hormone hypersecretion/remission, apoplexy, surgery, radiation, medication response, recurrence, quality of life, fertility, and need for hormone replacement.

## Imaging, Biomarker, And Measurement Endpoints

Maximum diameter, tumor volume, micro/macro status, chiasm contact/compression, cavernous sinus invasion, Knosp grade, residual tumor, postoperative nodular enhancement, apoplexy, optic pathway decompression, and serial growth rate.

## Common Covariates And Confounders

Important covariates include hormone subtype, size, sex, age, pregnancy status, prior treatment, endocrine labs, visual field status, cavernous sinus invasion, MRI protocol, contrast timing, and surveillance interval.

### Clinical Covariates

Age, sex, symptoms, endocrine syndrome, prolactin/GH/IGF-1/ACTH/cortisol/TSH/gonadotropin context, pregnancy, medication exposure, visual fields, hypopituitarism, and prior surgery/radiation/medical therapy.

### Imaging Covariates

Dedicated pituitary protocol, dynamic contrast availability, lesion size, enhancement pattern, cystic/hemorrhagic change, suprasellar extension, chiasm compression, cavernous sinus invasion, ICA encasement, and postoperative baseline.

### Treatment And Temporal Confounders

Dopamine agonist therapy, surgery, radiation, packing material, postoperative timing, steroid replacement, endocrine medication, and interval from baseline MRI affect apparent response or recurrence.

### Acquisition And Protocol Confounders

Routine brain MRI versus pituitary MRI, slice thickness, field strength, contrast bolus timing, motion, susceptibility from postoperative material, and CT/MRI availability affect detection and measurement.

### Research Design Implications

Stratify by microadenoma versus macroadenoma, functioning versus nonfunctioning, subtype, treatment state, and cavernous sinus invasion. Separate imaging-progression endpoints from biochemical-remission endpoints.

## Statistical Implications

Pituitary adenoma cohorts need precise label definitions: suspected versus confirmed, micro versus macro, functioning versus nonfunctioning, untreated versus postoperative, residual versus recurrent, and adenoma versus sellar mimic. Differential-only report labels should be adjudicated or analyzed as uncertain.

## Missing Information To Ask For

Ask for dedicated pituitary MRI sequences, lesion size, endocrine labs, functional subtype, visual field status, prior MRI, surgery/radiation/medication history, pathology, Knosp/cavernous sinus status, and whether adenoma is favored or only in the differential.
