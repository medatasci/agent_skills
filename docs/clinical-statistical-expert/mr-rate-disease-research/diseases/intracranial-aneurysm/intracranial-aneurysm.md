# Intracranial Aneurysm

## Goals

This disease chapter is designed to help a clinical-statistical reviewer:

1. Given a known or suspected intracranial aneurysm context, describe how a
   cerebral aneurysm appears on diagnostic vascular imaging, especially MRA,
   CTA, and DSA, using clinically realistic radiology-report language.
2. Identify imaging features, locations, structural patterns, report phrases,
   rupture clues, and clinical context that support or argue against
   intracranial aneurysm relative to similar MR-RATE categories.
3. Translate aneurysm appearance, report language, natural history, treatment
   context, and diagnostic uncertainty into research-design and statistical
   implications, including cohort definition, endpoint selection, covariates,
   adjudication, misclassification risk, and claims language.

## Scope

This chapter covers intracranial aneurysm as a vascular imaging finding,
report label, and clinical-statistical research phenotype. It includes
incidentally detected unruptured aneurysms, ruptured aneurysms associated with
subarachnoid hemorrhage, treated aneurysms, residual/recurrent aneurysm,
thrombosed or giant aneurysms, posterior circulation aneurysms, and aneurysms
with vessel-wall MRI features.

The chapter does not treat subarachnoid hemorrhage as equivalent to aneurysm
unless an aneurysm is identified or the report specifically attributes the
hemorrhage to aneurysmal rupture. It also does not treat infundibula,
dolichoectasia, AVMs, dural AV fistulas, or nonspecific vascular loops as
aneurysms without report or imaging evidence.

## Clinical Context

Intracranial aneurysms are focal dilations or outpouchings of intracranial
arteries, most often saccular lesions arising at branch points around the
circle of Willis. Many are asymptomatic and incidentally detected on CTA, MRA,
or angiography. Rupture commonly produces aneurysmal subarachnoid hemorrhage,
which is a high-morbidity emergency. Large or giant aneurysms may cause mass
effect, cranial nerve palsy, thromboembolism, or obstructive hydrocephalus.

The primary imaging task is vascular: define whether there is a true aneurysm,
then characterize location, parent vessel, branch incorporation, size, neck,
morphology, rupture status, thrombus, calcification, wall enhancement, and
treatment state. DSA remains the reference standard when noninvasive imaging is
inadequate or treatment planning requires precise anatomy. CTA and MRA are
common detection, screening, and follow-up tools.

For research use, the key distinction is whether the label means:

- incidental unruptured aneurysm
- symptomatic unruptured aneurysm causing cranial nerve or mass-effect symptoms
- ruptured aneurysm with aneurysmal SAH
- treated aneurysm after clipping, coiling, stent-assisted coiling, or flow
  diversion
- residual, recurrent, growing, or newly detected aneurysm
- suspected aneurysm versus infundibulum, vascular loop, AVM-associated
  aneurysm, or dissection/pseudoaneurysm

## Known-Diagnosis Review Frame

When the diagnosis or report label is intracranial aneurysm, characterize:

- location: ACoA, PCoA, MCA bifurcation, ICA terminus, ophthalmic/supraclinoid
  ICA, basilar tip, vertebrobasilar, PICA, SCA, distal branch, cavernous ICA
- morphology: saccular, fusiform, dissecting, blister, mycotic, giant,
  thrombosed, lobulated, irregular, daughter sac, or bleb
- size: maximal dome diameter, height, width, neck width, dome-to-neck ratio,
  volume when available
- rupture context: SAH pattern, intraventricular hemorrhage, sentinel bleed,
  wall enhancement, adjacent clot, or multiple aneurysms requiring culprit
  identification
- treatment state: untreated, clipped, coiled, stented, flow-diverted,
  bypassed, residual neck, recurrence, in-stent stenosis, or parent vessel
  occlusion
- mimics: infundibulum, arterial loop, vessel origin, venous structure,
  dolichoectasia, AVM nidus/flow-related aneurysm, cavernous malformation,
  hemorrhagic mass, calcification, or artifact

## What To Look For

### Acute Or Early Presentation

In acute rupture, noncontrast CT may show subarachnoid hemorrhage, often with a
pattern suggesting the likely aneurysm location. CTA or DSA may show a
saccular outpouching, irregular dome, bleb, daughter sac, focal contrast
extravasation, vasospasm, or multiple aneurysms. MRI may show FLAIR sulcal
hyperintensity, susceptibility from blood products, hydrocephalus, infarction,
or vessel-wall enhancement in the culprit aneurysm.

Report-like language:

- "A 5 mm anterior communicating artery aneurysm is present with adjacent
  diffuse subarachnoid hemorrhage, suspicious for ruptured aneurysm."
- "CTA demonstrates a lobulated right posterior communicating artery aneurysm
  with an apical bleb."
- "Multiple aneurysms are present; hemorrhage distribution favors the anterior
  communicating artery aneurysm as the likely culprit."

### Subacute Or Evolving Presentation

After rupture or treatment, imaging may show evolving SAH, vasospasm,
hydrocephalus, infarction, residual aneurysm filling, coil mass, clip artifact,
stent/flow-diverter artifact, or thrombosis. Wall enhancement or perianeurysmal
edema can be present but should be interpreted cautiously.

Report-like language:

- "Status post coil embolization with small residual neck filling."
- "Evolving subarachnoid hemorrhage with mild hydrocephalus and vasospasm of
  the bilateral anterior cerebral arteries."
- "Partially thrombosed aneurysm demonstrates peripheral flow-related signal
  and mural thrombus."

### Chronic Or Stable Residual Presentation

Stable unruptured aneurysms are usually followed by CTA or MRA and described
by unchanged size, morphology, and neck configuration. Treated aneurysms may
show stable coil mass or clip artifact without residual filling. On routine MRI
without angiography, a patent aneurysm may be a flow void, enhancing vascular
outpouching, or signal void; thrombosed aneurysms can appear mass-like and
heterogeneous.

Report-like language:

- "Stable 3 mm laterally directed left MCA bifurcation aneurysm."
- "No interval growth or new irregularity of the known basilar tip aneurysm."
- "Stable post-coiling appearance without definite residual aneurysm filling
  on MRA."

### Progressive, Recurrent, Or Worsening Presentation

Concerning change includes aneurysm growth, new irregularity, daughter sac,
wall enhancement, perianeurysmal edema, new symptoms, new SAH, residual or
recurrent filling after treatment, coil compaction, in-stent stenosis, or new
aneurysm formation in high-risk contexts.

Report-like language:

- "Interval growth of the anterior communicating artery aneurysm from 4 mm to
  6 mm with new irregular dome morphology."
- "Recurrent filling at the aneurysm neck after coil embolization."
- "New circumferential aneurysm wall enhancement; instability cannot be
  excluded and vascular-neurosurgical review is suggested in the research
  context."

### Improving, Resolving, Or Treatment-Response Presentation

Treatment response means aneurysm occlusion, reduced residual filling, stable
clip/coil/stent construct, preserved parent vessel, absence of new ischemia or
hemorrhage, and stable or resolving SAH complications. Flow diversion may
produce delayed aneurysm thrombosis and gradual occlusion.

Report-like language:

- "Complete occlusion of the treated aneurysm without residual neck filling."
- "Patent flow-diverter stent with progressive thrombosis of the aneurysm sac."
- "No new hemorrhage or infarct after aneurysm treatment."

### Context Clues

- Family history, polycystic kidney disease, smoking, hypertension, female sex,
  prior SAH, aneurysm growth, posterior circulation location, size, irregular
  shape, and daughter sac change rupture-risk interpretation.
- Thunderclap headache, diffuse basal cisternal SAH, hydrocephalus, or
  intraventricular hemorrhage changes urgency and endpoint definitions.
- A PCoA aneurysm with oculomotor palsy is a symptom-specific clinical context.
- A cavernous ICA aneurysm may cause cranial neuropathy but is outside the
  subarachnoid space.
- Tiny aneurysms and infundibula require careful adjudication because false
  positives can materially affect cohort labels.

### Report Language Patterns

Findings-style language should describe:

- parent artery and exact location
- dome direction and relationship to branch vessels
- maximal dome size, height, width, and neck width
- saccular, fusiform, blister, dissecting, mycotic, thrombosed, giant, or
  irregular morphology
- bleb, daughter sac, lobulation, calcification, thrombus, wall enhancement
- SAH pattern, hydrocephalus, vasospasm, infarct, or mass effect
- treatment device, residual filling, recurrence, or parent-vessel patency

Impression-style language should synthesize:

- "Small unruptured left MCA bifurcation aneurysm."
- "Ruptured anterior communicating artery aneurysm with diffuse aneurysmal
  subarachnoid hemorrhage."
- "Stable treated basilar tip aneurysm without residual filling."
- "Vascular outpouching favored to represent an infundibulum rather than true
  aneurysm."

Uncertainty and cohort-label language:

- Confident aneurysm: "saccular aneurysm," "intracranial aneurysm," "aneurysm
  neck," "aneurysm dome," "residual aneurysm filling."
- Probable aneurysm: "suspected small aneurysm," "vascular outpouching favored
  aneurysm."
- Low-confidence/nonspecific: "prominence," "vascular loop," "possible
  infundibulum," "artifact versus aneurysm."
- Adjudication trigger: tiny lesion, branch-origin funnel shape, discordant CTA
  and MRA, adjacent venous structure, thrombosed mass-like lesion, or treated
  aneurysm artifact.

## Primary Imaging Modality

MRA is useful for screening and follow-up, particularly time-of-flight MRA for
unruptured aneurysms and contrast-enhanced MRA in selected cases. CTA is often
used for acute SAH workup and high-resolution morphologic characterization.
DSA remains the reference standard when noninvasive imaging is equivocal,
small lesions are suspected, multiple aneurysms must be resolved, or treatment
planning requires exact branch and neck anatomy.

- T1: can show thrombus, mass effect, postcontrast enhancement, and vessel-wall
  MRI features.
- T2: may show flow void, thrombus layers, edema, or mass effect.
- FLAIR: detects SAH, perianeurysmal edema, and complications.
- DWI/ADC: detects ischemic complications, vasospasm infarcts, and treatment
  complications.
- SWI/GRE: detects hemorrhage and susceptibility from blood products or device
  artifact.
- Post-contrast T1: evaluates wall enhancement, dural/venous mimics, and
  treated or thrombosed aneurysm complications.
- Perfusion, spectroscopy, tractography, volumetry, or other advanced methods:
  vessel-wall MRI, 4D-flow MRI, computational flow modeling, and quantitative
  morphology can support research but should not replace CTA/MRA/DSA-based
  adjudication.

## Other Modalities And When They Matter

Noncontrast CT matters for acute thunderclap headache and SAH detection. CTA
maps aneurysm anatomy in acute and outpatient contexts. DSA provides the most
precise lumen, branch, perforator, and treatment-planning information but is
invasive. Lumbar puncture may matter when SAH is suspected and CT is negative,
but it does not localize the aneurysm. Genetic/family history, blood pressure,
smoking, and connective-tissue or renal disease context matter for risk
stratification.

## Locations And Structural Appearance

- cortical: distal cortical branch aneurysms may be mycotic, traumatic, or
  flow-related and require source-specific caution.
- subcortical: primary aneurysm is vascular, but associated infarct/hemorrhage
  can involve subcortical structures.
- deep white matter: abnormality usually reflects complication or mimic rather
  than aneurysm itself.
- periventricular: intraventricular hemorrhage or hydrocephalus can complicate
  rupture.
- corpus callosum: pericallosal/distal ACA aneurysms may project near the
  corpus callosum.
- deep gray nuclei: Charcot-Bouchard microaneurysms and hypertensive
  hemorrhage are distinct from typical saccular circle-of-Willis aneurysms.
- brainstem: basilar, vertebral, PICA, SCA, and posterior circulation aneurysms
  can compress or threaten the brainstem.
- cerebellum: PICA/AICA/SCA aneurysms may present with posterior fossa SAH or
  infarction.
- hippocampus or mesial temporal structures: adjacent PCoA or ICA aneurysms may
  cause mass effect or SAH but are vascular lesions.
- postoperative, post-treatment, or post-injury regions: clips, coils, stents,
  bypasses, parent-vessel occlusion, residual neck, and recurrent filling must
  be compared with prior angiography.
- vascular-territory, tract-like, multifocal, diffuse, symmetric, asymmetric,
  mass-like, cavitary, atrophic, or volume-loss patterns: aneurysm is a focal
  vascular lesion; parenchymal patterns usually represent complications or
  mimics.

## Typical Appearance

Typical intracranial aneurysm appears as a focal rounded or lobulated vascular
outpouching from an intracranial artery, most often at an arterial bifurcation.
CTA and MRA show enhancing or flow-related signal within the lumen. DSA shows
the aneurysm neck, dome, branch incorporation, and dynamic filling. Report
language should specify size, location, direction, neck, morphology, treatment
state, and comparison.

## Atypical Or Red-Flag Appearance

Red flags include irregular morphology, daughter sac or bleb, interval growth,
posterior circulation location, symptomatic cranial nerve palsy, giant size,
partial thrombosis, perianeurysmal edema, wall enhancement, new SAH, multiple
aneurysms with uncertain culprit, suspected mycotic/dissecting/blister
aneurysm, or residual filling after treatment.

## Differential Diagnosis And Mimics

Use this section for questions such as "what else could this vascular
outpouching be?", "what imaging features support aneurysm?", and "what would
make a similar-appearing diagnosis more likely?"

### Quick Differential Diagnosis Guide

1. Infundibulum: funnel-shaped branch origin, usually small, with branch vessel
   arising from apex.
2. Arterial loop or tortuosity: elongated vessel course without a true neck or
   sac.
3. AVM or dural AV fistula: shunting lesion with nidus or abnormal early
   draining veins.
4. Cavernous malformation: intra-axial hemosiderin-rimmed lesion without an
   arterial lumen.
5. Hemorrhagic tumor or metastasis: enhancing intra-axial mass with edema and
   nonvascular morphology.
6. Subarachnoid hemorrhage of nonaneurysmal cause: perimesencephalic pattern,
   trauma, venous, vasculitis, or RCVS.
7. Pseudoaneurysm/dissection: irregular vessel dilation, wall injury, and
   different natural history than saccular aneurysm.

### Key Imaging Discriminators

- True aneurysm has a sac/dome and neck arising from an artery.
- Infundibulum is conical and has a branch arising from the apex.
- AVM has a nidus and early venous drainage; aneurysm is a focal arterial sac.
- Cavernous malformation is intra-axial with susceptibility and no arterial
  lumen.
- Thrombosed aneurysm may be mass-like; angiography can underestimate the
  thrombosed component.
- DSA may be needed for tiny aneurysms, equivocal branch-origin lesions, or
  treatment planning.

### Differential Diagnosis Matrix

See `differential-matrix.md` for MR-RATE-aware comparisons, including
cerebral hemorrhage, subdural hemorrhage, silent micro-hemorrhage, cavernous
hemangioma, cerebral infarction, glioma, metastasis, meningioma, arachnoid
cyst, ventriculomegaly, and Chiari-related or posterior fossa mimics.

### Similar-Presentation Diseases And Mimic-Aware Comparison

Intracranial aneurysm often enters a differential because a report describes
SAH, hemorrhage, a vascular outpouching, a mass-like thrombosed lesion, or
treated neurovascular device artifact. For MR-RATE labeling, reports that
mention aneurysm only as "no aneurysm identified" or "aneurysm excluded" should
not be counted as positive labels.

### Report Language That Supports Or Argues Against Each Diagnosis

- Supports aneurysm: "saccular aneurysm," "aneurysm neck," "dome projects,"
  "wide-neck aneurysm," "daughter sac," "residual aneurysm filling."
- Supports infundibulum: "funnel-shaped origin," "branch vessel arises from
  apex," "infundibular dilatation."
- Supports AVM/dAVF: "nidus," "arteriovenous shunting," "early draining
  vein," "dural fistula."
- Supports cavernous malformation: "popcorn lesion," "hemosiderin rim,"
  "developmental venous anomaly."
- Supports hemorrhagic tumor: "enhancing mass," "vasogenic edema," "multiple
  metastases."

### When Additional Imaging Or Clinical Context Helps

Additional imaging helps when the lesion is tiny, near a branch origin, near
bone, treated with coils/clips/stents, partially thrombosed, mass-like,
associated with SAH, or discordant between CTA and MRA. DSA, 3D rotational
angiography, contrast-enhanced MRA, vessel-wall MRI, and comparison with prior
studies can clarify the label.

## Natural History And Clinical Course

Most unruptured aneurysms do not rupture, but rupture risk is not uniform. Risk
depends on aneurysm size, location, shape, growth, prior SAH, hypertension,
smoking, age, population, family history, and other patient factors. Aneurysm
growth is an important imaging endpoint because it changes risk and management
considerations. Rupture produces aneurysmal SAH with risks of rebleeding,
hydrocephalus, vasospasm, delayed cerebral ischemia, infarction, and death or
disability.

## Treatment, Response, And Outcome Context

### Guideline-Based Management Context

AHA/ASA and ESO sources frame unruptured aneurysm care as a balance between
estimated rupture risk and preventive treatment risk. Aneurysmal SAH guidance
emphasizes prompt identification and securing of the ruptured aneurysm,
preferably in experienced centers. Imaging follow-up is used after treatment
and during observation to detect growth, recurrence, or residual filling.

### Common Treatment Pathways

- observation with serial CTA/MRA or selected DSA
- smoking cessation and blood-pressure management in appropriate clinical
  context
- microsurgical clipping
- endovascular coiling
- balloon- or stent-assisted coiling
- flow diversion
- parent-vessel occlusion, bypass, or complex reconstructive strategies
- acute SAH management including aneurysm securing, hydrocephalus management,
  vasospasm surveillance, and delayed cerebral ischemia prevention

### Imaging Appearance After Treatment

After clipping, CT/MRI/CTA/MRA may show clip artifact and need careful
assessment for residual filling. After coiling, a coil mass may obscure the
sac, and MRA/CTA/DSA may evaluate neck remnant or recurrence. After stents or
flow diverters, assess device patency, in-stent stenosis, branch patency, and
delayed aneurysm occlusion.

### Evidence Of Treatment Response

Treatment response is represented by complete occlusion, reduced residual
filling, stable device position, patent parent vessel, absence of new ischemia
or hemorrhage, and durable stability on follow-up imaging.

### Evidence Of Progression, Recurrence, Or Treatment Failure

Progression or failure is suggested by growth, new irregularity, new bleb,
aneurysm wall enhancement, recurrent filling after coiling, coil compaction,
residual neck enlargement, stent stenosis, parent-vessel compromise, or new
SAH.

### Expected Outcomes And Prognostic Factors

Relevant factors include aneurysm size, location, morphology, prior SAH,
growth, family history, smoking, hypertension, age, treatment modality, center
experience, rupture status, initial clinical grade, hydrocephalus, vasospasm,
delayed cerebral ischemia, and functional recovery.

### Statistical Implications Of Treatment And Progression

Treatment is strongly confounded by indication because higher-risk aneurysms
are more likely to be treated. Imaging surveillance intensity also differs by
risk and treatment status. Studies should separate rupture, growth, treatment,
retreatment, occlusion grade, recurrence, morbidity, mortality, and functional
outcome endpoints.

## Evidence Of Active Disease, Progression, Or Recurrence

Active or worsening aneurysm disease is suggested by interval growth, new
irregularity, daughter sac, wall enhancement, new symptoms, new SAH, residual
or recurrent filling, coil compaction, or new aneurysm formation in high-risk
contexts. These findings require source-grounded language because imaging
biomarkers such as wall enhancement are promising but not definitive.

## Stable Or Chronic Residual Findings

Stable unruptured aneurysm is supported by unchanged size, neck, morphology,
and branch relationship on comparable CTA/MRA/DSA. Stable treated aneurysm is
supported by unchanged occlusion status, no residual or recurrent filling, and
patent parent vessels.

## Improvement, Treatment Response, Or Resolution

Improvement after treatment means complete or improved occlusion, decreasing
residual filling, stable device construct, and no new hemorrhagic or ischemic
complications. Flow-diverter response may be delayed and requires follow-up
imaging.

## Serial Imaging Assessment And Interval Change

Compare modality, technique, field strength, slice thickness, reconstruction,
MIP/3D reformats, measurement plane, aneurysm size, neck width, dome direction,
morphology, branch incorporation, and treatment artifacts. Avoid overcalling
tiny changes when protocols differ. Confirm clinically meaningful growth with
comparable vascular imaging or expert adjudication.

## Clinical Endpoints

Common endpoints include rupture, aneurysmal SAH, death, functional outcome,
modified Rankin Scale, Glasgow Outcome Scale, cranial nerve palsy, headache
syndrome, treatment, retreatment, procedural complications, hydrocephalus,
vasospasm, delayed cerebral ischemia, infarction, seizure, and discharge
disposition.

## Imaging, Biomarker, And Measurement Endpoints

Relevant endpoints include aneurysm detection, size, neck width, dome-to-neck
ratio, volume, location, irregularity, daughter sac, wall enhancement, growth,
rupture status, occlusion grade, residual neck, recurrence, coil compaction,
stent patency, in-stent stenosis, parent-vessel occlusion, vasospasm,
hydrocephalus, infarction, and hemorrhage burden.

## Common Covariates And Confounders

### Clinical Covariates

- age, sex, population/geography, family history, prior SAH
- hypertension, smoking, alcohol, stimulant use, connective-tissue disorders,
  polycystic kidney disease, infection, trauma, renal function
- symptom status, cranial nerve palsy, thunderclap headache, initial clinical
  grade in SAH

### Imaging Covariates

- modality: CTA, MRA, DSA, vessel-wall MRI
- aneurysm size, location, morphology, neck, branch incorporation, multiplicity
- rupture status, SAH pattern, hydrocephalus, vasospasm, infarct, thrombus,
  calcification, wall enhancement
- treatment device and artifact

### Treatment And Temporal Confounders

- observation versus clipping versus coiling versus flow diversion
- time from discovery, rupture, treatment, and follow-up imaging
- antiplatelet or anticoagulant exposure
- retreatment, recurrence, surveillance intensity, and center expertise

### Acquisition And Protocol Confounders

- CTA slice thickness, bone subtraction, MIP/VR methods
- MRA field strength, TOF versus contrast-enhanced MRA, flow artifacts
- DSA projection and 3D rotational angiography availability
- device artifact from clips, coils, and stents
- measurement plane, windowing, and reader expertise

### Research Design Implications

- stratify unruptured, ruptured, treated, residual/recurrent, and suspected
  aneurysm labels
- adjudicate tiny outpouchings and infundibula
- adjust for risk factors used in treatment selection and follow-up intensity
- define growth and recurrence using reproducible measurement criteria
- avoid claims that wall enhancement alone establishes rupture risk

## Statistical Implications

Intracranial aneurysm research is vulnerable to label noise because reports may
mention suspected aneurysm, infundibulum, treated aneurysm, ruled-out aneurysm,
or SAH without a confirmed aneurysm. Cohorts should distinguish detection,
rupture, growth, treatment, residual filling, recurrence, and complications.
Treatment comparisons require careful control for confounding by indication and
center expertise. Surveillance analyses must address interval censoring and
informative imaging frequency.

## Missing Information To Ask For

- Was the finding on CTA, MRA, DSA, routine MRI, or CT?
- Is the aneurysm ruptured, unruptured, treated, residual, recurrent, or only
  suspected?
- What is the exact location, size, neck width, and morphology?
- Are there multiple aneurysms?
- Is there SAH, hydrocephalus, vasospasm, infarct, or cranial nerve palsy?
- Is there prior imaging to assess growth?
- Was there clipping, coiling, stent-assisted coiling, flow diversion, or
  bypass?
- Is the report language distinguishing aneurysm from infundibulum?

## Safety And Claim Boundaries

This chapter supports research planning, imaging-feature extraction,
report-language interpretation, cohort definition, endpoint selection,
covariate review, and mimic-aware evidence review. It is not a substitute for
qualified clinical interpretation or urgent aneurysm/SAH care.
